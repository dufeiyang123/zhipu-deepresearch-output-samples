# Section 1：章节研究计划

## Chapter 1：Introduction and Historical Context of Technological Change in Labor Markets
### 研究目标
- Establish the scope, motivation, and historical precedent for studying AI-driven labor market restructuring
- Define "AI" clearly (narrow AI, ML, generative AI/LLMs) and justify definitional importance for synthesizing heterogeneous literatures
- Draw parallels with prior industrial revolutions (mechanization, electrification, computerization) and reappraise the "Luddite fallacy"
- Present the Fourth Industrial Revolution thesis and explain what distinguishes AI-era automation from earlier waves
- Justify the review's time scope (2015–2026), selection criteria (peer-reviewed English-language journals), and organizational logic

### 关键发现
- Agrawal, Gans, and Goldfarb (2019) provide the most economically precise definition of AI for labor market analysis: recent AI advances are fundamentally advances in *prediction*, and the prediction-judgment decomposition clarifies which tasks are substitutes for vs. complements to AI. [Agrawal, Gans & Goldfarb (2019)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0167624518301136 "Information Economics and Policy, 47, pp. 1–6")
- Brynjolfsson and Mitchell (2017) propose a rubric of conditions under which tasks are "suitable for machine learning" (SML), emphasizing that ML automates *tasks*, not entire jobs. [Brynjolfsson & Mitchell (2017)](https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.aap8062 "Science, 358(6370), pp. 1530–1534")
- The task-based framework distinguishing routine vs. non-routine and cognitive vs. manual tasks originates with Autor, Levy, and Murnane (2003), who show computer capital substitutes for routine tasks and complements non-routine problem-solving. [Autor, Levy & Murnane (2003)](https://www.nber.org/papers/w8337 "Quarterly Journal of Economics, 118(4), pp. 1279–1333")
- Eloundou et al. (2024) demonstrate a key definitional challenge: with simple LLM interfaces ~1.8% of U.S. jobs could have >50% of tasks affected, but with complementary software tools this jumps to >46%, underscoring that how "AI" is bounded materially affects exposure estimates. [Eloundou et al. (2024)](https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.adj0998 "Science, 384(6702), pp. 1306–1308")
- Mokyr, Vickers, and Ziebarth (2015) provide the definitive historical survey of technological anxiety, tracing three recurring concerns: fear of machine-labor substitution, moral anxiety about dehumanization, and worry that major progress is over. [Mokyr, Vickers & Ziebarth (2015)](https://www.aeaweb.org/articles?id=10.1257/jep.29.3.31 "Journal of Economic Perspectives, 29(3), pp. 31–50")
- Autor (2015) argues automation has not wiped out most jobs because it complements labor and raises output, and identifies labor market "polarization" — disproportionate wage gains at top and bottom with hollowing of middle-skill routine occupations. [Autor (2015)](https://www.aeaweb.org/articles?id=10.1257/jep.29.3.3 "Journal of Economic Perspectives, 29(3), pp. 3–30")
- Frey and Osborne (2017) estimate that 47% of total U.S. employment is at "high risk" of computerization, classifying 702 occupations by susceptibility using ML and mobile robotics advances. [Frey & Osborne (2017)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0040162516302244 "Technological Forecasting and Social Change, 114, pp. 254–280")
- Arntz, Gregory, and Zierahn (2016) challenge Frey & Osborne's occupation-level approach using a task-based methodology, finding only ~9% of OECD jobs are at high automation risk — a central methodological divergence. [Arntz, Gregory & Zierahn (2016)](https://www.oecd.org/content/dam/oecd/en/publications/reports/2016/05/the-risk-of-automation-for-jobs-in-oecd-countries_g17a27d8/5jlz9h56dvq7-en.pdf "OECD Working Papers, No. 189") *(influential working paper, not peer-reviewed journal)*
- Bresnahan and Trajtenberg (1995) formalize general purpose technologies (GPTs) characterized by pervasiveness, improvement potential, and innovational complementarities — identifying steam engine, electric motor, and semiconductors as canonical examples. [Bresnahan & Trajtenberg (1995)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/030440769401598T "Journal of Econometrics, 65(1), pp. 83–108")
- Cockburn, Henderson, and Stern (2018) argue that deep learning may serve as a new general-purpose "method of invention" reshaping innovation itself. *(NBER conference volume chapter)*
- Brynjolfsson, Rock, and Syverson (2018) address the "modern productivity paradox": despite AI matching/surpassing human performance in many domains, productivity growth has declined — arguing *implementation lags* and complementary intangible capital investments are the explanation. *(NBER conference volume chapter)*
- Acemoglu and Restrepo (2019) provide the dominant theoretical framework with two countervailing forces: a *displacement effect* and a *reinstatement effect*. Empirical decomposition shows slower U.S. employment growth reflects accelerated displacement and weaker reinstatement. [Acemoglu & Restrepo (2019)](https://www.aeaweb.org/articles?id=10.1257/jep.33.2.3 "Journal of Economic Perspectives, 33(2), pp. 3–30")
- Frank, Autor, Bessen, Brynjolfsson et al. (2019) identify fundamental barriers to forecasting AI's labor impacts and emphasize that AI, unlike previous technologies, concentrates in highly educated, well-paid, predominantly urban industries — marking a qualitative departure from prior automation waves. [Frank et al. (2019)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6452673/ "PNAS, 116(14), pp. 6531–6539")
- Eloundou et al. (2024) provide empirical evidence that LLMs possess key GPT characteristics while disproportionately affecting higher-wage, higher-education occupations — inverting the historical pattern of automation displacing lower-skill workers. [Eloundou et al. (2024)](https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.adj0998 "Science, 384(6702)")

## Chapter 2：Theoretical Frameworks for AI, Automation, and Labor
### 研究目标
- Survey and critically compare major economic theories explaining how AI interacts with labor demand, wages, and employment structure
- Cover classical/Keynesian technological unemployment perspectives, SBTC theory, the task-based framework (Autor, Levy & Murnane 2003; Acemoglu & Restrepo 2018/2019), GPT theory applied to AI
- Analyze the augmentation vs. automation dichotomy (Autor 2015; Brynjolfsson & McAfee) and "so-so automation"
- Review emerging theoretical contributions addressing generative AI (exposure-based frameworks, "task frontier" concept)

### 关键发现
- Katz and Murphy (1992) established the canonical SBTC framework, demonstrating that U.S. relative wage changes (1963–1987) are explained by persistent demand increases for skilled relative to unskilled workers. [Katz & Murphy (1992)](https://academic.oup.com/qje/article-abstract/107/1/35/1925833 "QJE, 107(1), pp. 35–78")
- Acemoglu (2002) provided a comprehensive survey and endogenous-direction theory of SBTC, arguing that the rapid increase in skilled worker supply induced development of skill-complementary technologies, and that much 19th-century technical change was actually skill-replacing. [Acemoglu (2002)](https://www.aeaweb.org/articles?id=10.1257/0022051026976 "Journal of Economic Literature, 40(1), pp. 7–72")
- Goos, Manning, and Salomons (2014) formally demonstrated job polarization across 16 European countries, attributed to routine-biased technological change (RBTC) and offshoring — a pattern the simple SBTC high-skill/low-skill dichotomy cannot explain. [Goos, Manning & Salomons (2014)](https://www.aeaweb.org/articles?id=10.1257/aer.104.8.2509 "American Economic Review, 104(8), pp. 2509–2526")
- Acemoglu and Restrepo (2018) formalized the task-based approach into a general-equilibrium growth model decomposing technological change into automation of existing tasks (shifting task content against labor) and creation of new tasks (shifting in favor of labor). [Acemoglu & Restrepo (2018)](https://www.aeaweb.org/articles?id=10.1257/aer.20160696 "American Economic Review, 108(6), pp. 1488–1542")
- Acemoglu and Restrepo (2019) presented displacement effect vs. reinstatement effect, showing that slower U.S. employment growth over three decades reflects accelerated displacement (especially in manufacturing), weaker reinstatement, and slower productivity growth. [Acemoglu & Restrepo (2019)](https://www.aeaweb.org/articles?id=10.1257/jep.33.2.3 "Journal of Economic Perspectives, 33(2), pp. 3–30")
- Acemoglu and Restrepo (2020) extended the task-based framework to subsume SBTC, showing automation can reduce real wages with only small productivity gains, while new tasks can increase or reduce inequality depending on skill composition. [Acemoglu & Restrepo (2020)](https://www.aeaweb.org/articles?id=10.1257/pandp.20201063 "AEA Papers and Proceedings, 110, pp. 356–361")
- Acemoglu and Restrepo (2020) introduced the concept of "so-so automation" — technologies just productive enough for firms to adopt and displace workers but not productive enough to generate compensating productivity gains — in "The Wrong Kind of AI?" [Acemoglu & Restrepo (2020)](https://economics.mit.edu/sites/default/files/publications/The%20Wrong%20Kind%20of%20AI%20-%20Artificial%20Intelligence%20and.pdf "Cambridge Journal of Regions, Economy and Society, 13(1), pp. 25–35")
- Agrawal, Gans, and Goldfarb (2019) reframed AI as fundamentally a prediction technology, arguing that the labor market impact depends on whether automating prediction leads to automating full decisions (displacing) or enhancing human decision-making (complementing). [Agrawal, Gans & Goldfarb (2019)](https://www.aeaweb.org/articles?id=10.1257/jep.33.2.31 "Journal of Economic Perspectives, 33(2), pp. 31–50")
- Brynjolfsson (2022) formalized the automation-vs-augmentation tension as "The Turing Trap," arguing that the dominant AI paradigm focuses on replacing workers rather than creating new capabilities that complement them. [Brynjolfsson (2022)](https://direct.mit.edu/daed/article-abstract/151/2/272/110622 "Daedalus, 151(2), pp. 272–287")
- Felten, Raj, and Seamans (2021) introduced the AI Occupational Exposure (AIOE) measure, linking AI application advances to occupation-level abilities via O*NET — a widely adopted empirical tool and precursor to the Eloundou et al. (2024) LLM-exposure framework. [Felten, Raj & Seamans (2021)](https://sms.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1002/smj.3286 "Strategic Management Journal, 42(12), pp. 2195–2217")
- Acemoglu (2024) applied the task-based framework to AI, predicting TFP gains of no more than 0.66% over 10 years — "nontrivial but modest" — and arguing AI will widen the gap between capital and labor income. [Acemoglu (2024)](https://www.nber.org/papers/w32487 "NBER WP 32487 / Economic Policy, 40(5)") *(working paper, subsequently published in Economic Policy)*
- Autor (2024) proposed that AI's unique theoretical opportunity lies in democratizing expertise — enabling a larger set of workers to perform higher-stakes decision-making tasks, potentially restoring the hollowed-out middle-skill labor market. [Autor (2024)](https://www.nber.org/papers/w32140 "NBER Working Paper 32140") *(working paper)*
- Korinek and Suh (2024) modeled scenarios for AGI transition, showing that if human-performable task complexity is bounded, sufficiently advanced AI leads to wage collapse; the viability of "new tasks" as a counterweight depends on whether human capabilities define an unbounded frontier. [Korinek & Suh (2024)](https://www.nber.org/papers/w32255 "NBER Working Paper 32255") *(working paper)*
- Brynjolfsson, Li, and Raymond (2025) documented in the first major empirical study of generative AI at work (5,172 customer-support agents) that AI disproportionately benefits less-experienced and lower-skilled workers, providing empirical grounding for Autor's (2024) expertise-democratization thesis. [Brynjolfsson, Li & Raymond (2025)](https://academic.oup.com/qje/article/140/2/889/7990658 "Quarterly Journal of Economics, 140(2), pp. 889–942")
- Five key unresolved theoretical debates: (1) displacement vs. reinstatement pace in the GenAI era, (2) so-so automation vs. transformative GPT characterization, (3) augmentation of experts vs. non-experts, (4) bounded vs. unbounded task frontier, (5) whether generative AI as a creation technology requires new theoretical primitives beyond the prediction framework.

## Chapter 3：Empirical Evidence on AI Adoption and Aggregate Labor Market Outcomes
### 研究目标
- Synthesize empirical literature on AI's actual effects on employment levels, wage structures, job displacement, and job creation at aggregate and firm levels
- Address the gap between theoretical predictions of massive displacement and nuanced, often modest, documented effects
- Cover measurement challenges in operationalizing "AI adoption" and "AI exposure"
- Review evidence on wage polarization, labor share of income, AI-driven productivity gains
- Examine early empirical evidence on generative AI / LLM adoption and labor market impacts (post-2022)

### 关键发现
- Webb (2020) constructs patent-based occupational exposure scores for AI, robots, and software separately, establishing that AI exposure is highest for high-skilled occupations — contrasting with robots (middle-skill manufacturing) and software (routine clerical). [Webb (2020)](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=3482150 "Stanford working paper") *(working paper)*
- Felten, Raj, and Seamans (2021) develop the AI Occupational Exposure (AIOE) index linking 10 AI applications to 52 O*NET abilities; highest-exposed occupations are white-collar and cognitive-intensive (financial examiners, actuaries), lowest-exposed are physically demanding (roofers, construction). [Felten, Raj & Seamans (2021)](https://sms.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1002/smj.3286 "Strategic Management Journal, 42(12), pp. 2195–2217")
- Acemoglu, Autor, Hazell, and Restrepo (2022) operationalize AI adoption at establishment level using the near-universe of U.S. online job postings (2007–2018); AI postings surged after 2016 but at aggregate level no significant employment or wage effects are detectable. [Acemoglu et al. (2022)](https://www.journals.uchicago.edu/doi/10.1086/718327 "Journal of Labor Economics, 40(S1), pp. S293–S340")
- Babina, Fedyk, He, and Hodson (2024) track firm-level AI investment via employee AI-skill resumes; AI-investing firms experience higher growth in sales, employment, and valuations, primarily through product innovation, but this growth concentrates among larger firms and raises industry concentration. [Babina et al. (2024)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0304405X2300185X "Journal of Financial Economics, 151, article 103745")
- Eloundou et al. (2024) estimate ~80% of U.S. workforce could have ≥10% of tasks affected by LLMs, ~19% could see ≥50% of tasks impacted. [Eloundou et al. (2024)](https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.adj0998 "Science, 384(6702), pp. 1306–1308")
- Acemoglu and Restrepo (2020) find that across 722 U.S. commuting zones (1990–2007), one additional robot per thousand workers reduces employment-to-population ratio by 0.2 percentage points and wages by 0.42%; between 1990–2007, robots reduced U.S. employment-to-population ratio by 0.37pp (~400,000 jobs). [Acemoglu & Restrepo (2020)](https://www.journals.uchicago.edu/doi/abs/10.1086/705716 "Journal of Political Economy, 128(6), pp. 2188–2244")
- Graetz and Michaels (2018) analyze industrial robots across 17 countries (1993–2007); robot adoption contributed ~0.36pp to annual labor productivity growth while raising TFP and lowering output prices; no significant reduction in total employment overall, though low-skilled workers' employment share fell. [Graetz & Michaels (2018)](https://direct.mit.edu/rest/article/100/5/753/58489 "Review of Economics and Statistics, 100(5), pp. 753–768")
- Acemoglu (2024) predicts AI's TFP gain at no more than 0.66% over 10 years (likely <0.53% accounting for hard-to-learn tasks), and that AI will widen capital-labor income gap. [Acemoglu (2024)](https://www.nber.org/papers/w32487 "NBER WP 32487 / Economic Policy, 40(5)") *(working paper, subsequently in Economic Policy)*
- Autor, Chin, Salomons, and Seegmiller (2024) document that ~60% of employment in 2018 was in job titles that did not exist in 1940, and new work creation has been accelerating — the key counterweight to automation. [Autor et al. (2024)](https://academic.oup.com/qje/article-abstract/139/3/1399/7630187 "Quarterly Journal of Economics, 139(3), pp. 1399–1465")
- Brynjolfsson, Li, and Raymond (2025) study 5,172 customer-support agents: generative AI assistance increased productivity by 15% on average, with least experienced workers gaining up to 30%; treated agents with 2 months of tenure performed as well as untreated agents with >6 months — a "skill-leveling" pattern. [Brynjolfsson, Li & Raymond (2025)](https://academic.oup.com/qje/article/140/2/889/7990658 "Quarterly Journal of Economics, 140(2), pp. 889–942")
- Noy and Zhang (2023) find in a pre-registered experiment with 453 professionals that ChatGPT access reduced writing task completion time by 40% and raised quality by 18%, with less-skilled workers benefiting most. [Noy & Zhang (2023)](https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.adh2586 "Science, 381(6654), pp. 187–192")
- Hui, Reshef, and Zhou (2024) document that freelancers in GenAI-exposed occupations on Upwork experienced reductions in both employment and earnings after ChatGPT/DALL-E/Midjourney launch. [Hui, Reshef & Zhou (2024)](https://pubsonline.informs.org/doi/abs/10.1287/orsc.2023.18441 "Organization Science")
- Demirci, Hannane, and Zhu (2025) find that jobs most exposed to generative AI (writing, software development, image work) on freelancing platforms experienced significant declines in postings and earnings post-ChatGPT. [Demirci, Hannane & Zhu (2025)](https://pubsonline.informs.org/doi/abs/10.1287/mnsc.2024.05420 "Management Science, 71(4)")
- Brynjolfsson, Chandar, and Chen (2025) using ADP payroll data find early-career workers (ages 22–25) in AI-exposed occupations experienced 16% relative employment declines; software developers aged 22–25 declined nearly 20% from late-2022 peak. [Brynjolfsson, Chandar & Chen (2025)](https://digitaleconomy.stanford.edu/app/uploads/2025/11/CanariesintheCoalMine_Nov25.pdf "Stanford working paper") *(working paper)*
- Hartley et al. (2026) report 35.9% of U.S. workers used generative AI by December 2025, indicating rapid diffusion vs. slower adoption curves for prior GPTs. [Hartley et al. (2026)](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=5136877 "SSRN working paper") *(working paper)*
- Key tension: micro-level productivity studies show large gains (15–56% faster), yet aggregate employment studies show null or negative effects — the productivity-vs.-displacement paradox mirrors Acemoglu's "so-so automation" concern.
- Generative AI evidence breaks from historical skill-biased technical change: AI assistance compresses productivity distribution by disproportionately helping lower-skilled workers (skill-leveling pattern).

## Chapter 4：Sectoral and Occupational Heterogeneity in AI's Labor Market Impact
### 研究目标
- Examine how AI's restructuring effects vary across industries and occupations, beyond aggregate analysis
- Cover manufacturing/logistics (industrial robotics, warehouse automation), healthcare (AI diagnostics, drug discovery), financial services (algorithmic trading, robo-advisory), creative/knowledge-intensive industries (generative AI impact on writing, design, software, legal research), and public sector/education
- Comparatively analyze occupational exposure frameworks (Frey & Osborne 2017; Webb 2020; Eloundou et al. 2023) and their predictive validity

### 关键发现
- Acemoglu and Restrepo (2020) find manufacturing-heavy US commuting zones most affected by robots: one additional robot per thousand workers reduces employment-to-population ratio by 0.2pp and wages by 0.42%, with strongest effects on routine manual, blue-collar, and assembly occupations and workers without college education; 360,000–670,000 US jobs lost through 2007. [Acemoglu & Restrepo (2020)](https://www.journals.uchicago.edu/doi/abs/10.1086/705716 "Journal of Political Economy, 128(6)")
- Dauth, Findeisen, Suedekum, and Woessner (2021) show industrial robots had no negative aggregate employment effect in Germany (1994–2014) despite ~4× higher robot density than US; ~275,000 manufacturing jobs destroyed but fully offset by services sector gains; works councils and unions enabled incumbent workers to transition to new tasks within plants; displacement fell disproportionately on young labor market entrants. [Dauth et al. (2021)](https://academic.oup.com/jeea/article-abstract/19/6/3104/6179884 "Journal of the European Economic Association, 19(6), pp. 3104–3153")
- Autor and Dorn (2013) document that automation of routine tasks drove simultaneous growth of low-skill service and high-skill professional employment (1980–2005), hollowing out middle-skill routine jobs — the foundational job polarization finding. [Autor & Dorn (2013)](https://www.aeaweb.org/articles?id=10.1257/aer.103.5.1553 "American Economic Review, 103(5), pp. 1553–1597")
- Topol (2019) reviews AI in medicine across three levels (clinicians, health systems, patients); deep learning achieves physician-level performance in radiology, pathology, dermatology, ophthalmology, and cardiology tasks; dominant paradigm is augmentation rather than replacement, constrained by regulatory barriers, trust deficits, bias, and lack of transparency. [Topol (2019)](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41591-018-0300-7 "Nature Medicine, 25(1), pp. 44–56")
- Rajpurkar, Chen, Banerjee, and Topol (2022) document that despite >2,000 AI publications in PubMed during 2021, prospective clinical validation remains limited; a significant gap persists between research performance and real-world deployment due to data scarcity, racial bias, and limited cross-institutional generalizability. [Rajpurkar et al. (2022)](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35058619/ "Nature Medicine, 28(1), pp. 31–38")
- Dell'Acqua, McFowland, Mollick et al. (2026) find in a randomized field experiment with 758 BCG consultants that GPT-4 increased output quality by 30–40% and speed by 25%+ on within-frontier tasks, but on an out-of-frontier task, AI-assisted consultants were 19pp less likely to arrive at the correct answer; the concept of AI's "jagged technological frontier" shows that adjacent tasks of similar apparent difficulty can fall on opposite sides of AI capability. [Dell'Acqua et al. (2026)](https://pubsonline.informs.org/doi/10.1287/orsc.2025.21838 "Organization Science")
- Choi and Schwarcz (2024) show that GPT-4 significantly enhances law student performance on simple multiple-choice questions (~45% improvement for lower performers), but provides no significant benefit on complex essay questions requiring nuanced legal reasoning. [Choi & Schwarcz (2024)](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=4539836 "Journal of Legal Education, forthcoming") *(working paper at experiment time)*
- Comparative analysis of occupational exposure frameworks: Frey & Osborne (2017) estimate 47% high-risk at occupation level but overstate displacement risk due to lack of task-level granularity; Webb (2020) correctly anticipated AI targeting higher-skill occupations; Felten et al. (2021) provide more granular ability-based measurement; Eloundou et al. (2024) offer LLM-specific task-level assessment showing 80% of workforce ≥10% task-affected, with higher-wage occupations more exposed — a reversal from prior automation patterns. All frameworks share the limitation of measuring exposure rather than actual impact.
- Cross-sectoral pattern: manufacturing/logistics exhibits predominantly displacement of routine manual tasks (with institutional variation US vs. Germany); healthcare shows predominantly augmentation constrained by regulation; creative/knowledge work shows complex augmentation-on-frontier + quality degradation off-frontier.

## Chapter 5：Skills, Education, and Workforce Adaptation Strategies
### 研究目标
- Review how AI reshapes human capital requirements and how workers, firms, and educational institutions respond
- Analyze the changing skill premium: declining returns to routine tasks, rising demand for complex problem-solving, socio-emotional skills, and AI-complementary technical competencies
- Assess empirical evidence on reskilling/upskilling effectiveness (corporate training, public workforce development, lifelong learning platforms)
- Examine formal education adaptation (curriculum reform, STEM vs. liberal arts debates)
- Address distributional concerns: age, gender, geographic, and socioeconomic disparities in adaptation capacity

### 关键发现
- Deming (2017) shows that between 1980 and 2012, employment and wage growth were highest in occupations requiring high levels of both social and math skills; jobs requiring high social skills grew by 12pp as a share of the U.S. labor force, while mathematically intensive but socially undemanding occupations shrank. [Deming (2017)](https://academic.oup.com/qje/article-abstract/132/4/1593/3861633 "Quarterly Journal of Economics, 132(4), pp. 1593–1640")
- Deming and Noray (2020) find that STEM workers earn a substantial initial wage premium (~25% over business graduates), but applied technical skills depreciate faster than in non-STEM fields, with the advantage narrowing substantially by age 40 — implying education must balance occupation-specific training with transferable skills. [Deming & Noray (2020)](https://academic.oup.com/qje/article/135/4/1965/5858010 "Quarterly Journal of Economics, 135(4), pp. 1965–2005")
- Acemoglu and Restrepo (2022) find that 50–70% of U.S. wage structure changes over the last four decades are accounted for by relative wage declines of workers specialized in routine tasks in industries that adopted automation. [Acemoglu & Restrepo (2022)](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.3982/ECTA19815 "Econometrica, 90(5), pp. 1973–2016")
- Brynjolfsson, Li, and Raymond (2025) document that generative AI's skill-leveling effect consistently benefits less experienced/lower-skilled workers most across customer support (up to 30% productivity gains for bottom-quintile workers), compressing within-occupation skill distributions. [Brynjolfsson, Li & Raymond (2025)](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=4426942 "Quarterly Journal of Economics, 140(2), pp. 889–942")
- Cui, Demirer, Jaffe, Musolff, Peng, and Salz (2026) find in RCTs across Microsoft, Accenture, and a Fortune 100 company with 4,867 developers that an AI coding assistant increased completed tasks by 26.08%, with less experienced developers seeing higher adoption and greater productivity gains. [Cui et al. (2026)](https://pubsonline.informs.org/doi/10.1287/mnsc.2025.00535 "Management Science, Articles in Advance")
- Katz, Roth, Hendra, and Schaberg (2022) show in randomized evaluations of nine sectoral employment programs that sector-focused training combining screening, occupational + soft-skills training, and wraparound services generates persistent earnings gains of 12–34%, driven by access to higher-wage jobs in higher-earning industries. [Katz et al. (2022)](https://www.journals.uchicago.edu/doi/abs/10.1086/717932 "Journal of Labor Economics, 40(S1)")
- Hershbein and Kahn (2018) demonstrate that recessions accelerate routine-biased technological change: skill requirements in job postings differentially increased in MSAs hit hard by the Great Recession, with effects most pronounced in routine-cognitive occupations and persisting through 2015. [Hershbein & Kahn (2018)](https://www.aeaweb.org/articles?id=10.1257/aer.20161570 "American Economic Review, 108(7), pp. 1737–1772")
- Modestino, Shoag, and Ballance (2020) find that a 1pp increase in state unemployment rate is associated with a 0.6pp increase in employers requiring a bachelor's degree — Great Recession-driven unemployment accounted for 18–25% of total skill requirement increases (2007–2010). [Modestino et al. (2020)](https://direct.mit.edu/rest/article/102/4/793/96774 "Review of Economics and Statistics, 102(4), pp. 793–805")
- Deming and Kahn (2018) show firms hiring for professional positions demand combinations of cognitive (analytics, problem-solving), social (teamwork, communication), and specific technical skills; large, high-wage firms disproportionately demand both cognitive and social skills simultaneously. [Deming & Kahn (2018)](https://www.journals.uchicago.edu/doi/10.1086/694106 "Journal of Labor Economics, 36(S1), pp. S337–S369")
- MOOC/online learning platforms face persistent completion rate challenges (5–15% typical), and experimental evidence from Costa Rica found only 10% course completion with males and wealthier individuals more likely to complete — raising concerns that online reskilling may exacerbate inequalities. [Aedo, Aramburu & Blanco (2024)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0304387824000348 "Journal of Development Economics, 2024")
- Generative AI changes skill requirements qualitatively from prior automation: (1) it disproportionately affects higher-wage knowledge-intensive occupations rather than routine manual/cognitive tasks, (2) meta-cognitive skills to recognize AI's capability boundaries become critical ("jagged frontier"), (3) returns to narrow expertise may decline while returns to judgment, interpersonal communication, creativity, and problem-framing rise.

## Chapter 6：Ethical, Social, and Policy Dimensions of AI-Driven Labor Market Restructuring
### 研究目标
- Critically review scholarly discourse on normative and governance challenges from AI's labor market effects
- Cover income inequality and labor market polarization, "winner-take-all" dynamics
- Examine algorithmic bias in hiring and workforce management (fairness, accountability, transparency scholarship)

### 关键发现
- Korinek and Stiglitz (2018) provide a foundational taxonomy of AI inequality channels: surplus accruing to innovators and factor price changes from labor displacement; they argue that under plausible conditions, non-distortionary taxation can compensate losers even in extreme automation scenarios. *(NBER conference volume chapter)*
- Acemoglu and Restrepo (2022) document that automation reduced the real wage of male high-school dropouts by 8.8% during 1980–2016 while yielding only a cumulative 3.4% TFP increase — automation accounts for 50–70% of U.S. wage structure changes. [Acemoglu & Restrepo (2022)](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.3982/ECTA19815 "Econometrica, 90(5), pp. 1973–2016")
- Autor (2019) shows automation and trade have eliminated the bulk of non-college production, administrative, and clerical jobs, yielding disproportionate polarization of urban labor markets and attenuating the urban wage premium for non-college workers. [Autor (2019)](https://www.aeaweb.org/articles?id=10.1257/pandp.20191110 "AEA Papers and Proceedings, 109, pp. 1–32")
- Acemoglu (2021) warns of potential AI harms including excessive automation without productivity gains, market power concentration among AI platform firms, privacy erosion, and manipulation through AI-generated content — all with disproportionate distributional consequences. [Acemoglu (2021)](https://www.nber.org/papers/w29247 "NBER Working Paper 29247") *(working paper)*
- Raghavan et al. (2020) document significant gaps between vendor claims about bias mitigation in employment algorithms and actual practice, identifying critical risks in prediction targets, data collection, and tensions with antidiscrimination law. [Raghavan et al. (2020)](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3351095.3372828 "FAT* Conference, 2020")
- Mehrabi et al. (2021) provide a comprehensive survey identifying over 20 distinct types of bias in ML systems and cataloging fairness definitions, documenting discriminatory outcomes in hiring, criminal justice, and healthcare. [Mehrabi et al. (2021)](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3457607 "ACM Computing Surveys, 54(6), pp. 1–35")
- Kleinberg et al. (2018) show ML predictions can improve upon human judicial decisions — with implications for employment screening, demonstrating both the potential and pitfalls of replacing human discretion with algorithmic prediction. [Kleinberg et al. (2018)](https://academic.oup.com/qje/article-abstract/133/1/237/4095198 "Quarterly Journal of Economics, 133(1), pp. 237–293")
- Bernhardt, Kresge, and Suleiman (2023) identify harms from data-driven employment systems (invasive monitoring, algorithmic wage-setting, automated scheduling, productivity tracking) and propose a comprehensive "worker technology rights" framework. [Bernhardt et al. (2023)](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/00197939221131558 "ILR Review, 76(1), pp. 3–29")
- Verho, Hämäläinen, and Kanninen (2022) report from the Finnish basic income experiment (2017–2018, n=2,000, €560/month): despite lowering participation tax rates by 23pp for full-time employment, days in employment remained statistically unchanged in year 1; importantly, participation in reemployment services remained high even with search requirements waived. [Verho et al. (2022)](https://www.aeaweb.org/articles?id=10.1257/pol.20200143 "American Economic Journal: Economic Policy, 14(1), pp. 501–522")
- The EU AI Act (2024) classifies AI systems used in recruitment, selection, promotion, task allocation, performance monitoring, and termination as "high-risk," requiring conformity assessments and transparency; scholars identify shortcomings including reliance on self-assessment and insufficient enforcement. [Wright (2025)](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/full/10.1177/00221856251394780 "Journal of Industrial Relations, 67(5)")
- Adams-Prassl et al. (2023) propose a regulatory blueprint centered on transparency obligations, data access rights for workers/representatives, and meaningful human oversight requirements to address algorithmic management gaps. [Adams-Prassl et al. (2023)](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=4373355 "European Labour Law Journal, 14(2), pp. 124–151")
- Rosenblat and Stark (2016) demonstrate through empirical study of Uber drivers that platforms leverage information and power asymmetries to exert employer-like control while classifying workers as independent contractors. [Rosenblat & Stark (2016)](https://ijoc.org/index.php/ijoc/article/view/4892 "International Journal of Communication, 10, pp. 3758–3784")
- Kellogg, Valentine, and Christin (2020) identify six mechanisms of algorithmic workplace control (restricting, recommending, recording, rating, replacing, rewarding) and find that algorithmic control creates a "new contested terrain" triggering worker resistance strategies. [Kellogg et al. (2020)](https://journals.aom.org/doi/10.5465/annals.2018.0174 "Academy of Management Annals, 14(1), pp. 366–410")
- Collective bargaining is identified as the most effective mechanism for addressing sector-specific algorithmic management, provided trade unions and works council members are upskilled to understand the "black box" of algorithmic systems.

## Chapter 7：Synthesis, Research Gaps, and Future Directions
### 研究目标
- Integrate findings across preceding chapters: reconcile theoretical predictions with empirical evidence and policy response landscape
- Identify methodological challenges: endogeneity, AI exposure measurement, causal identification difficulties, GenAI research limitations
- Survey under-researched domains: developing economies, informal sectors, long-run general equilibrium effects, AI × demographic change
- Map emerging frontiers: agentic AI, multimodal AI, human–AI teaming, potential for AI to reshape the nature of "work"
- Articulate a concrete, tractable research agenda for the next five years

### 关键发现
- Autor (2022) traces the intellectual evolution from "unbridled enthusiasm" through "qualified optimism" to "vast uncertainty" across four paradigms, cautioning that "forecasting the 'consequences' of technological change treats the future as a fate to be divined rather than an expedition to be undertaken." [Autor (2022)](https://www.nber.org/papers/w30074 "NBER Working Paper 30074") *(working paper)*
- Empirical evidence on AI's aggregate employment effects remains collectively inconclusive: Kolko (2026) reviews research combining AI exposure with employment data and finds results are highly sensitive to which AI measure is chosen, with some studies finding employment declines in AI-exposed occupations and others finding the opposite. [Kolko (2026)](https://www.brookings.edu/articles/research-on-ai-and-the-labor-market-is-still-in-the-first-inning/ "Brookings Institution, March 2026")
- Multiple, disagreeing AI-exposure measures create interpretive fragility: occupations rank differently on exposure vs. usage, and even among exposure measures there is significant disagreement — a "streetlamp bias" where conclusions depend on available metrics.
- Frank et al. (2019) identified three fundamental and still-unresolved barriers to forecasting AI's labor impacts: (1) difficulty mapping AI capabilities to occupational task requirements, (2) interconnectedness of tasks within occupations making single-task predictions misleading, (3) failure to account for economic restructuring and new task/industry creation. [Frank et al. (2019)](https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.1900949116 "PNAS, 116(14), pp. 6531–6539")
- Bonfiglioli et al. (2025) use shift-share instruments across US commuting zones (2000–2020) to estimate AI's employment effects, finding robust negative effects — illustrating both the promise and difficulty of causal identification in AI-labor research. [Bonfiglioli et al. (2025)](https://academic.oup.com/economicpolicy/article/40/121/145/7908355 "Economic Policy, 40(121)")
- Current GenAI research findings are "weak signals": commercial LLM diffusion is so recent that lasting economic impacts take years to appear; AI capabilities advance fast enough that robust current conclusions could quickly become obsolete; research tools and definitions remain immature.
- Developing-economy labor markets are systematically under-studied; Cazzaniga et al. (2024) estimate AI exposure at 60% in advanced economies but only 40% in emerging markets and 26% in low-income countries, yet these lower figures do not mean lower stakes. [Cazzaniga et al. (2024)](https://www.imf.org/en/publications/staff-discussion-notes/issues/2024/01/14/gen-ai-artificial-intelligence-and-the-future-of-work-542379 "IMF Staff Discussion Note SDN/2024/001")
- Fossen et al. (2023) develop a semantic-similarity methodology to transfer US-based AI impact measures to developing countries (Lao PDR, Vietnam), finding that exposure patterns differ qualitatively from US predictions — underscoring the need for country-specific measurement. [Fossen et al. (2023)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9936490/ "Journal of the Economics of Inequality")
- Labor supply effects of AI are severely under-researched: AI could affect supply through improved job search, reduced personal chore burdens, or making leisure more appealing — yet almost no rigorous research exists on these channels.
- Korinek and Suh (2024) model AGI transition scenarios showing that if human-task complexity is bounded, wages collapse as AI approaches full automation; declines may occur even before full automation if large-scale automation outpaces capital accumulation. [Korinek & Suh (2024)](https://www.nber.org/papers/w32255 "NBER Working Paper 32255") *(working paper)*
- O'Neill et al. (2023) find human-AI teaming research is fragmented across disciplines and lacks a unified conceptual framework, arguing for a socio-technical approach viewing AI as a collaborative teammate. [O'Neill et al. (2023)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10570436/ "Frontiers in Artificial Intelligence")
- Agentic AI — systems capable of autonomous multi-step action — represents a qualitative shift beyond current task-level analysis frameworks; existing exposure measures and task-based models were designed for discrete-task AI and may require new theoretical primitives.
- Eight priority research questions proposed: (1) developing-economy AI displacement with country-specific measurement, (2) general equilibrium effects on new task/occupation creation in the GenAI era, (3) standardized AI exposure measurement for causal inference, (4) labor supply effects of AI, (5) AI × demographic change interaction, (6) within-occupation distributional effects and long-run "leveling-up" generalizability, (7) agentic AI's effect on demand for human judgment and oversight, (8) transition dynamics compared to prior technological disruptions (China shock, computerization).

# Section 2：给 Write 阶段的执行建议

1. **Citation quality gate**: Only cite articles published in peer-reviewed, English-language academic journals (e.g., *American Economic Review*, *Quarterly Journal of Economics*, *Journal of Labor Economics*, *Research Policy*, *Nature Human Behaviour*, *Science*, *Management Science*). Exclude grey literature, news articles, and policy briefs. Exceptionally influential working papers (e.g., Acemoglu & Restrepo NBER papers pre-journal publication) may be cited with explicit working-paper status noted.

2. **Handling conflicting evidence**: Present competing findings side by side with explicit attention to (a) methodological differences, (b) time horizon differences (ex-ante predictions vs. ex-post measurement), (c) geographic/sectoral context differences, and (d) differences in AI definition used. Avoid false synthesis.

3. **Terminological consistency**: Standardize key terms — "automation" (substitution of human labor in existing tasks), "augmentation" (AI enhancing human productivity without displacing), "displacement effect" (reduction in labor demand from task automation), "reinstatement effect" (creation of new tasks/jobs), "exposure" (degree of occupation susceptibility to AI), "adoption" (actual firm-level implementation). Define in Chapters 1–2, use uniformly thereafter.

4. **Tone and voice**: Formal, analytical, third-person academic voice. Avoid colloquial expressions and journalistic sensationalism. Use measured research language ("the evidence suggests," "the balance of findings indicates").

5. **Theory–evidence integration**: Each empirical chapter (3–6) should explicitly link findings back to theoretical frameworks in Chapter 2 (e.g., when discussing job polarization, reference the task-based model's predictions).

6. **Chapter section architecture**: Within each chapter follow: (a) introduce the theme, (b) survey and synthesize findings thematically (not study-by-study), (c) identify debates/contradictions, (d) note gaps/limitations. This four-part structure applies to every chapter.

7. **Temporal layering**: Distinguish three generations of literature: (i) pre-2017 (industrial automation/robotics, occupation-level probability estimates), (ii) 2017–2022 (task-based models, firm-level AI adoption), (iii) post-2022 (generative AI/LLM diffusion). Be explicit about which wave each finding belongs to.

8. **Quantitative precision**: Always specify study sample (country, time period, population), methodology, and key effect sizes. Avoid vague summaries. Example: "Using DiD across 722 U.S. commuting zones (1990–2007), Acemoglu and Restrepo (2020) find that one additional robot per thousand workers reduces the employment-to-population ratio by 0.2 percentage points."

9. **Avoid techno-determinism**: Outcomes depend on institutional context, managerial decisions, policy choices, and complementary innovation. Technology creates possibilities, not inevitabilities.

10. **Geographic limitations**: Acknowledge the U.S./European data concentration in the literature. Note generalizability limits when discussing country-specific findings; integrate credible non-Western studies where available.

11. **Distinguish prediction from observation**: Consistently separate (a) predictive/exposure-based studies estimating potential susceptibility from (b) empirical studies measuring realized labor market effects.

12. **Future directions without speculation**: Chapter 7 should identify concrete, tractable research questions grounded in documented gaps and limitations, not speculative futurism.

13. **Time scope**: Literature primarily from 2015–2026, with emphasis on 2020–2026 publications. The review's temporal anchor is April 2026.
