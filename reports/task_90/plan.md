# Section 1：章节研究计划

## Chapter 1: Technical Foundations of Advanced Driver-Assistance Systems
### 研究目标
- Establish the engineering baseline for liability analysis: SAE J3016 automation taxonomy (L0–L5) with emphasis on the L2/L3 boundary where control authority is ambiguous.
- Describe core sensor architectures (camera-centric, LiDAR-fusion, radar-fusion), perception–planning–actuation pipelines, and the concept of Operational Design Domain (ODD).
- Explain control-authority handoff mechanisms—transition demand (take-over request) timing and failure-mode behavior (minimal risk condition/maneuver).
- Clarify why the technical architecture of a given ADAS directly shapes the liability question: designed capability vs. reasonable user belief.

### 关键发现
- SAE J3016:2021 defines six levels (L0–L5); L0–L2 are "driver support" where the human performs all OEDR, while L3–L5 are "Automated Driving Systems" where the ADS performs the DDT including OEDR within its ODD. The L2/L3 boundary is the most consequential for liability: at L2 the driver supervises and bears responsibility; at L3 the ADS monitors the environment and primary liability shifts to the system. SAE J3016 is explicitly a terminology standard, not a safety standard, and prohibits marketing terms like "Level 2+" [SAE J3016 User Guide](https://users.ece.cmu.edu/~koopman/j3016/ "Koopman CMU guide to SAE J3016:2021").
- Levels apply to features, not vehicles—a single vehicle may operate multiple features at different SAE levels during a single drive cycle [SAE J3016 User Guide](https://users.ece.cmu.edu/~koopman/j3016/ "Koopman CMU: Myth #18").
- Tesla employs a camera-only approach ("Tesla Vision") since mid-2021. In March 2026, NHTSA escalated its FSD investigation (engineering analysis) into 3.2 million Tesla vehicles, finding the camera-based system "did not detect common roadway conditions like glare, dust or other airborne obstructions" and "lost track of or never detected a lead vehicle" in many crashes reviewed, identifying nine incidents including one fatal crash [Reuters](https://www.reuters.com/business/autos-transportation/us-auto-safety-regulator-opens-probe-into-tesla-vehicles-with-fsd-2026-03-19/ "NHTSA escalates FSD probe, March 2026").
- Waymo uses 5th-gen multi-sensor fusion (360° LiDAR >300m range, cameras >500m, imaging radar), designed so no single sensor type alone provides sufficient detail—each modality compensates for others' weaknesses [Waymo](https://waymo.com/blog/2020/03/introducing-5th-generation-waymo-driver "5th-gen Waymo Driver, March 2020").
- Mercedes DRIVE PILOT uses radar, LiDAR, cameras, road moisture sensors, ultrasonic sensors, and microphones. Redundancy design allows continued operation if a single sensor fails, with control handover to fallback-ready user [Mercedes-Benz VSSA](https://group.mercedes-benz.com/dokumente/innovation/sonstiges/2023-03-06-vssa-mercedes-benz-drive-pilot.pdf "DRIVE PILOT VSSA document").
- The perception–planning–actuation pipeline can fail at any stage with cascading effects: perception failures (missed detections, false positives, misclassification, sensor degradation), planning failures (incorrect trajectory prediction, edge-case inability), and actuation failures (latency, hardware faults). Mercedes addresses these through ISO 26262 and SOTIF analysis [Mercedes-Benz VSSA](https://group.mercedes-benz.com/dokumente/innovation/sonstiges/2023-03-06-vssa-mercedes-benz-drive-pilot.pdf "DRIVE PILOT safety concept: SOTIF and ISO 26262").
- Mercedes DRIVE PILOT's ODD at U.S. launch: fully access-controlled highways, ≤40 mph, medium-to-dense traffic, fair weather, machine-detectable lane markings, no tunnels/toll booths, pre-mapped routes, daytime only. December 2024: speed increased to 95 km/h (~59 mph) for Europe under revised UN R157 [Mercedes-Benz VSSA](https://group.mercedes-benz.com/dokumente/innovation/sonstiges/2023-03-06-vssa-mercedes-benz-drive-pilot.pdf "DRIVE PILOT ODD specifications").
- Tesla FSD (Supervised) operates as L2 with no formally published ODD boundary—the system can be engaged anywhere, even where it may not perform reliably, with liability remaining on the driver. At L3/L4, ODD enforcement is definitionally required; if engagement outside ODD is possible, the feature is by definition L1 or L2 [SAE J3016 User Guide](https://users.ece.cmu.edu/~koopman/j3016/ "Koopman: Myth #9—ODD enforcement requirement").
- UN Regulation No. 157 (ALKS) mandates a minimum 10-second transition demand before minimal risk maneuver. Mercedes DRIVE PILOT provides ~10 seconds; if user fails to respond, escalation includes controlled lane stop, hazard lamps, emergency call, and door unlock [Mercedes-Benz VSSA](https://group.mercedes-benz.com/dokumente/innovation/sonstiges/2023-03-06-vssa-mercedes-benz-drive-pilot.pdf "DRIVE PILOT: ~10-second TOR, MRC procedures") and [SAE J3016 User Guide](https://users.ece.cmu.edu/~koopman/j3016/ "Koopman: Myth #14—ALKS 10-second minimum").
- MRC (Minimal Risk Condition) does not guarantee safety—stopping in a lane of high-speed traffic introduces its own risks. MRC depends on both vehicle condition and operating environment [SAE J3016 User Guide](https://users.ece.cmu.edu/~koopman/j3016/ "Koopman: Myth #13—MRC ≠ safe").
- Nordhoff et al. (2023), interviewing 103 Tesla users, found progressive complacency: 48/103 used Autopilot outside designed ODD, 46/103 engaged in eyes-off-road driving, 21/103 defeated steering-wheel torque sensor, 15/103 reported mind-off or fatigued driving. One respondent drove 44 miles without looking up [Nordhoff et al. 2023](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9996345/ "Nordhoff et al., 2023: misuse patterns among 103 Tesla users, Frontiers in Psychology").
- The California DMV ruled on December 16, 2025, that Tesla violated state law by marketing "Autopilot" and "Full Self-Driving Capability" as tantamount to autonomous driving capability, ordering marketing language modification within 60 days [California DMV](https://www.dmv.ca.gov/portal/news-and-media/news-releases/dmv-finds-tesla-violated-california-state-law/ "California DMV: Tesla violated state law, December 2025").
- Mercedes-Benz is the first OEM to formally accept liability when its L3 DRIVE PILOT is engaged within ODD—structurally enabled by narrow enforceable ODD, robust driver monitoring, and guaranteed MRC [The Verge](https://www.theverge.com/2023/9/27/23892154/mercedes-benz-drive-pilot-autonomous-level-3-test "Verge: Mercedes accepts L3 liability").
- Under NHTSA's Standing General Order, through November 2025, 5,202 incidents were reported (Tesla 2,732 / 52.5%, Waymo 1,443 / 27.7%, Cruise 155 / 3.0%); approximately 7.4% resulted in injury and 1.2% (65 incidents) in fatality. NHTSA warns raw numbers are not normalized by fleet size or miles traveled [NHTSA SGO](https://www.nhtsa.gov/laws-regulations/standing-general-order-crash-reporting "NHTSA Standing General Order") and [Craft Law Firm analysis](https://www.craftlawfirm.com/autonomous-vehicle-accidents-2019-2024-crash-data/ "SGO data analysis: 5,202 incidents through November 2025").

## Chapter 2: Legal and Regulatory Frameworks Across Jurisdictions
### 研究目标
- Map and compare statutory and regulatory regimes governing ADAS/ADS liability in major jurisdictions: US (federal NHTSA SGO, FMVSS, state-level AV statutes), EU (AI Act, AI Liability Directive, General Safety Regulation 2019/2144, Germany StVG §1a-1l), UNECE WP.29 (UN R157 ALKS, forthcoming ADS GTR), and China (MIIT ICV regulations, OTA approval mandates, L3 safety standards).
- Identify how each framework allocates responsibility among driver, manufacturer, and operator—and where gaps or conflicts exist.

### 关键发现
- **US Federal — NHTSA SGO**: NHTSA's Standing General Order 2021-01, third-amended effective June 16, 2025, requires manufacturers to report qualifying crashes for ADS (within 5 calendar days for severe crashes, monthly otherwise) and Level 2 ADAS (fatality, airbag deployment, hospitalization, vulnerable road user). Violations carry penalties up to $27,874/violation/day, to a maximum of $139,356,994 for a related series (as of December 30, 2024) [NHTSA SGO page](https://www.nhtsa.gov/laws-regulations/standing-general-order-crash-reporting "Standing General Order on Crash Reporting — NHTSA official page").
- **US Federal — AV Framework**: On April 24, 2025, DOT Secretary Duffy unveiled NHTSA's Automated Vehicle Framework (prioritize safety, unleash innovation, enable deployment). The first action expanded the AVEP to domestically produced vehicles. In June 2025, NHTSA streamlined the Part 555 exemption process, allowing up to 2,500 non-FMVSS-compliant vehicles/year (including those without traditional steering wheels or brakes) [DOT Press Release](https://www.transportation.gov/briefing-room/trumps-transportation-secretary-sean-p-duffy-unveils-new-automated-vehicle-framework "DOT AV Framework, April 2025").
- **US Federal — FMVSS Reform**: On March 16, 2026, NHTSA published two proposed rulemakings amending FMVSS Nos. 102, 103, 104 for ADS vehicles without manual controls. Key barriers remain in FMVSS Nos. 108, 135, 111, 101. The agency is "working toward establishing minimum performance standards for ADS competency" [Sidley Austin analysis](https://environmentalhealthsafetybrief.sidley.com/2026/03/23/nhtsa-proposes-amending-federal-crash-avoidance-standards-for-autonomous-vehicles/ "Sidley Austin: NHTSA Proposes Amending Crash Avoidance FMVSS, March 2026").
- **US Federal — SELF DRIVE Act**: H.R. 7390 (February 2026) targets L4/L5 vehicles: requires safety-case-based FMVSS for ADS vehicles, creates a National AV Safety Data Repository, preempts state regulation of ADS design/construction/performance while preserving state authority over licensing/registration/insurance. Finalization deadline: September 30, 2027 [Upstream Security analysis](https://upstream.auto/blog/the-self-drive-act-returns-why-congress-is-taking-another-shot-at-av-regulation/ "SELF DRIVE Act analysis, March 2026").
- **California**: Three-step AV permitting (drivered testing → driverless testing → deployment). December 2025 revised proposed regulations require 50,000 miles drivered testing pre-driverless permit, comprehensive safety-case submission, and expanded incident reporting. Heavy-duty vehicles (>10,001 lbs) require 1 million total test miles before deployment [Sidley Austin analysis](https://environmentalhealthsafetybrief.sidley.com/2025/12/22/the-state-of-play-in-california-for-autonomous-vehicles/ "Sidley Austin: California AV regulatory state of play, December 2025").
- **Texas**: Transportation Code §§545.451–545.459 (effective September 1, 2025): when ADS is engaged, the ADS (not a human) is deemed the "operator"; L4/L5 vehicles may operate without a human physically present; citations for ADS-operated vehicles issued to owner/authorization holder. Texas prohibits state agencies from regulating ADS beyond existing law [CMS Expert Guide — Texas](https://cms.law/en/int/expert-guides/cms-expert-guide-to-autonomous-vehicles-avs/texas-united-states "CMS Expert Guide: AV law in Texas, September 2025").
- **Florida**: Permits fully autonomous vehicles without human operators (§316.85(2)). §316.86(2) shields original manufacturer from liability for defects caused by third-party conversion. §627.749 requires $1 million primary liability coverage for driverless AV operation [Florida Bar Journal](https://www.floridabar.org/the-florida-bar-journal/navigating-the-road-ahead-floridas-autonomous-vehicle-statute-and-its-effect-on-liability/ "Florida Bar Journal: Florida AV Statute and Liability") and [FL §627.749](https://www.flsenate.gov/laws/statutes/2023/627.749 "FL §627.749 — AV insurance requirements").
- **EU AI Act**: Regulation (EU) 2024/1689, in force August 1, 2024, classifies ADS/ADAS as high-risk AI under Article 6. However, Article 2(2) carves out AI systems already governed by EU harmonized legislation (Type-Approval Regulation 2018/858, GSR 2019/2144), deferring to those sectoral frameworks. High-risk provisions apply from August 2, 2026 (August 2, 2027 for products under existing harmonization legislation). Penalties up to €15 million or 3% global turnover [Taylor Wessing](https://www.taylorwessing.com/en/insights-and-events/insights/2025/03/ai-act-and-the-automotive-industry "Taylor Wessing: AI Act and Automotive Industry, March 2025").
- **EU Product Liability Directive (Revised)**: Directive (EU) 2024/2853, transposition deadline December 9, 2026, explicitly covers software, AI, and OTA updates as "products." Cybersecurity vulnerabilities and post-sale software changes (including ML behavior drift and OTA updates) can constitute defects. Introduces rebuttable presumptions of defect and causation for technically complex cases. Standard 10-year liability period, extended to 25 years for latent injuries. The EU AI Liability Directive (fault-based) has been effectively shelved [Reed Smith](https://www.reedsmith.com/articles/the-new-eu-product-liability-key-implications-autonomous-vehicle/ "Reed Smith: New EU PLD implications for automotive/AV, October 2025").
- **EU GSR 2019/2144**: Mandates ADAS on all new EU vehicles (ISA, AEB, DDAW, ADDW, ELKS, EDR, reversing detection). Implementing Regulation (EU) 2022/1426 covers type-approval for L4 fully automated vehicles (currently limited to small series). Regulatory sandboxes and automated driving corridors planned from 2026 [Taylor Wessing](https://www.taylorwessing.com/en/insights-and-events/insights/2026/02/legal-frameworks-for-autonomous-driving-and-teledriving "Taylor Wessing: Legal framework for autonomous driving, February 2026").
- **Germany StVG**: Autonomous Driving Act (July 28, 2021) amended StVG §§1a–1l. L3 permitted in regular traffic with driver remaining ready; Mercedes DRIVE PILOT approved by KBA. L4 requires type-approval + operating license for geo-fenced areas, mandatory "technical supervision" (human-in-the-loop). Holder (Halter) bears strict keeper liability (Gefährdungshaftung) under §7 StVG with mandatory insurance. DSSAD required in all autonomous vehicles. December 2025: Road Traffic Remote Control Ordinance (StVFernLV) created framework for teleoperated driving [Taylor Wessing](https://www.taylorwessing.com/en/insights-and-events/insights/2026/02/legal-frameworks-for-autonomous-driving-and-teledriving "Taylor Wessing: German StVG liability provisions") and [UNECE presentation](https://unece.org/sites/default/files/2024-12/Presentation9-GE.3-09-12e.pdf "UNECE: German framework for autonomous driving").
- **UNECE WP.29 — UN R157**: First binding international L3 regulation, adopted June 2020, in force January 2021. Mandates: 10-second minimum transition demand, driver availability recognition, DSSAD, cybersecurity/software-update requirements (R155/R156). Original speed limit 60 km/h, raised to 130 km/h; scope extended to heavy vehicles November 2021. Revision 1 published June 2025. Applied by 56 Contracting Parties. Importantly, R157 sets safety performance standards but does not assign civil/criminal liability [UNECE](https://unece.org/transport/documents/2021/03/standards/un-regulation-no-157-automated-lane-keeping-systems-alks "UN Regulation No. 157 — ALKS").
- **China — OTA Circular**: March 1, 2025, MIIT/SAMR issued binding OTA governance requirements: activation/execution/exit policies for assisted driving, user notification, regulatory filing, 44 technical parameters, prohibition on using OTA to conceal defects [APCO](https://apcoworldwide.com/blog/road-safety-china-tightens-regulations-on-intelligent-driving/ "APCO: China tightens regulations on intelligent driving, 2025").
- **China — Advertising Ban**: April 2025, MIIT ordered 60 automakers to ban misleading terms ("automatic driving," "autonomous driving") in marketing. Driverless features (automated valet parking, smart summon, remote control) ordered disabled. Ministry of Public Security confirmed all current Chinese vehicles operate at L2 [APCO](https://apcoworldwide.com/blog/road-safety-china-tightens-regulations-on-intelligent-driving/ "APCO: MIIT April 2025 advertising bans").
- **China — Mandatory L3 Standard**: February 2026, MIIT published first mandatory L3 safety standard, effective July 1, 2027. Requires ADS to independently execute MRM (lane change, safe stop, non-obstructing parking) when driver fails take-over. Mandates DSSAD compliant with national recording standard (in force January 2026). 13-month transition for approved vehicles [ADT Media](https://www.adt.media/autonomous-driving-systems/china-tightens-rules-for-autonomous-driving/2618053 "ADT Media: China tightens L3 rules, February 2026").
- **China — Liability Framework**: Under the Road Traffic Safety Law, the human driver remains primary liability subject at L2 and below. MIIT's July 2025 ethics guidelines establish scenario-dependent liability: at L3, ADS must enable traceability to relevant persons; at L5, ADS is primary responsibility subject unless user intervenes. First L3 license plates issued in Chongqing and Beijing late 2025 [JunHe](https://www.junhe.com/legal-updates/2901 "JunHe: China AD liability framework and ethics guidelines, 2025").

## Chapter 3: Case Law and Enforcement Precedents
### 研究目标
- Analyze the most significant judicial decisions, regulatory enforcement actions, and settlement outcomes illuminating ADAS/ADS liability disputes.
- Core cases: Tesla Autopilot fatal-crash litigation (2025 Florida jury verdict, 2026 post-trial ruling), Uber ATG pedestrian fatality (2018 incident, 2023 guilty plea), GM Cruise incidents (2023 San Francisco pedestrian-dragging, NHTSA penalty, DOJ criminal admission), and emerging lawsuits.
- Extract liability principles applied—product defect, failure to warn, contributory negligence, misuse doctrine—and evidentiary issues (EDR data access, software opacity).

### 关键发现
- **Walter Huang / Tesla (2018 Mountain View)**: Apple engineer killed when Model X on Autopilot steered into a damaged crash attenuator at the Highway 85/101 interchange. NTSB found Autopilot misread faded lane markings, failed to detect the crumpled barrier via camera or radar, and accelerated to cruise speed (75 mph). Contributing factors included Huang's failure to monitor (possibly playing a phone game), Caltrans' delayed barrier repair, and Autopilot's inability to handle stationary objects. Tesla settled the wrongful-death lawsuit in April 2024; terms undisclosed [AP News](https://apnews.com/article/tesla-autopilot-lawsuit-settlement-f4c19ce05e17669de212fc262265e351 "Tesla settles Huang lawsuit, April 2024") and [NTSB/Forbes](https://www.forbes.com/sites/bradtempleton/2020/02/13/ntsb-releases-report-on-2018-fatal-silicon-valley-tesla-autopilot-crash/ "NTSB docket analysis, Feb 2020").
- **$243M Florida Verdict (August 2025)**: On August 1, 2025, a Miami federal jury ordered Tesla to pay $243 million (Tesla's share of a $329M total) in the Benavides Leon / Angulo wrongful death/injury case. Driver McGee, with Autopilot engaged on a dark rural road, was distracted and drove through flashing lights and a stop sign at 62 mph, striking a parked vehicle. Jury found Tesla 33% responsible, McGee 67%. Tesla's share: $19.5M + $23.1M compensatory + $200M punitive damages. On February 21, 2026, U.S. District Judge Beth Bloom rejected Tesla's motion to overturn, ruling evidence "more than supports" the verdict [U.S. News/AP](https://www.usnews.com/news/us/articles/2025-08-01/jury-says-tesla-must-pay-329-million-for-a-deadly-crash-involving-autopilot "AP report, Aug 2025") and [Times of India](https://timesofindia.indiatimes.com/auto/cars/us-judge-rejects-tesla-bid-to-scrap-243-million-autopilot-crash-verdict/articleshow/128641810.cms "Judge upholds verdict, Feb 2026").
- **Florida verdict — evidentiary bombshell**: Plaintiffs proved Tesla systematically withheld "collision snapshot" data (sensor/camera data uploaded to Tesla's servers, local copy marked for deletion). Tesla denied existence for years; a forensic expert recovered data from vehicle hardware, revealing Autopilot did not issue a "Take Over Immediately" alert and map data flagged the area as a "restricted Autosteer zone" yet Autopilot remained engaged. The $200M punitive damages strongly suggest jury was influenced by data concealment [Hemmings](https://www.hemmings.com/stories/hacker-uncovers-data-tesla-found-withholding-evidence-in-florida-wrongful-death-case/ "Hacker uncovers Tesla withheld evidence, 2025") and [Washington Post](https://www.washingtonpost.com/technology/2025/08/29/tesla-autopilot-crashes-evidence-testimony-wrongful-death/ "WaPo on Tesla crash data, Aug 2025").
- **Kevin Riad — First Felony Prosecution (2019 crash, California)**: Riad's Model S on Autopilot ran a red light at 74 mph in Gardena, CA, killing two occupants of a Honda Civic. First U.S. felony prosecution against a driver using widely available partial-autopilot. Riad pleaded no contest June 22, 2023; sentenced to two years probation (four-year prison sentence suspended), 31 days Caltrans work, 100 hours community service, 90 days house arrest. Established criminal liability attaches to human driver, not manufacturer [Daily Breeze](https://www.dailybreeze.com/2023/06/30/driver-of-tesla-on-autopilot-gets-probation-for-crash-that-killed-2-in-gardena/ "Riad probation sentence, June 2023").
- **Uber ATG / Elaine Herzberg (March 2018, Tempe AZ)**: Modified Volvo XC90 in autonomous mode struck and killed Elaine Herzberg, 49, crossing outside a crosswalk at 45 mph. NTSB: ADS detected pedestrian 5.6 seconds before impact but never accurately classified her (cycling through "vehicle," "bicycle," "other," "unknown"). Emergency braking was disabled by system design; safety driver Vasquez was watching "The Voice" on her phone. Vasquez pleaded guilty July 28, 2023 to "undesignated felony" of endangerment, sentenced to 3 years supervised probation. Uber settled with family (undisclosed). Prosecutors declined to charge Uber corporately despite NTSB's finding of "inadequate safety culture" [NTSB](https://www.ntsb.gov/investigations/Pages/HWY18MH010.aspx "NTSB official investigation page") and [NPR](https://www.npr.org/2023/07/28/1190866476/autonomous-uber-backup-driver-pleads-guilty-death "NPR: Vasquez plea, July 2023").
- **GM Cruise — Pedestrian Dragging (October 2, 2023)**: Cruise robotaxi dragged a pedestrian ~20 feet at ~7 mph in San Francisco after she was first struck by a human-driven vehicle. Cruise's initial NHTSA report omitted the dragging. California DMV suspended deployment/driverless testing permits October 24, 2023. NHTSA imposed $1.5M civil penalty (September 2024) for incomplete crash reporting. DOJ announced deferred prosecution agreement November 14, 2024: Cruise admitted false reporting, agreed to $500K criminal fine, 3-year compliance monitoring. CEO Kyle Vogt and co-founder Dan Kan resigned; 25% workforce cut; GM settled with injured pedestrian for at least $8M [CBS News](https://www.cbsnews.com/sanfrancisco/news/nhtsa-robotaxi-cruise-pay-penalty-failing-report-san-francisco-crash-involving-pedestrian/ "CBS on Cruise penalty, Sept 2024"), [CNBC](https://www.cnbc.com/2023/10/24/california-dmv-suspends-cruises-self-driving-car-permits.html "CNBC: DMV permit suspension, Oct 2023"), and [Reuters](https://www.reuters.com/business/autos-transportation/gm-self-driving-unit-cruise-admits-submitting-false-report-will-pay-500000-fine-2024-11-15/ "Reuters: Cruise DOJ DPA, Nov 2024").
- **NHTSA December 2023 Autopilot Recall (23V-838)**: 2,031,220 vehicles recalled — largest-ever Tesla recall. NHTSA found Autosteer controls "may not be sufficient to prevent driver misuse," characterized as "foreseeable misuse" constituting safety defect. Remedy: OTA software update adding additional visual alerts and simplified engagement controls. Tesla disagreed with analysis but deployed update. Investigation followed probe opened August 2021 after identifying 12+ crashes into stationary emergency vehicles [Reuters](https://www.reuters.com/business/autos-transportation/tesla-update-software-autopilot-control-issue-2-mln-vehicles-nhtsa-2023-12-13/ "Reuters: Tesla 2M vehicle recall, Dec 2023").
- **NHTSA FSD Escalation (2025–2026)**: October 2025: preliminary evaluation opened into 2.88M Tesla vehicles after 58 FSD safety incidents including crashes through red lights and into opposing traffic. March 18, 2026: escalated to Engineering Analysis (EA26002) covering 3,203,754 vehicles — required step before NHTSA can seek recall. Nine incidents including one fatal crash linked to camera degradation detection failures [CBS News](https://www.cbsnews.com/news/tesla-fsd-nhtsa-investigation-traffic-violations/ "CBS: FSD investigation, Oct 2025") and [Reuters](https://www.reuters.com/business/autos-transportation/us-auto-safety-regulator-opens-probe-into-tesla-vehicles-with-fsd-2026-03-19/ "Reuters: NHTSA FSD engineering analysis, March 2026").
- **Cybertruck FSD Lawsuit (Texas, March 2026)**: Plaintiff Justine Saint Amour sued after Cybertruck on FSD attempted to drive off a Houston overpass rather than following highway curve (August 18, 2025). Novel legal theory of "negligently hiring and retaining Elon Musk as CEO, allowing him to override engineer recommendations on radar/LiDAR." Strict liability claims for design and marketing defects. References December 2025 California judicial ruling that Tesla's FSD marketing is "actually, unambiguously false" [Electrek](https://electrek.co/2026/03/11/tesla-cybertruck-fsd-lawsuit-musk-negligent-hiring/ "Electrek: Cybertruck FSD lawsuit, March 2026").
- **China SPC Guiding Case (February 2026)**: Supreme People's Court issued guiding case establishing drivers remain liable even when using ADAS and even when vacating driver's seat. Underlying case: intoxicated driver activated L2 system, bypassed safety protocols, fell asleep in passenger seat. Convicted of dangerous driving, sentenced to criminal detention and 4,000 yuan fine. Establishes unified national standard: human drivers retain primary responsibility until full autonomy [Caixin Global](https://www.caixinglobal.com/2026-02-14/chinas-top-court-rules-drivers-are-liable-for-cars-with-assistance-systems-102414579.html "Caixin: China SPC ruling, Feb 2026").
- **Mercedes DRIVE PILOT**: First OEM to formally accept L3 liability when system is engaged within ODD. Type-approved in Germany (December 2021), certified in Nevada and California (2023–2024). No publicly reported crashes or lawsuits as of early 2026 [Mercedes-Benz Group](https://group.mercedes-benz.com/technology/autonomous-driving/driving/legal-framework.html "Mercedes official legal framework page").
- **Emerging Liability Principles**: (1) Product liability survives driver fault — Florida verdict shows manufacturer can be held partially liable even when driver was reckless ($243M awarded despite 67% driver fault). (2) Misleading marketing as independent liability basis — multiple cases target "Autopilot"/"Full Self-Driving" terminology. (3) Criminal liability defaults to human operator — both Vasquez and Riad cases charged the driver, not the manufacturer/developer. (4) Regulatory false reporting triggers independent corporate criminal exposure — Cruise DOJ DPA. (5) NHTSA's enforcement posture is escalating progressively (preliminary evaluation → expanded evaluation → engineering analysis).
- **Evidentiary Challenges**: (a) Tesla's collision snapshot concealment pattern — data uploaded to servers, local copy deleted, denied in discovery. (b) OTA updates create moving-target problem — pre-crash software version may differ from trial version, potential spoliation. (c) Neural network "black box" opacity — proving defect requires demonstrating training data/model architecture inadequacy (Uber case: ADS detected Herzberg but misclassified her through 4 categories in 5.6 seconds). (d) Comparative fault allocation allows shared liability between driver and manufacturer.

## Chapter 4: Liability Theory and Allocation Models
### 研究目标
- Rigorous doctrinal analysis of competing liability frameworks: strict product liability (design defect, manufacturing defect, failure to warn), negligence (manufacturer duty of care, driver monitoring obligation), vicarious/enterprise liability (OEM vs. Tier-1 vs. software supplier), contributory/comparative negligence.
- Examine the emerging concept of "automated driving system entity" (ADSE) as a single accountable legal person.
- Propose a multi-factor allocation model mapping liability shares to degree of automation active at the moment of accident.

### 关键发现
- **Design Defect — Consumer-Expectation vs. Risk-Utility Test**: Under Restatement (Third) §2(b), a design defect exists when foreseeable risks could have been reduced by a reasonable alternative design. The risk-utility test, adopted by a majority of U.S. jurisdictions, dominates over the consumer-expectation test, which is "generally considered particularly inapplicable in cases involving the analysis of technical and scientific information." For ADAS, the consumer-expectation test creates the "driver's double bind": marketing ("Full Self-Driving") forms expectations radically different from manual warnings ("driver must supervise"). Under risk-utility, the question is whether alternative designs (more robust DMS, LiDAR fusion, stricter ODD enforcement) could have reduced harm cost-effectively [Marchant & Lindor, 52 Santa Clara L. Rev. 1321 (2012)](https://digitalcommons.law.scu.edu/cgi/viewcontent.cgi?article=2731&context=lawreview "Marchant & Lindor 2012") and [Robertson, Case Western Faculty Publications 2188 (2023)](https://scholarlycommons.law.case.edu/cgi/viewcontent.cgi?article=3188&context=faculty_publications "Robertson 2023 — driver's double bind and autonowashing").
- **Manufacturing Defect and Software-as-Product**: Geistfeld (2017) argues software coding errors are analytically design defects, not manufacturing defects, because all vehicles running the same code share the same flaw. Manufacturing defect claims for AVs are "largely limited to quality-control problems with the hardware." Under the revised EU PLD (2024/2853), OTA updates and ML behavioral adaptations explicitly fall within "product" definition, and parties that "substantially modify" products via software can be deemed "manufacturers" [Geistfeld, 105 Calif. L. Rev. 1611 (2017)](https://lawcat.berkeley.edu/record/1127996/files/fulltext.pdf "Geistfeld 2017") and [Reed Smith](https://www.reedsmith.com/articles/the-new-eu-product-liability-key-implications-autonomous-vehicle/ "Reed Smith: EU PLD, Oct 2025").
- **Failure to Warn and "Autonowashing"**: Under Restatement (Third) §2(c), warnings are insufficient when users are "likely to be inattentive" or when a safer design exists — "adoption of that design is required over a warning that leaves a significant residuum of risks." Robertson (2023) analyzes "autonowashing" — manufacturers' systematic inflation of autonomous capabilities. California's December 2025 ruling found Tesla's FSD marketing "actually, unambiguously false and counterfactual," providing a legal basis for arguing that manufacturer marketing undermines its own warnings [Robertson 2023](https://scholarlycommons.law.case.edu/cgi/viewcontent.cgi?article=3188&context=faculty_publications "Robertson 2023 — autonowashing and failure to warn") and [Geistfeld 2017](https://lawcat.berkeley.edu/record/1127996/files/fulltext.pdf "Geistfeld 2017 — Restatement §2 cmt. l").
- **State-of-the-Art Defense**: Marchant & Lindor (2012) warn of acute hindsight-bias problems: plaintiff experts can almost always identify alternative approaches "the manufacturer should have known about." Under the revised EU PLD, Member States may omit the state-of-the-art defense entirely, creating potentially more expansive liability for ADAS in Europe [Marchant & Lindor 2012](https://digitalcommons.law.scu.edu/cgi/viewcontent.cgi?article=2731&context=lawreview "Marchant & Lindor 2012 — state-of-the-art and hindsight bias").
- **Manufacturer Duty of Care and Fault-Tolerant Design**: Geistfeld (2017) establishes that manufacturers must adopt "fault-tolerant designs" accounting for foreseeable human errors. Manufacturers "cannot merely warn drivers to stay 'alert'; they must instead adopt fault-tolerant designs for DAS whenever doing so would be a cost-effective method for reducing the risk of driver error." ISO 26262/SOTIF compliance is probative but not dispositive under Restatement (Third) §4 cmt. e [Geistfeld 2017](https://lawcat.berkeley.edu/record/1127996/files/fulltext.pdf "Geistfeld 2017 — fault-tolerant design obligation").
- **Foreseeable Misuse Doctrine**: NHTSA's December 2023 recall of 2,031,220 Tesla vehicles explicitly characterized driver misuse as "foreseeable misuse," establishing that when a manufacturer knows drivers will predictably over-rely on ADAS, it bears a duty to design against such misuse — not merely to warn against it [Reuters](https://www.reuters.com/business/autos-transportation/tesla-update-software-autopilot-control-issue-2-mln-vehicles-nhtsa-2023-12-13/ "Reuters: Tesla recall, foreseeable misuse framing, Dec 2023").
- **Automation Complacency and HMI Design Negligence**: Robertson (2023) draws on Bainbridge's "ironies of automation": partially automated systems create paradoxical safety risks — better automation degrades human supervisory capacity. Google abandoned L2/L3 development after internal testing revealed drivers falling asleep and streaming content. When DMS are inadequate or easily defeated (e.g., Tesla's torque-based monitoring), the manufacturer may bear negligence liability for designing an HMI that invites the complacency it claims to guard against [Robertson 2023](https://scholarlycommons.law.case.edu/cgi/viewcontent.cgi?article=3188&context=faculty_publications "Robertson 2023 — ironies of automation and HMI negligence").
- **Vicarious/Enterprise Liability — Multi-Vendor Supply Chain**: The OEM is "the party most likely to be held liable" due to deepest pockets and responsibility for the final product. Under Restatement (Third) §5 cmt. b, component suppliers owe no duty to warn ultimate consumers when the buyer is sophisticated. Tesla's camera-only "Tesla Vision" decision illustrates integrator-liability: NHTSA's 2026 findings target Tesla as the system integrator, not individual camera suppliers [Marchant & Lindor 2012](https://digitalcommons.law.scu.edu/cgi/viewcontent.cgi?article=2731&context=lawreview "Marchant & Lindor 2012 — multi-party liability").
- **Abraham & Rabin's Manufacturer Enterprise Responsibility**: Abraham & Rabin (2019) propose a strict no-fault manufacturer responsibility regime for L4–5 HAVs: manufacturers bear strict responsibility for all accidents in automated mode, funded through a vehicle sales surcharge, triggered when HAVs reach 25% market penetration, eliminating driver negligence claims [Abraham & Rabin, 105 Va. L. Rev. 127 (2019)](https://www.virginialawreview.org/wp-content/uploads/2020/12/A&R_Book.pdf "Abraham & Rabin 2019 — manufacturer enterprise responsibility").
- **Comparative Negligence and the Expectation Gap**: Florida $243M verdict: jury assigned 33% Tesla / 67% driver. Robertson (2023) argues courts should "consider collaborative driving as a system when allocating liability," recognizing human and machine performance are interdependent. The "expectation gap" functions as a liability multiplier — in modified comparative fault jurisdictions, manufacturer marketing practices may be the decisive factor in whether a plaintiff crosses the recovery threshold [Robertson 2023](https://scholarlycommons.law.case.edu/cgi/viewcontent.cgi?article=3188&context=faculty_publications "Robertson 2023 — collaborative driving system approach").
- **ADSE Concept**: Originated with Australia's NTC (2018): "when the ADS is engaged, it is the ADSE that is responsible for complying with dynamic driving task obligations." UK Automated Vehicles Act 2024 creates the "Authorised Self-Driving Entity" (ASDE) with user-in-charge criminal immunity during automated driving. Germany's Halter (keeper) strict liability under StVG §7, with doubled caps (€10M personal injury, €2M property damage) for automated driving, serves as an ADSE-equivalent. Mercedes DRIVE PILOT liability acceptance backstops the Halter's compulsory insurance [Australia NTC](https://www.ntc.gov.au/sites/default/files/assets/files/NTC-Decision-RIS-In-service-safety-for-AVs.pdf "NTC Australia — ADSE origin") and [CMS Expert Guide — Germany](https://cms.law/en/int/expert-guides/cms-expert-guide-to-autonomous-vehicles-avs/germany "CMS: German Halter strict liability and doubled caps").
- **Multi-Factor Allocation Model (Five Variables)**: Synthesizing across the literature, a liability allocation model indexes to five variables: (a) SAE level active at time of accident, (b) ODD compliance, (c) DMS status and adequacy, (d) take-over request compliance, (e) marketing/expectation gap. Geistfeld's "aggregate performance" benchmark (manufacturer satisfies tort obligation if fleet performs ≥2x safer than conventional vehicles) provides a macro-level standard [Geistfeld 2017](https://lawcat.berkeley.edu/record/1127996/files/fulltext.pdf "Geistfeld 2017 — aggregate fleet performance standard") and [Villasenor, Brookings Institution (2014)](https://www.brookings.edu/articles/products-liability-and-driverless-cars-issues-and-guiding-principles-for-legislation/ "Brookings 2014 — existing products liability framework").
- **Malfunction Doctrine and Software Opacity**: Under Restatement (Third) §3, plaintiffs can prove defect through circumstantial evidence when the product performed in a way "that ordinarily occurs as a result of product defect." For neural-network-based ADAS, where the "black box" problem makes direct identification of specific code failure practically impossible, the malfunction doctrine is critical. The Uber ATG case illustrates: the ADS detected Herzberg 5.6 seconds before impact but cycled through 4 classification categories without ever identifying her correctly [Geistfeld 2017](https://lawcat.berkeley.edu/record/1127996/files/fulltext.pdf "Geistfeld 2017 — malfunction doctrine applied to ADS").

## Chapter 5: Data Governance, Evidentiary Infrastructure, and Insurance
### 研究目标
- EDR mandates (NHTSA 2024 final rule extending pre-crash recording), DSSAD under UN R157, data ownership/access/interpretation questions.
- OTA update liability implications: post-accident update as spoliation, degraded-safety update exposure, China's 2025 regulatory approval mandate.
- Insurance model evolution: shift from driver-centric to product-liability-oriented coverage, usage-based insurance tied to ADAS telemetry, mandatory insurance pools for L3+ vehicles.

### 关键发现
- **NHTSA EDR Final Rule (December 2024)**: On December 18, 2024 (89 FR 102810), NHTSA published a final rule extending EDR pre-crash recording from 5 seconds at 2 Hz to 20 seconds at 10 Hz — tenfold increase in data sampling, fourfold increase in duration. Virginia Tech study found 20 seconds captures the 90th percentile recording duration for rear-end, intersection, and road departure crashes (~70% of all passenger vehicle crashes). Compliance date: September 1, 2027 (most manufacturers). Part 563 remains "if equipped" but 99.5% of MY2021 vehicles have EDRs, making it de facto universal [NHTSA Federal Register](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2024/12/18/2024-29862/event-data-recorders "89 FR 102810 — NHTSA EDR final rule, December 2024").
- **EDR Critical Gap — No ADAS Data**: Current Part 563 does not capture ADAS/ADS engagement status, sensor data, camera feeds, object detection, or path planning. NHTSA acknowledged the gap but deferred ADAS data elements to potential future rulemaking. EDRs capture vehicle dynamics (what happened physically) but not what the automated system perceived, decided, or commanded [NHTSA Federal Register](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2024/12/18/2024-29862/event-data-recorders "NHTSA deferral of ADAS data elements").
- **DSSAD under UN R157**: Mandated for all ALKS-equipped vehicles. Records timestamped data on system activation/deactivation, transition demands, driver responses, MRM initiation, driver overrides, and system failures. Data retained ≥6 months, accessible to authorities for accident investigation. DSSAD complements EDR: EDR reconstructs crash dynamics, DSSAD reconstructs who was in control [UN R157 Revision 1](https://unece.org/sites/default/files/2025-06/R157r1e.pdf "UN R157 Revision 1, June 2025") and [AUTOCRYPT](https://autocrypt.io/edr-dssad-vehicle-accident-analysis-tools/ "AUTOCRYPT: EDR and DSSAD comparison, Nov 2024").
- **Germany DSSAD Mandate**: StVG requires DSSAD in all L3/L4 vehicles. Data stored in the vehicle; manufacturer cannot demand exclusive access. KBA entitled to non-personal data for road safety assessment. Storage periods aligned to statutes of limitations [CMS Expert Guide — Germany](https://cms.law/en/int/expert-guides/cms-expert-guide-to-autonomous-vehicles-avs/germany "CMS: German StVG DSSAD provisions") and [Freshfields](https://technologyquotient.freshfields.com/post/102gr7g/amendments-to-germanys-laws-on-autonomous-driving-what-does-it-mean-for-data-pr "Freshfields: manufacturer cannot demand exclusive access").
- **China DSSAD Standard**: Mandatory national DSSAD recording standard took effect January 2026. February 2026 L3 mandatory safety standard requires DSSAD compliance. Must "accurately record critical operational data, allowing investigators to reconstruct scenarios after traffic accidents" [CarNewsChina](https://carnewschina.com/2026/02/26/new-chinese-regulations-push-l3-autonomous-vehicles-closer-to-l4-capabilities-with-enhanced-safety-protocols/ "CarNewsChina: China mandatory DSSAD standard, Feb 2026").
- **OTA Spoliation Risk**: When OTA overwrites pre-crash software, original decision logic may be irretrievably lost. Tesla's December 2023 recall (2,031,220 vehicles) — remedy implemented entirely via OTA — illustrates the tension: the same mechanism that "fixes" the defect simultaneously alters the software configuration that constituted the defect, complicating future litigation over pre-recall incidents [NHTSA Part 573 Report 23V-838](https://static.nhtsa.gov/odi/rcl/2023/RCLRPT-23V838-8276.PDF "NHTSA Recall 23V-838, Dec 2023").
- **EU PLD and OTA Updates**: Revised PLD (EU) 2024/2853 explicitly treats OTA updates as "products." Each safety-relevant OTA constitutes a new product placement, restarting the 10-year liability period. Parties that "substantially modify" products via software can be deemed "manufacturers." Rebuttable presumptions of defect and causation apply to technically complex cases [Reed Smith](https://www.reedsmith.com/articles/the-new-eu-product-liability-key-implications-autonomous-vehicle/ "Reed Smith: EU PLD OTA updates, Oct 2025").
- **China OTA Circular (March 2025)**: First binding mandatory global framework for AD-related OTA governance. Mandates documentation, user notification, regulatory filing (44 technical parameters), prohibition on concealing defects via OTA. Enterprises must not use OTA as substitute for proper recall [APCO](https://apcoworldwide.com/blog/road-safety-china-tightens-regulations-on-intelligent-driving/ "APCO: China OTA Circular, March 2025").
- **Manufacturer Data Asymmetry**: Tesla generates 5–10 GB raw CAN bus data per 10-minute window but historically produces only curated "EDR summaries" in litigation. Proprietary DBC file needed to decode raw CAN data is controlled exclusively by the manufacturer. Courts have found Tesla's claims to not possess crash data "not credible and appears to have been a willful and/or intentional misrepresentation" [FlyingPenguin](https://www.flyingpenguin.com/guide-to-tesla-hiding-their-crash-data/ "FlyingPenguin: Tesla crash data forensic analysis, Oct 2025").
- **Insurer Data Access Requirements**: ABI/Thatcham Research (April 2024) demand collision data "immediately available on a neutral and equitable basis" with minimum 30-second pre-collision recording. Under AEVA 2018, insurers become liable for AV-mode accidents but may lack access to data determining whether automated mode was active [ABI/Thatcham](https://www.abi.org.uk/globalassets/files/publications/public/motor/2024/insurer-requirements-for-automated-vehicles.pdf "ABI/Thatcham: Insurer Requirements for AVs, April 2024").
- **UK AEVA 2018 Insurance Model**: When an AV causes an accident "when driving itself," the insurer is directly liable — strict liability for insurers in automated mode. Insurer has subrogation rights against the manufacturer/ASDE. Policyholder becomes a potential claimant (third party to vehicle control) during automated driving — fundamentally changing motor insurance economics [AEVA 2018, Part 1](https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2018/18/part/1 "AEVA 2018 — Liability of insurers").
- **German Doubled Insurance Caps**: StVG doubled maximum keeper liability to €10M personal injury / €2M property damage for automated driving functions (standard: €5M / €1M). Mercedes DRIVE PILOT manufacturer liability acceptance backstops keeper's compulsory insurance during L3 operation [CMS Expert Guide — Germany](https://cms.law/en/int/expert-guides/cms-expert-guide-to-autonomous-vehicles-avs/germany "CMS: German doubled insurance caps").
- **Actuarial Pricing Gap**: CAS AVTF (2018) found current pricing models exhibit significant lag — a vehicle reducing losses by 50% receives only 8% premium discount after 4 years. Shift from personal auto to products liability would increase per-vehicle premiums 2–3x ($781 → $1,578–$2,355) due to higher expense ratios (38.7% vs. 34.4%), defense costs (76¢ vs. 6¢ per claim dollar), and profit targets (10% vs. 3.1%). Accident frequency would need to decrease ~75% for premium parity [CAS AVTF](https://www.casact.org/sites/default/files/2021-02/pubs_forum_18spforum_01_avtf_2018_report.pdf "CAS: Automated Vehicles and Insurance Industry, Winter 2018").
- **Market Projections**: S&P Global Mobility forecasts by 2035: ~87M L2, ~45M L2+, ~3.5M L3, ~1.7M L4 vehicles on U.S. roads. Greater China projected to lead with >23% of new sales at L3–5 by 2036; Europe and North America below 5%. Data access remains "one of the biggest hurdles" for AV insurance innovation [S&P Global Mobility](https://www.spglobal.com/automotive-insights/en/blogs/2025/08/autonomous-vehicles-future-of-car-insurance "S&P Global: AV insurance forecasts, Aug 2025").
- **UBI and Dynamic Coverage**: Insurance policies "may need to adapt dynamically depending on whether the vehicle was under human or system control." Underwriting shifting from driver demographics to "feature-level detail and software versioning." Robotaxis expected to shift to per-mile exposure (~52,000 miles/year vs. ~13,000 private) [S&P Global Mobility](https://www.spglobal.com/automotive-insights/en/blogs/2025/08/autonomous-vehicles-future-of-car-insurance "S&P Global: Dynamic insurance, per-mile exposure").
- **Mercedes DRIVE PILOT Liability Ambiguity**: Koopman (2024) analysis reveals Mercedes's "accept liability" commitment distinguishes product liability (not new) from tort liability. The DRIVE PILOT manual still places residual obligations on the user-in-charge. "What matters is who takes responsibility for the $10M+ wrongful death tort lawsuit that blows well past the insurance coverage of the driver, and which MB might (or might not) conveniently find an excuse to walk away from" [Koopman](https://philkoopman.substack.com/p/mercedes-benz-drive-pilot-and-driver "Koopman: DRIVE PILOT and driver blame, July 2024").

## Chapter 6: Emerging Trends and Forward-Looking Regulatory Recommendations
### 研究目标
- L3 conditional-automation liability inflection: Mercedes Drive Pilot liability acceptance, BMW's 2026 retreat from L3, commercial viability under current liability regimes.
- EU AI Act intersection with automotive type-approval; emergence of unified "AI product liability" doctrine.
- Ethical/AI-accountability frameworks: transparency, explainability of perception failures, algorithmic audit obligations.
- V2X communication implications for shared liability in cooperative driving scenarios.
- Concrete regulatory recommendations: tiered liability framework indexed to SAE level, mandatory DSSAD/EDR standards, OTA governance protocols, harmonized international minimum standards, model insurance mandate for L3+ ADSE entities.

### 关键发现
- **Mercedes DRIVE PILOT — Pause, Not Abandonment**: In January 2026, Mercedes confirmed it would not include DRIVE PILOT in the facelifted 2026 S-Class. Spokesperson Tobias Mueller cited restrictive ODD limiting customer value, high LiDAR costs, and Luminar's bankruptcy (former LiDAR supplier). Mercedes is deploying "MB.Drive Assist Pro" (L2++) covering urban scenarios while redirecting resources to a future L3 system at motorway speeds and an L4 prototype on public roads in Abu Dhabi [The Verge](https://www.theverge.com/transportation/860935/mercedes-drive-pilot-level-3-scrapped "Mercedes temporarily scraps L3 Drive Pilot, Jan 13, 2026").
- **BMW Abandons Level 3**: On February 24, 2026, BMW confirmed discontinuation of Personal Pilot L3 from the facelifted 7 Series. The L3 system (€5,110–€6,000) will be replaced by "Motorway Assistant" (L2++, ~€1,450, hands-off at up to 130 km/h). R&D boss Joachim Post: "We had the level 3 system in the vehicles, but we realized that the demand for this was not at a stage where we could be profitable" [BMW Blog](https://www.bmwblog.com/2026/03/17/bmw-explains-why-dropping-level-3-self-driving-tech/ "BMW explains dropping L3, March 2026") and [Automotive World](https://www.automotiveworld.com/news/bmw-drops-level-3-adas-as-industry-backing-ebbs-away/ "BMW drops L3, Feb 24, 2026").
- **Stellantis Shelves AutoDrive L3**: In August 2025, Stellantis shelved STLA AutoDrive 1.0 L3 program, which had been declared "deployment-ready" six months earlier. Driven by cost overruns, limited market demand, and broader software restructuring. Sources indicate permanent cancellation rather than postponement [Automotive World](https://www.automotiveworld.com/news/bmw-drops-level-3-adas-as-industry-backing-ebbs-away/ "Automotive World: Stellantis retreated Aug 2025").
- **L3 Commercial Viability Crisis**: The simultaneous retreat of Mercedes, BMW, and Stellantis represents a decisive inflection point. L3 is undermined by: (1) high unit costs (LiDAR, HD mapping, redundant hardware → €5,000–€6,000 option pricing with minimal uptake); (2) severely restricted ODDs (≤60 km/h, divided highways, fair weather) delivering limited value vs. L2++ systems at higher speeds; (3) unresolved tort exposure with no actuarial pricing model; (4) regulatory fragmentation (type-approval in Germany and CA/NV only) [Automotive World](https://www.automotiveworld.com/news/bmw-drops-level-3-adas-as-industry-backing-ebbs-away/ "Industry backing ebbs, Feb 2026").
- **Second-Wave L3 Commitments (2028)**: Ford announced at CES January 2026: L3 eyes-off driving in 2028 on ~$30,000 EV pickup, developing LiDAR in-house to reduce supplier dependency. GM announced October 2025: L3 on Cadillac Escalade IQ (>$127,000) in 2028, beginning with highways. Honda: 0 Series EVs targeting L3 post-2026 [CNBC](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/01/07/ford-eyes-off-driving-ev-2028.html "Ford L3 EV 2028") and [GM News](https://news.gm.com/home.detail.html/Pages/news/us/en/2025/oct/1022-AI-GM-launch-eyes-off-driving-conversational-AI.html "GM L3 Escalade IQ 2028").
- **EU AI Act — Indirect Application to Automotive**: Article 2(2) carve-out means AI Act applies "indirectly" to automotive AI through sectoral adaptation of type-approval regulations, not directly under Chapter III high-risk provisions. No concrete regulatory proposal for this adaptation has been published. High-risk AI provisions apply from August 2, 2026 (August 2, 2027 for products under existing harmonization legislation). Relevant standards: ISO 26262, ISO 21448/SOTIF, new ISO/PAS 8800 (AI/ML in vehicles), ISO/SAE 21434 (cybersecurity) [Taylor Wessing](https://www.taylorwessing.com/en/insights-and-events/insights/2026/02/faq-ki-und-software "Taylor Wessing: AI and vehicle software, Feb 2026").
- **AI Liability Directive Formally Withdrawn**: European Commission formally withdrew the AILD in its 2025 work program (adopted February 11, 2025). Official withdrawal published in OJ October 6, 2025. Commission cited "no foreseeable agreement." The revised PLD (2024/2853) becomes the sole EU instrument for AI product liability. This creates a gap: no harmonized EU fault-based civil liability framework for AI harms — left to divergent national law across 27 Member States [IAPP](https://iapp.org/news/a/european-commission-withdraws-ai-liability-directive-from-consideration "IAPP: AI Liability Directive withdrawn, Feb 2025") and [EAPIL](https://eapil.org/2025/10/09/european-commission-withdraws-two-proposals-assignments-of-claims-regulation-and-ai-liability-directive/ "EAPIL: Official withdrawal in OJ, Oct 2025").
- **China's July 2025 Driving Automation Ethics Guidelines**: Published July 23, 2025 by MOST. Four principles: people-oriented, safety first, fairness/avoid bias, informed protection. Tiered liability: L2 driver primary; L3–4 scenario-dependent with traceability to responsible persons; L5 ADS primary. Requires ADS decision-making to be "traceable and auditable" (可追溯、可审查) with algorithms "recorded and made accessible." First integration of algorithmic accountability into AD governance by a major jurisdiction [Reuters](https://www.reuters.com/sustainability/china-issues-ethical-guidelines-autonomous-driving-technology-2025-07-23/ "Reuters: China AD ethics guidelines, July 2025") and [JunHe](https://junhe.com/law-reviews/2766 "JunHe: Ethics Guidelines analysis, July 2025").
- **V2X Deployment Status**: EU: ~1.5M V2X-equipped vehicles, >2,700 RSUs across 21+ countries, >€1B EU co-funding. US: 9,300 RSUs operational, USDOT targets 20% of NHS by 2028 and full deployment by 2036. China: 20 pilot cities under "vehicle-road-cloud" strategy. Japan: 500K vehicles, 115 RSUs. Critical challenge: FCC spectrum reassignment limiting V2X to 10–20 MHz channels [Vector](https://www.vector.com/us/en/know-how/v2x/v2x-worldwide-status-and-outlook-2025/ "Vector: V2X Worldwide Status and Outlook 2025").
- **V2X Liability Gap**: V2X extends liability beyond driver and manufacturer to infrastructure operators, RSU equipment manufacturers, network providers, and municipalities. No jurisdiction has enacted a comprehensive V2X liability framework. Cooperative driving scenarios (platooning, cooperative merge) and cybersecurity risks (V2X message spoofing) create novel multi-party liability chains not addressed by existing ADSE concepts or Halter/keeper liability.
- **Recommendation 1 — Tiered Liability by SAE Level**: L2: driver primary, manufacturer bears concurrent product liability for design defects (inadequate DMS, ODD failures, marketing-induced expectation gaps), five-factor allocation model applies. L3: manufacturer/ADSE primary when ADS engaged within ODD; driver residual liability only for TOR non-compliance (10-second window per R157). L4–5: ADSE/fleet operator strict liability for all accidents during automated driving, per Abraham & Rabin (2019) manufacturer enterprise responsibility model.
