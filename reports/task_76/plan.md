# Section 1：章节研究计划

## Chapter 1：The Gut Microbiota Ecosystem — Composition, Diversity, and Core Functions
### 研究目标
- Define the gut microbiota and survey its major phyla and genera (Firmicutes, Bacteroidetes, Actinobacteria, Proteobacteria, Verrucomicrobia)
- Explain core functional roles: SCFA production, bile acid biotransformation, vitamin synthesis, mucin maintenance, immune priming, colonization resistance
- Distinguish eubiosis vs. dysbiosis and identify key factors shaping microbiota composition (diet, age, antibiotics, geography)
- Establish the foundational context required before addressing probiotics, pathogens, and disease in subsequent chapters

### 关键发现
- The total number of bacteria in a 70 kg reference man is estimated at 3.8 × 10¹³, with 3.0 × 10¹³ human cells, yielding a bacteria-to-human-cell ratio of approximately 1.3:1 (total bacterial mass ~0.2 kg). The vast majority reside in the colon (~0.9 × 10¹¹ bacteria per gram wet stool). [Sender et al., 2016](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4991899/ "Revised Estimates for the Number of Human and Bacteria Cells in the Body, PLoS Biology 2016")
- The Integrated Gene Catalog (IGC) of the human gut microbiome comprises ~9.9 million non-redundant genes from 1,267 gut samples, representing a 150- to 500-fold expansion over the ~20,000 human protein-coding genes. [Li et al., 2014](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/24997786/ "An integrated catalog of reference genes in the human gut microbiome, Nature Biotechnology 2014")
- The UHGG catalog encompasses 204,938 non-redundant genomes from 4,644 gut prokaryotic species, encoding >170 million protein sequences; >70% lack cultured representatives. A 2025 unified catalog expanded this to 14,062 species reference genomes. [Almeida et al., 2021](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41587-020-0603-3 "UHGG, Nature Biotechnology 2021"); [Unified catalog, 2025](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s40168-025-02232-5 "14,062 species catalog, Microbiome 2025")
- Firmicutes and Bacteroidetes together represent >90% of the total gut community. Additional phyla: Actinobacteria (*Bifidobacterium*), Proteobacteria (*Escherichia*, *Klebsiella*), Verrucomicrobia (*Akkermansia muciniphila*, 1–5% of community). [Magne et al., 2020](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11505267/ "Firmicutes/Bacteroidetes Ratio and Diseases, Nutrients 2024")
- The three principal SCFAs (acetate, propionate, butyrate) are present at ~60:20:20 molar ratio, accounting for 90–95% of total SCFAs. Colonic concentration: 131 ± 9 mmol/kg (caecum) to 80 ± 11 mmol/kg (descending colon). Butyrate provides ~70% of colonocyte energy (ATP). [Cummings et al., 1987](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC1433442/ "SCFA in human large intestine, Gut 1987"); [Donohoe et al., 2011](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3099420/ "Microbiome and Butyrate, Cell Metabolism 2011")
- 95% of bile acids are reabsorbed; the remaining 5% undergo microbial biotransformation (7α-dehydroxylation) to secondary bile acids (DCA, LCA) by *C. perfringens* and *C. scindens*, activating FXR and TGR5 receptors and shaping microbiota composition. [Thursby & Juge, 2017](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/microbiology/articles/10.3389/fmicb.2018.01835/full "Human Gut Microbiome, Frontiers in Microbiology 2018")
- Gut microbiota synthesizes vitamin K₂, B₅, and B₁₂; is indispensable for innate/adaptive immune maturation (GF mouse evidence); induces FOXP3⁺ Tregs and IgA production; SFB induce Th17 cells. [Al-Rashidi, 2022](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8913379/ "Gut microbiota and immunity, Saudi J Biol Sci 2022")
- Colonization resistance operates via (1) direct niche/nutrient competition and (2) enhancement of host defenses (AMPs, secondary bile acids, SIgA). Classic example: *C. scindens*-derived secondary bile acids inhibit *C. difficile* growth. [Thursby & Juge, 2017](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/microbiology/articles/10.3389/fmicb.2018.01835/full "Human Gut Microbiome, Frontiers in Microbiology 2018")
- Dysbiosis is characterized by (1) pathobiont bloom, (2) commensal loss, or (3) diversity loss. A 2024 PNAS study proposed a mechanistic definition centered on increased host-derived electron acceptors (oxygen, nitrate) shifting composition toward facultative anaerobes. [Byndloss & Bäumler, 2024](https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.2316579120 "Gut dysbiosis, PNAS 2024")
- Key shaping factors: birth mode (vaginal vs. C-section), breastfeeding (HMOs promote *Bifidobacterium*), diet (Western diet reduces diversity), antibiotics (lifelong effects possible), age, geography, and host genetics (environmental factors dominate). [Al-Rashidi, 2022](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8913379/ "Gut microbiota and immunity, Saudi J Biol Sci 2022")
- 2025–2026 advances: 14,062-species unified catalog; global genetic diversity study at strain-level (Cell, 2025); *Bacteroides* reclassification; CRISPR-based gut microbe reprogramming (UC Berkeley, March 2026); imidazole propionate linked to atherosclerosis (Nature, 2025). [Cell, 2025](https://www.cell.com/cell/fulltext/S0092-8674(25)00416-7 "Global genetic diversity, Cell 2025"); [UC Berkeley, 2026](https://news.berkeley.edu/2026/03/05/reprogramming-our-gut-bacteria-could-be-key-to-fighting-disease/ "CRISPR gut bacteria, Berkeley News 2026")

## Chapter 2：Probiotics and Prebiotics — Definitions, Mechanisms, and Functional Roles
### 研究目标
- Identify predominant probiotic genera and species (Lactobacillus, Bifidobacterium, Saccharomyces, next-generation candidates such as Akkermansia muciniphila and Faecalibacterium prausnitzii)
- Clarify the distinction between strain-specific vs. genus-level evidence for probiotic efficacy
- Define prebiotics rigorously and catalog major classes (inulin, FOS, GOS, resistant starch, HMOs)
- Explain mechanisms of probiotic action (competitive exclusion, immunomodulation, metabolite production, epithelial barrier reinforcement)
- Introduce synbiotics and postbiotics as related concepts

### 关键发现
- The 2014 ISAPP expert panel defined probiotics as "live microorganisms that, when administered in adequate amounts, confer a health benefit on the host," distinguishing them from dead microbes, microbial products, and microbial components. Certain health effects apply to probiotics as a general class, while others (e.g., immunomodulatory effects) are strain-specific. [Hill et al. 2014](https://www.nature.com/articles/nrgastro.2014.66 "ISAPP probiotic consensus, Nat Rev Gastroenterol Hepatol 2014")
- The 2017 ISAPP consensus defined a prebiotic as "a substrate that is selectively utilized by host microorganisms conferring a health benefit," expanding the concept beyond carbohydrates to include polyphenols and beyond the GI tract. The concept evolved from 1995 (Gibson & Roberfroid) → 2004 → 2008 FAO → 2017 ISAPP. [Gibson et al. 2017](https://www.nature.com/articles/nrgastro.2017.75 "ISAPP prebiotic consensus, Nat Rev Gastroenterol Hepatol 2017")
- The most studied probiotic genera are *Lactobacillus* (now reclassified into *Lacticaseibacillus*, *Limosilactobacillus*, *Lactiplantibacillus*, etc.), *Bifidobacterium*, and *Saccharomyces*. *Lactobacillus rhamnosus* GG is the most frequently studied strain; a 2015 meta-analysis confirmed its efficacy in preventing antibiotic-associated diarrhea. *S. boulardii* reduces AAD risk by ~57% in children and ~51% in adults. [Szajewska & Kołodziej 2015](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26365389/ "LGG AAD meta-analysis, Aliment Pharmacol Ther 2015"); [Szajewska et al. 2015](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26216624/ "S. boulardii AAD meta-analysis, Aliment Pharmacol Ther 2015")
- Next-generation probiotics: In the first-in-human RCT (n=32), pasteurized *A. muciniphila* improved insulin sensitivity by +28.62% (P=0.002), reduced insulinemia by −34.08% (P=0.006), and decreased total cholesterol by −8.68% (P=0.02) over 3 months. The outer membrane protein Amuc_1100 interacts with TLR2 to enhance barrier function. [Depommier et al. 2019](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41591-019-0495-2 "Akkermansia RCT, Nature Medicine 2019")
- *F. prausnitzii* represents ~5% of the fecal microbiome in healthy adults, is the most abundant butyrate producer, and produces MAM (microbial anti-inflammatory molecule) that inhibits NF-κB. Decline in *F. prausnitzii* is consistently associated with IBD, particularly Crohn's disease. No human clinical trials completed yet due to extreme oxygen sensitivity. [Al-Fakhrany & Elekhnawy 2024](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11018693/ "Next-generation probiotics review, Mol Biol Rep 2024")
- Four main probiotic mechanisms: (1) competitive exclusion via mucus-binding proteins, fimbriae/pili, SLPs; (2) immunomodulation — IgA stimulation, Treg induction, NF-κB inhibition (intestine contains 70–80% of all IgA-producing B cells); (3) metabolite production — SCFAs (acetate 36%, propionate 9%, butyrate 2% systemic bioavailability) and bacteriocins; (4) epithelial barrier reinforcement — mucin production, tight junction proteins (claudin-1, ZO-1). [Mazziotta et al. 2023](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9818925/ "Probiotics mechanism, Cells 2023")
- Major prebiotic classes: FOS and GOS (most documented; selectively metabolized by *Bifidobacterium* via β-fructanosidase/β-galactosidase); inulin (only prebiotic with EU health claim); HMOs (selectively promote *B. longum* subsp. *infantis*); resistant starch; polyphenols (90–95% reach colon unabsorbed for microbial biotransformation). [Gibson et al. 2017](https://www.nature.com/articles/nrgastro.2017.75 "ISAPP prebiotic consensus 2017")
- Synbiotics defined (2020 ISAPP, Swanson et al.): "a mixture comprising live microorganisms and substrate(s) selectively utilized by host microorganisms that confers a health benefit." Two types: complementary (each component independently qualifies) and synergistic (substrate selectively utilized by co-administered microbe). [ISAPP 2021](https://isappscience.org/a-roundup-of-the-isapp-consensus-definitions-probiotics-prebiotics-synbiotics-postbiotics-and-fermented-foods/ "ISAPP consensus definitions roundup")
- Postbiotics defined (2021 ISAPP, Salminen et al.): "a preparation of inanimate microorganisms and/or their components that confers a health benefit on the host." Pasteurized *A. muciniphila* is a leading example. [ISAPP 2021](https://isappscience.org/a-roundup-of-the-isapp-consensus-definitions-probiotics-prebiotics-synbiotics-postbiotics-and-fermented-foods/ "ISAPP consensus definitions roundup")
- 2025–2026 advances: A 2025 multicenter RCT (200 patients) showed type 2 resistant starch (40 g/day, 4 months) reduced fatty liver markers by 39.42%, with response predicted by baseline microbiota. EU expanded pasteurized *A. muciniphila* authorization to adolescents ≥12 years (Regulation (EU) 2026/391, effective Feb 2026) — the first NGP with formal regulatory approval for both adult and adolescent populations. [Prebiotic Association 2026](https://prebioticassociation.org/whats-the-latest-in-prebiotic-research-february-2026-edition/ "Feb 2026 prebiotic research roundup"); [CIRS Group 2026](https://www.cirs-group.com/en/food/expanded-use-of-pasteurised-akkermansia-in-the-eu-now-permitted-for-adolescents-aged-12-and-above "EU Akkermansia approval for adolescents")

## Chapter 3：Pathogenic Bacteria and Toxic Metabolites — The Detrimental Side of the Gut Microbiota
### 研究目标
- Identify key pathogenic/pathobiont species (Fusobacterium nucleatum, ETBF, E. coli pks+, C. difficile, E. faecalis, Desulfovibrio spp.)
- Catalog toxic metabolites produced under dysbiotic conditions (H₂S, secondary bile acids, N-nitroso compounds, TMAO, polyamines, bacterial ROS)
- Explain genotoxicity mechanisms (colibactin, cytolethal distending toxin, B. fragilis toxin)
- Describe virulence factors and adhesion mechanisms

### 关键发现
- *Fusobacterium nucleatum* subspecies animalis clade C2 (Fna C2) dominates the CRC tumor niche: detected in 29.2% of CRC patients (n=627) vs. 4.8% of healthy controls (n=619), pooled effect size 0.45 (95% CI 0.34–0.56, P=5.55×10⁻¹⁵). Fna C2 possesses unique virulence factors (*fap2*, *cmpA*, *fusolisin*) and gastric acid resistance (GDAR system). In ApcMin⁺/⁻ mice, Fna C2 significantly increased large intestinal adenomas (ANOVA P=0.0009) with 3.5-fold elevated oxidative stress. [Nature 2024](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-024-07182-w "Distinct Fn clade dominates CRC niche, Nature 2024")
- Fn virulence factors: FadA binds E-cadherin EC5, activating β-catenin/cell proliferation; Fap2 binds Gal-GalNAc on CRC cells for tumor-specific homing and interacts with TIGIT to suppress anti-tumor immunity. [Nature Communications 2025](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-025-63451-w "Structural basis of Fap2, Nat Commun 2025")
- pks+ *E. coli* produces colibactin, which alkylates DNA via cyclopropane warheads creating interstrand cross-links. Mutational signature SBS88 found in 7.5% (398/5,292) of CRCs; strongest recurrent driver mutation *APC*:c.835-8A>G (OR=65.5, 95% CI 39.0–110.0). SBS88-positive CRCs enriched in distal colon and rectum, associated with better survival (HR=0.69, 95% CI 0.52–0.90). [Science 2019](https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.aar7785 "Colibactin alkylates DNA, Science 2019"); [Georgeson et al. 2024](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10120801/ "Colibactin mutational signature in CRC")
- ETBF secretes BFT/fragilysin, a zinc-dependent metalloprotease. BFT-2 is most virulent (TC₅₀ 3.6 ng/mL vs. BFT-1's 8.9 ng/mL), determined by a single residue at position 357. BFT cleaves E-cadherin EC4, triggering β-catenin/c-Myc and STAT3/IL-17/NF-κB cascades. 80% of ETBF-positive patients developed precancerous lesions within 12–15 years. ETBF and pks+ *E. coli* frequently co-colonize CRC biofilms, synergistically promoting tumorigenesis. [Cell Chemical Biology 2025](https://www.cell.com/cell-chemical-biology/fulltext/S2451-9456(25)00425-8 "BFT structures, Cell Chem Biol 2025"); [Frontiers in Bacteriology 2023](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/bacteriology/articles/10.3389/fbrio.2023.1229077/full "B. fragilis and E. coli in CRC")
- *C. difficile*: >120,000 infections/yr in EU, ~29,000 deaths/yr in USA. Pathogenesis driven by TcdA (308 kDa) and TcdB (270 kDa) glucosylating toxins that inactivate Rho GTPases, disrupting cytoskeleton and tight junctions. Hypervirulent ribotype 027 additionally produces binary toxin CDT. [Okunye et al. 2023](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9815241/ "C. difficile pathogenicity, Virulence 2023")
- *E. faecalis* uniquely produces extracellular superoxide and H₂O₂ causing oxidative DNA damage and chromosomal instability (CIN) in colonic epithelial cells. [Huycke et al. 2002](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/11895869/ "E. faecalis ROS, Carcinogenesis 2002")
- Secondary bile acids: A 2025 Gut study provided causal evidence that 7α-dehydroxylating bacteria (*C. scindens*, *C. hylemonae*) convert primary bile acids to DCA, promoting Western diet-associated CRC in gnotobiotic mouse models. DCA promotes cancer stemness via β-catenin, triggers DNA damage in Lgr5⁺ stem cells by antagonizing FXR. [Osswald et al. 2025](https://gut.bmj.com/content/early/2025/12/17/gutjnl-2024-332243 "Secondary bile acids promote CRC, Gut 2025")
- H₂S: Produced by SRB (*Desulfovibrio*, *Bilophila wadsworthia*); exhibits bell-shaped dose-response — low concentrations stimulate CRC proliferation (Akt/ERK), high concentrations inhibit cytochrome c oxidase and induce apoptosis. CBS expression elevated in colon tumors. "Sulfur microbial diet" associated with increased distal CRC risk. [Hou et al. 2023](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9841368/ "H₂S in CRC, World J Gastroenterol 2023")
- TMAO: Microbial TMA from dietary choline/carnitine → hepatic FMO3 → TMAO. Meta-analysis: 23% higher CVD risk (HR=1.23, 95% CI 1.07–1.42) and 55% higher all-cause mortality (HR=1.55, 95% CI 1.19–2.02) with elevated plasma TMAO. Promotes atherosclerosis via macrophage foam cell formation and NF-κB activation. [Schiattarella et al. 2017](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5742728/ "TMAO and CVD meta-analysis, ESC Heart Fail 2017")
- N-nitroso compounds (NOCs): Formed by bacterial nitrosation of dietary amines (red/processed meat). Cause O⁶-methylguanine DNA adducts. Significant dose-response for rectal (P-trend=0.01) and proximal colon (P-trend=0.003) tumors with increasing NDMA consumption. [Etemadi et al. 2023](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9962651/ "NOCs and GI cancer meta-analysis, Toxics 2023")
- Colibactin cross-links are repaired via the Fanconi anemia pathway; error-prone repair generates SBS88/ID18 signatures. *E. coli* self-protects via ClbS cyclopropane hydrolase. [Nature Communications 2025](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-025-65606-1 "Fanconi pathway repairs colibactin ICLs, Nat Commun 2025")

## Chapter 4：From Dysbiosis to Disease — Intestinal Inflammation and Colorectal Cancer Pathogenesis
### 研究目标
- Explain the mechanistic chain: microbial dysbiosis → impaired barrier → bacterial translocation → immune activation → chronic inflammation
- Detail specific inflammatory pathways (NF-κB, STAT3, Wnt/β-catenin, TLR/MyD88 signaling)
- Present the inflammation–cancer sequence (colitis-associated CRC model)
- Describe microbiota-specific carcinogenic mechanisms ("driver–passenger" model, bacterial biofilm formation, epigenetic modulation)
- Summarize epidemiological and clinical evidence linking microbial signatures to CRC risk
- Highlight recent advances from April 2025–April 2026 where available

### 关键发现
- Globally, 1.93 million new CRC cases and >900,000 CRC deaths occurred in 2022 (3rd most common cancer, 2nd leading cause of cancer mortality). IBD patients carry ~2-fold higher CRC risk; cumulative CRC incidence in UC: 2%/8%/18% at 10/20/30 years. CAC accounts for ~15% of IBD-related deaths. [WHO Fact Sheet](https://www.who.int/news-room/fact-sheets/detail/colorectal-cancer "WHO CRC, GLOBOCAN 2022"); [Nardone et al., 2023](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10136846/ "Inflammation-driven CRC, Cancers 2023")
- Dysbiosis impairs barrier function by disrupting tight junction proteins (claudins, occludin, ZO-1) and the mucus layer, permitting translocation of bacteria/LPS/peptidoglycan. Basolateral PRR activation (TLRs, NOD-like receptors) triggers NF-κB and release of TNF-α, IL-1β, IL-6, IL-8. [Shahgoli et al. 2024](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11513726/ "IBD, Colitis, and Cancer, Int J Colorectal Dis 2024")
- Key signaling pathways: (1) NF-κB — central regulator, upregulates pro-inflammatory cytokines, anti-apoptotic genes (Bcl-2), and proliferation genes (cyclin D1); (2) TLR4/MyD88 — Fn activates this axis upregulating oncogenic miR-21; (3) IL-6/STAT3 — constitutively active STAT3 in stromal cells increases tumor burden; (4) Wnt/β-catenin — APC mutation in 81% of sporadic CRC but only 11–22% of CAC; (5) NF-κB/Wnt synergy via BFT; (6) IL-17/NF-κB field cancerization paradox — NFKBIZ mutations in 30% of non-dysplastic IBD but <5% of CAC. [Peng et al. 2020](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7338561/ "NF-κB and GI tumorigenesis, Front Immunol 2020"); [Zhou et al. 2023](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41389-023-00492-0 "CAC molecular mechanisms, Oncogenesis 2023")
- Colitis-associated CRC (CAC) follows inflammation–dysplasia–carcinoma sequence: early TP53 mutations (~50% of IBD dysplasia) vs. late in sporadic CRC; up to 25-fold higher mutational burden in UC colon; aneuploidy in 41% of non-dysplastic IBD lesions; "Big Bang" model — driver mutations arise simultaneously in a single clone (8/9 CAC cases); CAC tumors match CMS4 (mesenchymal) subtype. [Zhou et al. 2023](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41389-023-00492-0 "CAC molecular mechanisms, Oncogenesis 2023")
- "Driver–passenger" model (Tjalsma et al. 2012): Driver bacteria (pks+ *E. coli*, ETBF) initiate DNA damage; passenger bacteria (*F. nucleatum*, *S. gallolyticus*) colonize the altered niche and sustain tumor-promoting inflammation. Co-inoculation of pks+ *E. coli* with ETBF significantly increased tumor burden vs. either alone. [Sorbara & Pamer 2021](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8265790/ "Driver-passenger model, Gut Microbes 2021")
- Bacterial biofilms: 89% of right-sided colon tumors and 100% of right-sided adenomas are biofilm-positive vs. only 12% left-sided tumors. Biofilm-positive patients have >5-fold higher CRC risk. Biofilm-positive normal tissues show reduced E-cadherin, enhanced IL-6/STAT3, and increased Ki67 proliferation even in healthy individuals. [Dejea et al. 2014](https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.1406199111 "Biofilms in proximal CRC, PNAS 2014")
- Butyrate paradox: Cancerous colonocytes shift to glycolysis, causing butyrate accumulation → HDAC inhibition → histone hyperacetylation → reactivation of silenced tumor suppressors (p21, BAX) → apoptosis. Dysbiosis-mediated depletion of butyrate producers removes this protective epigenetic effect. [Bhat & Kapila 2023](https://academic.oup.com/microlife/article/doi/10.1093/femsml/uqad032/7199775 "SCFA epigenetics, microLife 2023")
- pks+ *E. coli* found in 67% of CRC biopsies vs. 21% of healthy controls. *Morganella morganii* produces novel indolimine genotoxins — some more potent than colibactin — increasing tumor burden in mouse models. [Nardone et al. 2023](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10136846/ "Inflammation-driven CRC, Cancers 2023")
- 2025–2026 advances: (1) Chronic colitis leaves heritable AP-1 epigenetic memories in colonic stem cells persisting >100 days, accelerating subsequent CRC growth; blocking AP-1 eliminates the effect (Nagaraja et al., Nature 2026). (2) TL1A activates ILC3s → GM-CSF → emergency granulopoiesis → tumor-promoting neutrophils (Pires et al., Immunity 2026). (3) Gain-of-function mutant p53 (R175H, R248Q) sustains NF-κB/STAT3 inflammatory signaling in CAC. [NIH 2026](https://www.nih.gov/news-events/news-releases/chronic-inflammation-leaves-long-lasting-impression-gut-stem-cells-increasing-colorectal-cancer-risk "AP-1 epigenetic memory, NIH/Nature 2026"); [Cornell 2026](https://news.cornell.edu/stories/2026/01/discovery-illuminates-how-inflammatory-bowel-disease-promotes-colorectal-cancer "TL1A/ILC3 axis, Cornell/Immunity 2026")

## Chapter 5：Probiotics as Therapeutic Allies — Mitigating Inflammation and Retarding Colorectal Cancer Progression
### 研究目标
- Synthesize anti-inflammatory mechanisms of probiotics (SCFA-mediated Treg induction, IL-10 upregulation, tight junction restoration, competitive exclusion)
- Present anti-carcinogenic mechanisms (apoptosis induction, carcinogen detoxification, β-glucuronidase modulation, immune surveillance enhancement)
- Stratify evidence by hierarchy: in vitro → animal models → human clinical trials and meta-analyses
- Cover prebiotic/synbiotic interventions in inflammation and CRC prevention
- Contextualize fecal microbiota transplantation
- Identify limitations/gaps in current evidence and notable 2025–2026 findings

### 关键发现
- Anti-inflammatory mechanisms: SCFAs (especially butyrate) activate GPR43/GPR109A on epithelial/immune cells, inducing Treg differentiation and IL-10 while suppressing TNF-α/IFN-γ (evidence: in vitro/animal). Multiple *Lactobacillus* strains suppress NF-κB, downregulating COX-2, cyclin D1, Bcl-2; EPS from *L. plantarum* L-14 inhibits IL-6/TNF-α/IL-1β via TLR4 (evidence: in vitro/animal). Probiotics restore tight junctions (claudins, occludin, ZO-1) and stimulate IgA production (evidence: in vitro/animal). [Tbahriti et al. 2025](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s12672-025-01996-4 "Probiotics in CRC, Discover Oncology 2025")
- 2025 umbrella meta-analysis (20 meta-analyses, 46 datasets): Probiotics reduced IBD relapse vs. placebo (RR=0.55, 95% CI 0.22–0.88, P<0.001) but showed no advantage over mesalazine (RR=1.00). No consistent benefit for remission induction. Very high heterogeneity (I²>90%). *E. coli* Nissle 1917 shown equivalent to 5-ASA for UC relapse prevention; VSL#3 is the most studied multi-strain formulation. ECCO/AGA guidelines recognize probiotics only as adjunctive options. [Liu et al. 2025](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12486786/ "Umbrella meta-analysis, Nutrition & Metabolism 2025")
- Anti-carcinogenic mechanisms: (1) Apoptosis induction — *L. acidophilus* cell-free supernatants reduced CRC cell viability by up to 80.65% via p53/BAX upregulation; ferrichrome from *L. casei* induces JNK-DDIT3 ER stress apoptosis (evidence: in vitro/animal). (2) Carcinogen detoxification — probiotics bind carcinogens and modulate β-glucuronidase/nitroreductase/azoreductase. (3) Immune surveillance — enhanced NK cell activity, IgA production, DC differentiation. (4) *L. gallinarum* produces ILA (anti-tumor) and ICA (boosts anti-PD1 efficacy by suppressing Treg and enhancing CD8⁺ T cells via IDO1/Kyn/AhR pathway) (evidence: murine CRC models). [Tbahriti et al. 2025](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s12672-025-01996-4 "Probiotics in CRC, Discover Oncology 2025"); [Fong et al. 2023](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10715476/ "L. gallinarum boosts anti-PD1, Gut 2023")
- Clinical evidence in CRC: Systematic review (24 RCTs, 2,204 patients) found probiotics/synbiotics reduced postoperative infectious complications by 37% (RR=0.63, 95% CI 0.54–0.74) and surgical site infection (OR=0.53, 95% CI 0.36–0.78). LGG reduced grade 3–4 chemotherapy-induced diarrhea (22% vs. 37%, P=0.027) and chemotherapy dose reductions (21% vs. 47%, P=0.008). Multi-strain, longer-duration interventions appeared more effective. [Moreira et al. 2024](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/oncology/articles/10.3389/fonc.2024.1395966/full "Pre/pro/synbiotics in CRC, Frontiers in Oncology 2024")
- FMT for UC: Cochrane review (12 RCTs, 550 participants) — FMT may increase clinical remission (RR=1.79, 95% CI 1.13–2.84; low certainty). Separate meta-analysis (13 RCTs, 580 patients): clinical remission 50.17% vs. 29.02% (RR=1.73, 95% CI 1.41–2.12, P<0.00001); higher doses (≥300 g) and multi-donor preparations more effective. Major guidelines currently recommend against routine FMT for UC outside clinical trials. [Imdad et al. 2023](https://www.cochranelibrary.com/cdsr/doi/10.1002/14651858.CD012774.pub3/full "Cochrane FMT for IBD 2023"); [Feng et al. 2023](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-023-41182-6 "FMT meta-analysis for UC, Sci Rep 2023")
- 2026 retrospective cohort (n=80 advanced CRC): 12-week fiber + multi-strain probiotics improved immune markers (IgA, IgG, CD4⁺/CD8⁺ ratio, all P<0.01), nutritional status, and was associated with higher 3-year survival (83.5% vs. 67.4%, P=0.001). Limitation: retrospective, single-center, small sample. [Wang et al. 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41823995/ "Fiber + probiotics in advanced CRC, Support Care Cancer 2026")
- Key limitations: Majority of anti-carcinogenic mechanisms demonstrated only in vitro/animal; effects are highly strain-specific with no consensus on optimal strain/dose/duration; probiotics are supported as adjunctive therapy rather than standalone CRC prevention agents; no large-scale RCT has demonstrated probiotics alone prevent CRC in humans; FMT evidence rated "low certainty" by Cochrane.

## Chapter 6：From Bench to Plate — Evidence-Based Dietary Recommendations for Gut Health
### 研究目标
- Identify dietary patterns associated with favorable microbiota profiles (Mediterranean diet, high-fiber, plant-rich diets)
- Catalog specific food categories and their microbiota effects (fermented foods, dietary fiber sources, polyphenol-rich foods, cruciferous vegetables)
- Identify foods/patterns associated with dysbiosis and elevated CRC risk (ultra-processed foods, red/processed meat, Western diet, excessive alcohol)
- Provide practical integration guidance for probiotics and prebiotics in daily diet
- Address inter-individual variability and personalized nutrition concepts
- Stratify recommendations by evidence strength (RCT-supported → mechanistic plausibility → traditional practice)

### 关键发现
- Mediterranean diet: 2024 meta-analysis (26 studies, 2,217,404 participants) — 16% CRC risk reduction (HR=0.84, 95% CI 0.78–0.91, P<0.01), consistent across sexes. Protective mechanisms via SCFA production, polyphenol-activated SIRT1/Nrf2 pathways, enhanced microbiota diversity. Evidence tier: T1 (meta-analysis). [Fekete et al. 2024](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11357-024-01296-9 "Mediterranean diet and CRC, GeroScience 2024")
- Dietary fiber dose-response: WCRF/AICR-funded meta-analysis (25 prospective studies, 1,985,552 participants, 14,514 CRC cases) — 10% CRC risk reduction per 10 g/day total fiber (RR=0.90, 95% CI 0.86–0.94, I²=0%), linear dose-response with no threshold. Cereal fiber and whole grains most protective: whole grains RR=0.83 per 90 g/day (~17% reduction). WCRF/AICR 2018 CUP confirms "probable" protection. Evidence tier: T1. [Aune et al. 2011](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3213242/ "Fiber and CRC dose-response, BMJ 2011"); [WCRF CUP 2018](https://www.wcrf.org/wp-content/uploads/2024/10/Colorectal-cancer-report.pdf "WCRF CRC Report 2018")
- IARC classified processed meat as Group 1 carcinogen and red meat as Group 2A (2015, 22 experts, >800 studies). Each 50 g/day of processed meat increases CRC risk by 18%. WCRF/AICR quantified: processed meat RR=1.16 (95% CI 1.08–1.26) per 50 g/day; red meat RR=1.12 (95% CI 1.00–1.25) per 100 g/day. Evidence tier: T1. [IARC 2015](https://www.iarc.who.int/wp-content/uploads/2018/07/pr240_E.pdf "IARC Press Release No. 240")
- Stanford FeFiFo RCT (n=36, 17 weeks, Cell 2021): High-fermented-food diet (6.3 servings/day) steadily increased gut microbiota diversity (LME P=2.3×10⁻³) and decreased 19 inflammatory markers (IL-6, IL-10, IL-12b). Diversity increase was largely indirect (~5% overlap with consumed microbes), sustained post-intervention. High-fiber diet did not increase diversity but increased glycan-degrading CAZymes; fiber response was modulated by baseline microbiota diversity. Evidence tier: T1 (RCT). [Sonnenburg et al. 2021](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9020749/ "FeFiFo fermented food trial, Cell 2021")
- Polyphenols (berries, tea, cocoa, coffee, red wine): Prebiotic-like effects promoting *Bifidobacterium*/*Lactobacillus* while inhibiting pathogens; 90–95% of dietary polyphenols reach the colon for microbial biotransformation into more bioactive forms (urolithins, equol). Evidence tier: T2 (mechanistic/preclinical). [Nutrients 2022](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9220293/ "Polyphenol-microbiota interactions, Nutrients 2022")
- Ultra-processed foods: Harvard/Tufts cohort (206,248 participants, 3,216 CRC cases, 24–28 yr follow-up) — men in highest UPF quintile had 29% higher CRC risk (HR=1.29, 95% CI 1.08–1.53, P-trend=0.01), strongest for distal colon (HR=1.72). Meat/poultry/seafood-based ready-to-eat products showed strongest association (HR=1.44). No significant overall association in women. Evidence tier: T1. [Wang et al. 2022](https://www.bmj.com/content/378/bmj-2021-068921 "UPF and CRC, BMJ 2022")
- Alcohol: Meta-analysis of 57 studies — moderate drinking (2–3 drinks/day) increases CRC risk by 21% (RR=1.21, 95% CI 1.13–1.28); heavy drinking (≥4 drinks/day) by 52% (RR=1.52, 95% CI 1.27–1.81). WCRF/AICR: "convincing" cause above ~30 g/day ethanol. Evidence tier: T1. [Fedirko et al. 2011](https://www.annalsofoncology.org/article/S0923-7534(19)38342-5/fulltext "Alcohol and CRC, Ann Oncol 2011")
- WCRF/AICR evidence matrix: **Convincing** causes — processed meat, alcohol >30 g/day, body fatness, adult height; physical activity convincingly protective against colon cancer. **Probable** protectors — wholegrains, dietary fiber, dairy products, calcium supplements. **Probable** cause — red meat. Recommendations: diet rich in wholegrains/vegetables/fruits/beans; limit red/processed meat, sugar-sweetened drinks, "fast foods," and alcohol; do not use supplements for cancer prevention. Evidence tier: T1. [WCRF 2018](https://www.wcrf.org/wp-content/uploads/2024/10/Colorectal-cancer-report.pdf "WCRF CUP 2018")
- Personalized nutrition: ZOE METHOD RCT (n=347, 18 weeks, Nature Medicine 2024) — personalized dietary program using postprandial glucose/TG responses, gut microbiome profiles, and health history achieved greater reductions in TGs (−0.13 mmol/L, P=0.016), body weight (−2.46 kg), and HbA1c (−0.05%) vs. standard USDA advice, with sustained gut microbiome remodeling. PREDICT studies (>34,000 participants): identical twins share only ~37% of gut microbiome composition. Evidence tier: T1 (RCT). [ZOE METHOD 2024](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41591-024-02951-6 "Personalized nutrition RCT, Nat Med 2024")
- Practical guidance: Start with ≥1 serving/day fermented foods, increase to 2+ (1 serving = 6 oz yogurt/kefir/kombucha, ¼ cup kimchi/sauerkraut, or 2 oz vegetable brine drink). ISAPP distinguishes probiotics from fermented foods — not all fermented foods qualify as probiotic products. Evidence tier: T1/T2. [Stanford Nutrition](https://med.stanford.edu/nutrition/education/Resources/Fermenting-the-Facts/What-Counts-as-a-Serving-of-Fermented-Foods.html "Stanford fermented food serving guidance")

## Chapter 7：Conclusions and Future Perspectives
### 研究目标
- Provide an integrative summary of the bidirectional relationship between gut microbiota and intestinal health
- Critically appraise evidence maturity across all topics covered
- Identify emerging frontiers: precision probiotics, engineered microbiota, microbiome-based CRC screening biomarkers, AI-driven dietary personalization, phage therapy
- Highlight translational gaps between mechanistic findings and clinical/dietary practice
- Anchor future perspectives to the state of the field as of April 2026

### 关键发现
- Microbiome-based CRC screening: Cologuard Plus™ (FDA-approved Oct 2024, BLUE-C study ~19,000 participants) achieved 95% CRC sensitivity and 94% specificity. Fecal microbiome signatures differentiate CRC from controls with AUC >80–99%; multi-omics models achieve AUC 83–93% across 8 countries and >2,600 participants. 4Bac panel (*F. nucleatum*, *P. micra*, *C. hathewayi*, *S. moorei*) showed higher sensitivity than FIT but lower specificity. Key challenge: geographical variation causes AUC drops from 93% to 72–82% in external validation. [Exact Sciences 2024](https://www.exactsciences.com/news-events/press-releases/fda-approves-exact-sciences-cologuard-plus-test "Cologuard Plus FDA approval 2024"); [JNCI Cancer Spectrum 2025](https://academic.oup.com/jncics/article/9/3/pkaf026/8056043 "Microbiome in CRC: bench to bedside, 2025")
- Precision/engineered probiotics: SNIPR001 (first CRISPR-armed phage in humans) Phase 1 trial (36 volunteers) well tolerated, dose-dependent *E. coli* reduction; FDA Fast Track designation. Engineering bifidobacteria to deliver CRISPR antimicrobials targeting *F. nucleatum* in CRC validated preclinically (2026). >150 microbiome therapeutics in clinical development; microbiome therapeutic market projected at $1.6B by 2026. AGA reported a Phase 1 engineered probiotic for IBD planned in 2026. [SNIPR Biome 2023](https://lundbeckfonden.com/business-activities/biocapital-portfolio-news/snipr-biome-reports-positive-clinical-interim-results "SNIPR001 Phase 1, 2023"); [PubMed 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41496929/ "CRISPR bifidobacteria targeting Fn, 2026")
- Phage therapy: Landmark 100-case study (35 hospitals, 12 countries) — 77.2% clinical improvement, 61.3% bacterial eradication; eradication 70% less probable without concomitant antibiotics. Hydrogel-encapsulated phage cocktails (Nat Commun 2025) achieved ~2,000-fold *Salmonella* reduction while preserving commensal diversity. EMA developing regulatory guidelines; EU SoHO Regulation (2027) will explicitly include human microbiomes. Key challenge: personalized phage selection conflicts with standardized pharmaceutical models. [Pirnay et al. 2024](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41564-024-01705-x "100-case phage therapy, Nat Microbiol 2024"); [Nat Commun 2025](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-025-65498-1 "Hydrogel phage cocktails, Nat Commun 2025")
- AI-driven dietary personalization: Systematic review (2025) found AI-based approaches improved glycemic control, weight management, and microbiome diversity vs. conventional advice. BEAUT deep-learning tool (Cell 2025) identifies novel bile acid metabolizing enzymes in gut bacteria. AI personalized nutrition market projected at $4.26B by 2032. [PMC 2025](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12193492/ "AI dietary recommendations systematic review, 2025"); [Cell 2025](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0092867425008062 "BEAUT BA enzyme discovery, Cell 2025")
- Regulatory/translational gaps: Only 2 approved microbiome medicinal products (Rebyota™, VOWST®, both for rCDI) despite >150 in development. EU SoHO Regulation (EU 2024/1938, August 2027) will first explicitly regulate human microbiomes. Five major translational barriers: study design complexity, lack of validated analytical methods, no consensus "healthy" microbiome definition, inter-individual variability, and inadequacy of traditional PK/PD models. Cross-jurisdictional regulatory fragmentation impedes global development. [Rodriguez et al. 2025](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41522-025-00683-0 "Regulatory framework for microbiome therapies, npj Biofilms Microbiomes 2025")
- Unresolved questions: (1) Causation vs. correlation — definitive causal evidence for most candidate driver organisms lacking in human CRC in vivo; (2) "Hit-and-run" hypothesis difficult to validate experimentally; (3) ~80% of gut microbial species remain uncultured "dark matter"; (4) No consensus definition of "healthy" vs. "dysbiotic" microbiome; (5) Geographical/ethnic variation in CRC-associated microbial signatures complicates universal diagnostic panels; (6) Probiotic evidence for CRC remains largely adjunctive with insufficient standalone evidence. [JNCI Cancer Spectrum 2025](https://academic.oup.com/jncics/article/9/3/pkaf026/8056043 "Microbiome in CRC, 2025")
- Evidence maturity synthesis: **Strong** — microbiota-CRC association, dietary fiber/Mediterranean diet protection, processed meat carcinogenicity. **Moderate** — FMT for IBD remission, probiotics as surgical adjunct, microbiome screening signatures. **Early-stage** — precision/engineered probiotics (Phase 1), phage therapy (preclinical for CRC), AI-driven personalization (proof-of-concept), CRISPR microbiota editing.

# Section 2：给 Write 阶段的执行建议

- **术语一致性**：全文使用 "gut microbiota" 指代微生物群落本身，仅在讨论集体基因组内容时使用 "gut microbiome"，避免使用旧称 "intestinal flora"（除历史语境外）。统一使用 "dysbiosis"，不混用 "dysbacteriosis" 或 "microbial imbalance"。物种命名使用斜体，注意 *Lactobacillus* 属近年的重分类（*Lacticaseibacillus*、*Limosilactobacillus* 等），选定一种惯例后全文统一并加脚注说明。
- **语气与文体**：保持正式学术研究报告风格，面向有一定知识背景但不一定是专业人士的读者；首次使用技术术语时给出定义但不居高临下。避免对益生菌或特定产品的推广性语言，保持科学中立。因果证据不充分时使用审慎措辞（"evidence suggests""has been associated with"），仅在有强力干预或机制数据支持时才断言因果关系。
- **证据质量标注**：该领域动物模型与人体临床证据之间存在显著鸠距。讨论机制或疗效的章节应明确标注支持证据来源（体外实验、动物模型需注明物种、人体临床试验需注明设计类型：RCT/队列/病例对照）。Chapter 5 尤其需要对证据层级进行坦诚评估。不得将仅有动物数据的发现作为已确立的临床事实呈现。
- **跨章节一致性**：Chapter 2 和 Chapter 3 构成概念对（有益 vs. 有害），应使用平行结构、可比深度和一致术语。Chapter 4 直接引用 Chapter 2–3 中介绍的实体——使用明确的交叉引用而非重新定义。Chapter 5 与 Chapter 4 形成镜像（致病机制 → 对策），应结构化地将干预证据映射回 Chapter 4 中描述的具体通路。Chapter 6 应引用 Chapter 2–5 的具体发现来支撑每条饮食建议。
