# Section 1：章节研究计划

## Chapter 1：Theoretical Foundations — Collective Memory, Cultural Memory, and the Politics of the Past
### 研究目标
- Establish the conceptual apparatus underpinning the entire report: survey major theoretical traditions in memory studies (Halbwachs, Nora, Jan/Aleida Assmann, Erll, Kansteiner, Rothberg).
- Clarify key definitional distinctions: history vs. memory, communicative vs. cultural memory, official vs. vernacular memory.
- Explain why these frameworks matter for analysing contemporary reinterpretation of the past.
- Core questions: What are the dominant theoretical frameworks? How do scholars distinguish "history" from "memory"? What analytical tools do these frameworks offer for diagnosing ideologisation and instrumentalisation?

### 关键发现
- Maurice Halbwachs (1877–1945) established the sociological foundation for collective memory studies in *Les cadres sociaux de la mémoire* (1925), arguing that individual memory operates within collective social frameworks — memory depends on socialisation, communication, and group membership. His posthumous *La mémoire collective* (1950) expanded this framework. [University of Chicago Press](https://press.uchicago.edu/ucp/books/book/chicago/O/bo3619875.html "On Collective Memory — English translation edited by Lewis A. Coser, 1992")
- Pierre Nora's landmark essay "Between Memory and History: Les Lieux de Mémoire" (*Representations*, No. 26, Spring 1989, pp. 7–24) introduced the influential opposition between "memory" and "history": memory is "life, borne by living societies," while history is "the reconstruction, always problematic and incomplete, of what is no longer." His multi-volume *Les lieux de mémoire* project (Gallimard, 1984–1992) created a new genre of national memory cartography subsequently adapted in Italy, Germany, the Netherlands, and elsewhere. [UC Press / *Representations*](https://online.ucpress.edu/representations/article/doi/10.2307/2928520/82272/Between-Memory-and-History-Les-Lieux-de-Memoire "Between Memory and History, 1989, pp. 7–24")
- Jan Assmann distinguished "communicative memory" (everyday, informal, orally transmitted, ~80–100 year horizon) from "cultural memory" (institutionalised, exteriorised in stable symbolic forms, maintained by specialists, extending to an "absolute past"). This typology was developed in *Das kulturelle Gedächtnis* (Beck, 1992) and his 2008 chapter "Communicative and Cultural Memory" (De Gruyter, pp. 109–118). He introduced the "identity-index": "Memory is knowledge with an identity-index, it is knowledge about oneself." [Jan Assmann](https://archiv.ub.uni-heidelberg.de/propylaeumdok/1774/1/Assmann_Communicative_and_cultural_memory_2008.pdf "Communicative and Cultural Memory, De Gruyter 2008, pp. 109–118")
- Aleida Assmann introduced "canon" (functional/working memory — actively circulated, identity-constitutive selections) and "archive" (storage memory — preserved but not actively circulating materials that may be reactivated), in *Cultural Memory Studies* (De Gruyter, 2008, pp. 97–108). Her broader synthesis *Cultural Memory and Western Civilization* (Cambridge UP, 2011) further developed these concepts. She and Jan Assmann received the Peace Prize of the German Book Trade in 2018. [De Gruyter](https://www.degruyterbrill.com/document/doi/10.1515/9783110207262.2.97/html "Canon and Archive, 2008/2010")
- Astrid Erll's *Memory in Culture* (Palgrave Macmillan, 2011) provided a comprehensive introduction to memory studies as an interdisciplinary field, translated into five languages. Her concept of "travelling memory" rejects memory as static or nationally contained, emphasising how memory is constituted through the movement of people, media, and practices across borders. Further developed in *Travels in Time: Essays on Collective Memory in Motion* (Oxford UP, 2025). [OUP](https://global.oup.com/academic/product/cognition-culture-and-political-momentum-9780197788332 "Erll bio confirming 2011 publication and Travels in Time, 2025")
- Wulf Kansteiner's "Finding Meaning in Memory: A Methodological Critique of Collective Memory Studies" (*History and Theory*, Vol. 41, No. 2, May 2002, pp. 179–197, over 2,500 citations) challenged the field to attend to the **reception side** — what audiences actually do with memory representations — rather than assuming cultural production automatically translates into collective remembering. [Google Scholar](https://scholar.google.com/citations?user=4n-4cBAAAAAJ&hl=en "Kansteiner profile showing 2,572 citations for the 2002 article")
- Michael Rothberg's *Multidirectional Memory: Remembering the Holocaust in the Age of Decolonization* (Stanford UP, June 2009) challenged the "competitive memory" framework (zero-sum struggles over victim status), proposing instead that memories of distinct historical events interact productively — memory of one atrocity enabling rather than diminishing attention to another. Demonstrated through readings of Arendt, Césaire, Delbo, and Du Bois. Extended in *The Implicated Subject: Beyond Victims and Perpetrators* (2019). [Stanford University Press](https://www.sup.org/books/literary-studies-and-literature/multidirectional-memory "Publisher page, release date June 2009")
- Jeffrey K. Olick's "Collective Memory: The Two Cultures" (*Sociological Theory*, Vol. 17, No. 3, 1999, over 2,260 citations) distinguished "collected memory" (aggregation of socially framed individual memories) from "collective memory" *sui generis* (public commemorations, national narratives, institutional archives), advocating "multidimensional rapprochement." [SAGE Journals](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1111/0735-2751.00083 "Article abstract and reference list")
- Michel-Rolph Trouillot's *Silencing the Past: Power and the Production of History* (Beacon Press, 1995; 20th anniversary edition with foreword by Hazel V. Carby, 2015) identified four moments where power produces historical silences: (1) fact creation/sources, (2) fact assembly/archives, (3) fact retrieval/narratives, (4) retrospective significance/history. The Haitian Revolution served as the central case — "unthinkable" within prevailing racial hierarchies. [Beacon Press](https://www.beacon.org/Silencing-the-Past-P1109.aspx "Publisher page with description and publication date")
- **Field evolution (key inflection points)**: 1925 (Halbwachs's sociological origins) → 1984–1992 (Nora's cultural turn) → 1992–1995 (Assmanns' typological refinement + Trouillot's power analysis) → 1999 (Olick's two-cultures intervention) → 2002 (Kansteiner's reception critique) → 2009 (Rothberg's multidirectional turn) → 2011 (Erll's travelling memory + institutional consolidation via *Memory Studies* journal and Frankfurt Platform).
- **Recent developments (2024–2026)**: (a) *Memory Studies Review* launched by Brill in 2024, with inaugural focus on "Memory and Environment" signalling convergence with environmental humanities [*Memory Studies Review*](https://brill.com/view/journals/mesr/mesr-overview.xml "Journal overview, Brill 2024"). (b) A 2024 special issue "Is Artificial Intelligence the Future of Collective Memory?" (edited by Gensburger & Clavert) examined how generative AI transforms collective memory practices [HAL](https://hal.science/hal-04839884v1 "Introduction to special issue, DOI: 10.1163/29498902-202400019"). (c) Erll & Hirst's *Cognition, Culture, and Political Momentum* (OUP, est. January 2026, 464 pp.) represents the most ambitious attempt to bridge psychological and cultural memory research, featuring Olick's "The Two Cultures Revisited" and Kansteiner's "Meta-Memory" concept [Oxford University Press](https://global.oup.com/academic/product/cognition-culture-and-political-momentum-9780197788332 "OUP product page with TOC"). (d) Ann Rigney's *Remembering Hope: The Cultural Afterlife of Protest* (Oxford UP, 2025) links memory studies to activism and "transformative memory." (e) First full English translation of Halbwachs's 1925 *Les cadres sociaux* forthcoming (Oxford UP, 2025, ed. John Sutton).
- **Analytical tools for diagnosing instrumentalisation**: Trouillot's four moments of silencing; Aleida Assmann's canon/archive dynamics (canonisation vs. functional forgetting); Rothberg's competitive vs. multidirectional memory framework; Kansteiner's reception-side methodology (requiring demonstration of actual uptake, not just production of narratives).

## Chapter 2：The Ideologisation of History — When the Past Becomes Doctrine
### 研究目标
- Examine how historical narratives are absorbed into ideological systems (nationalist, populist, authoritarian, identitarian) so that the past becomes a fixed script legitimising present power arrangements.
- Analyse mechanisms of ideologisation: state curricula, official historiography, propaganda ecosystems, legislative mandates ("memory laws").
- Provide comparative case studies: ethno-nationalist history in post-Soviet states, U.S. history-education battles, state-directed narratives in China or Turkey.
- Engage with the scholarly debate over whether all historiography is inherently ideological or whether a meaningful boundary exists between interpretation and instrumentalisation.
- Core questions: Through what mechanisms do states convert historical scholarship into ideological doctrine? How do recent legislative interventions function as instruments of ideologisation? Is there a defensible distinction between interpretive pluralism and ideologisation?

### 关键发现
- Hayden White's *Metahistory* (1973) argues all historical writing is structured by literary and rhetorical devices — four master tropes, four emplotment modes, four ideological implications — so that narrative form itself carries ideological weight irrespective of factual accuracy. In *The Content of the Form* (1987), he contends that narrativity naturalises particular social and moral orders. His "Politics of Historical Interpretation" essay (*Critical Inquiry*, 1982) argues the discipline of history domesticates the past's disruptive potential into orderly narratives, and objectivity claims are themselves ideological. Critics (Ginzburg, Momigliano) argue White's narrativism inadvertently provides cover for denial and distortion. [Hopkins Press](https://press.jhu.edu/books/title/2540/content-form "Publisher page for The Content of the Form, 1987") [JSTOR](https://www.jstor.org/stable/1343276 "White, 'Politics of Historical Interpretation,' Critical Inquiry 1982") [Open Research Europe](https://open-research-europe.ec.europa.eu/articles/5-259 "Scholarly analysis of White's narrativism, 2025")
- Hobsbawm and Ranger's *The Invention of Tradition* (1983) defines "invented traditions" as practices of ritual or symbolic nature that "seek to inculcate certain values and norms of behaviour by repetition, which automatically implies continuity with the past" — where that continuity is "largely factitious." This framework directly diagnoses how states fabricate historical legitimacy. [Cambridge University Press](https://psi424.cankaya.edu.tr/uploads/files/Hobsbawm_and_Ranger_eds_The_Invention_of_Tradition.pdf "Full text, 1983 edition")
- Nikolay Koposov's *Memory Laws, Memory Wars* (CUP, 2018) documents the proliferation of memory laws from 5 in the 1980s to 27 in the 2000s and traces a critical transformation: memory laws originated as democratic instruments (Germany's 1985 Holocaust denial ban, France's 1990 Gayssot Act) but became "weapons not of the weak, but of the strong" as authoritarian states adopted them to enforce mythical national histories. The 2008 Blois Appeal (Nora, Ginzburg et al.) declared: "In a free state, no political authority has the right to define historical truth and to restrain the freedom of the historian with the threat of penal sanctions." [Los Angeles Review of Books](https://lareviewofbooks.org/article/weapons-not-of-the-weak-but-of-the-strong-on-nikolay-koposovs-memory-laws-memory-wars-the-politics-of-the-past-in-europe-and-russia "Douglas Smith review, LARB, April 2018")
- **United States**: Since January 2021, 44 states introduced bills restricting teaching on race and racism; 20 states imposed bans. By mid-2025, over 70 bills across 26 states targeted higher education censorship, with 22 becoming law in 16 states; 7 higher-education censorship laws passed in 2025 alone; almost 40% of the U.S. population now lives in states with such laws. Mechanisms include: educational gag orders on "divisive concepts," curricular mandates, governance restructuring transferring faculty control to politically-appointed bodies, "institutional neutrality" mandates, and interference with accreditation. [Education Week](https://www.edweek.org/policy-politics/map-where-critical-race-theory-is-under-attack/2021/06 "CRT Tracker, updated January 2026") [PEN America](https://pen.org/with-a-wave-of-new-bills-in-2025-state-legislators-cast-a-web-of-control-over-higher-education/ "PEN America report, July 2025")
- **Russia**: Article 354.1 of the Russian Criminal Code (2014) criminalises "rehabilitation of Nazism" but functions to shield the official WWII "Great Patriotic War" narrative from criticism. After the full-scale Ukraine invasion, Article 13.48 (April 2022) banned comparing Soviet and Nazi actions and denying "the decisive role of the Soviet people." By July 2023, 620 people had been indicted in 78 regions on wartime-narrative-related charges, 81 sentenced to imprisonment. Memorial International, the Nobel Prize-winning organisation documenting Soviet repression, was dissolved by court order in December 2021. [Verfassungsblog](https://verfassungsblog.de/weaponizing-russias-memory-law/ "Nekoliak & Klochkova, 'Weaponizing Russia's Memory Law,' July 2023")
- **China**: The CCP's November 2021 "Resolution on the Major Achievements and Historical Experience of the Party over the Past Century" (only the third such resolution in party history) established Xi Jinping's era as the "New Era" with his thought as "the scientific guiding force for the Sinicization of Marxism." The accompanying party history study campaign was described as "a massive party history propaganda and education campaign on a scale unseen since the Mao era." Internet regulators created a platform to combat "historical nihilism" (*lishi xuwuzhuyi*), converting historical debate into a punishable offence. The campaign's biological metaphor — "inherit red genes" — merges ideological loyalty with identity formation. [CSIS Interpret](https://interpret.csis.org/translations/resolution-of-the-ccp-central-committee-on-the-major-achievements-and-historical-experience-of-the-party-over-the-past-century/ "CSIS translation") [Jamestown Foundation](https://jamestown.org/the-2021-party-history-study-campaign-stresses-revolution-and-sacrifice/ "Jamestown analysis, June 2021")
- **Poland**: PiS's January 2018 IPN amendment criminalised attributing Nazi crimes to the Polish nation/state (criminal sanctions in force March–July 2018, then repealed under international pressure; civil liability retained). Anna Wójcik (2023) argues Poland's memory laws constituted "a perversion of classic European memory laws" and "examples of penal populism and populist revanchism." PiS interfered with the Museum of WWII in Gdańsk, doubled the IPN budget, changed curricula, and deployed "pedagogy of shame" (*pedagogika wstydu*) to delegitimise critical scholarship. After PiS's October 2023 defeat, the Tusk government began reversing some policies, but civil liability provisions and IPN institutional changes remain. [Anna Wójcik, *Journal of Illiberalism Studies*](https://www.illiberalism.org/memory-laws-rule-of-law-and-democratic-backsliding/ "Wójcik, JIS 3(3), Fall 2023") [Lossi 36](https://lossi36.com/2024/09/13/opinion-it-is-time-to-dismantle-piss-memory-politics/ "Lossi 36, September 2024")
- **Hungary**: A 2026 University of Amsterdam PhD dissertation (Réka Deim) demonstrates that "memory politics has become a central ideological instrument in Viktor Orbán's illiberal regime." The 2014 Memorial for Victims of the German Occupation suggests Hungary was solely a Nazi victim, suppressing Hungarian co-responsibility for Holocaust persecution; citizens created a counter-memorial ("Living Memorial"). The 2011 Fundamental Law constitutionalises ethno-national historical identity. The House of Terror Museum emphasises Hungarian victimhood under communism while downplaying Nazi collaboration. [University of Amsterdam](https://www.uva.nl/shared-content/uva/en/news/news/2026/02/commemoration-as-a-tool-of-power-in-hungary.html "UvA, February 2026")
- **Turkey**: Under Erdoğan/AKP, the 1453 Constantinople conquest commemoration has been transformed into a massive "Conquest Festival" (*Fetih Şöleni*) — an instrument of neo-Ottoman ideologisation. The Panorama 1453 History Museum (2009) and Hagia Sophia's 2020 mosque reconversion physically recreate Ottoman "conquest" as contemporary political acts. Infrastructure is named after Ottoman sultans and timed to anniversary dates. A law criminalising the term "genocide" for 1915 Armenian massacres exemplifies what Koposov calls the "reversal" of memory laws. Dissard & Kurşunlugil (2021) argue these commemorations disguise the celebration of 20th-century "ethnic and religious purification." [Dissard & Kurşunlugil, *L'Espace Politique*](https://journals.openedition.org/espacepolitique/8806 "L'Espace Politique 41, 2021")
- **Cross-cutting mechanisms** identified across all five cases: (1) legislative codification (memory laws, gag orders); (2) curriculum mandates (state-directed rewriting of syllabi); (3) institutional capture (museums, research institutes); (4) commemorative engineering (monuments, festivals); (5) discursive disciplining ("historical nihilism," "pedagogy of shame," "rehabilitation of Nazism").
- **Interpretive pluralism vs. ideologisation**: The distinction can be maintained along four axes: (1) institutional coercion (legal penalties for divergent claims); (2) the Blois Appeal principle (no political authority may define historical truth); (3) closure of debate (ideologisation forecloses inquiry; pluralism accepts competing interpretations); (4) Hobsbawm's "invention" criterion (fabricated continuity with the past).

## Chapter 3：Reclaiming Silenced Narratives — Subaltern, Decolonial, and Counter-Hegemonic Historiographies
### 研究目标
- Analyse how marginalised communities challenge dominant historical narratives by recovering suppressed archives, oral traditions, and alternative epistemologies.
- Engage with decolonial theory (Mignolo, Quijano, Ndlovu-Gatsheni), subaltern studies (Spivak, Guha), and critical race approaches to historiography.
- Use recent examples: post-colonial repatriation debates, Indigenous knowledge reclamation, African storytelling-as-reparation initiatives, LGBTQ+ counter-histories.
- Examine tensions when counter-narratives themselves become institutionalised or co-opted.
- Core questions: What strategies are deployed to recover suppressed histories? How do decolonial/subaltern methods differ epistemologically from mainstream Western practice? What tensions arise when counter-narratives are institutionalised?

### 关键发现
- Trouillot's *Silencing the Past* (1995) identifies four moments where power produces historical silences: source creation, archival assembly, narrative construction, and retrospective significance. The Haitian Revolution was his central case — rendered "unthinkable" because it "challenged the ontological and political assumptions of the most radical writers of the Enlightenment." A 20th-anniversary edition (2015) with foreword by Hazel V. Carby underscored its enduring importance; it also inspired Raoul Peck's 2021 HBO miniseries *Exterminate All the Brutes*. [Beacon Press](https://www.beacon.org/Silencing-the-Past-P1109.aspx "Publisher page") [Ndlovu-Gatsheni](https://library.oapen.org/bitstream/handle/20.500.12657/41376/9781138588578_oachapter1.pdf "Epistemic Freedom in Africa, Ch.1, 2018, quoting Trouillot 1995:26")
- Spivak's "Can the Subaltern Speak?" (1985 *Wedge*; 1988 in *Marxism and the Interpretation of Culture*; substantially revised in *A Critique of Postcolonial Reason*, 1999) argues the subaltern cannot "speak" because colonial and postcolonial power structures foreclose self-representation within dominant epistemic systems. Spivak critiques Western intellectuals (Foucault, Deleuze) who presume to "let the subaltern speak" while re-inscribing the structures that silence them, using *sati* (widow self-immolation) to expose how both colonial British and indigenous patriarchal narratives erase subaltern women's subjectivity. The essay had "a serious impact on the later intellectual trajectory" of the Subaltern Studies project. [Chakrabarty](https://files.libcom.org/files/subaltern.pdf "Subaltern Studies and Postcolonial Historiography, Nepantla 1:1, 2000, pp. 24–25") [Literary Encyclopedia](https://www.litencyc.com/php/sworks.php?rec=true&UID=19945 "Entry on 'Can the Subaltern Speak?'")
- The Subaltern Studies collective, founded by Ranajit Guha in 1982, declared both Cambridge and nationalist-Marxist schools of Indian history "elitist," positing an "autonomous domain" of subaltern politics. Guha rejected Hobsbawm's category of the "pre-political," insisting peasant insurgency was a contemporary, autonomous form of political action. He developed a critical reading strategy for colonial archives — reading not just for elite bias but for the "textual properties" of documents that reproduce domination. The project expanded globally (Latin American Subaltern Studies Association, 1992) but faced Marxist critique for fragmenting class-based solidarity. [Chakrabarty](https://files.libcom.org/files/subaltern.pdf "Chakrabarty, Nepantla 1:1, 2000, pp. 9–27")
- Chakrabarty's *Provincializing Europe* (Princeton UP, 2000; reissued 2007) argues European thought must be recognised as particular rather than universal. His distinction between "History 1" (universalising logic of capitalist modernity) and "History 2" (subaltern pasts that resist incorporation) creates space for the "politics of human diversity." He challenges "historicism" — the assumption that modernity appears "first in Europe and then elsewhere," relegating non-Western societies to a "waiting room." Bourke's 2025 reassessment in *Global Intellectual History* acknowledges innovations while critiquing some conclusions. [Not Even Past](https://notevenpast.org/dipesh-chakrabartys-provincializing-of-europe/ "University of Texas review") [Bourke](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/23801883.2025.2497092 "Bourke, 'Provincializing Europe Revisited,' Global Intellectual History, May 2025")
- Mignolo's "Epistemic Disobedience, Independent Thought and Decolonial Freedom" (*Theory, Culture & Society* 26(7–8), 2009) calls for conscious refusal to comply with Eurocentric epistemic norms and insists on "geo- and body-politics of knowledge" — all knowledge is produced from a specific location, and the pretension of a "zero-point epistemology" (view from nowhere) is imperial. [Mignolo](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/0263276409349275 "Theory, Culture & Society, 2009")
- Quijano's "coloniality of power" (*colonialidad del poder*; first articulated 1991; landmark essay in *Nepantla* 1(3), 2000, pp. 533–580) argues that the colonial classification of populations by race became the foundational axis of capitalist power, organising labour, knowledge, authority, and intersubjective relations globally. Coloniality persists long after formal colonial administration. Duke University Press published *Foundational Essays on the Coloniality of Power* (2024, ed. Walsh, Mignolo, Segato). [Duke UP](https://dokumen.pub/anibal-quijano-foundational-essays-on-the-coloniality-of-power-1478030321-9781478030324.html "Duke UP, 2024")
- Ndlovu-Gatsheni's *Epistemic Freedom in Africa* (Routledge, 2018) argues "epistemic freedom" — "the right to think, theorize, interpret the world, develop own methodologies and write from where one is located" — is foundational to political and economic freedoms. He traces colonial epistemicide, linguicide, and "theft of history" through forced conversion, colonial education, and the asymmetric global division of intellectual labour. His concept of "deprovincializing Africa" extends Chakrabarty's project into an explicitly decolonial framework. [Ndlovu-Gatsheni](https://library.oapen.org/bitstream/handle/20.500.12657/41376/9781138588578_oachapter1.pdf "Epistemic Freedom in Africa, Ch.1, 2018, Open Access")
- **Benin Bronzes repatriation (2022–2026)**: Cambridge transferred ownership of 116 bronzes to Nigeria (February 2026); Switzerland returned 34 objects (March 2026); Netherlands returned 119 artefacts (June 2025); Germany transferred over 1,000 in 2022. The Digital Benin database lists over 5,000 artefacts across 131 institutions in 20 countries. NCMM Director-General Holloway stated: "The return of cultural items for us is not just the return of the physical object, but also the restoration of the pride and dignity that was lost." [Museums Association](https://www.museumsassociation.org/museums-journal/news/2026/02/cambridge-university-transfers-ownership-of-116-benin-bronzes-to-nigeria/ "Museums Association, Feb 2026") [MoMAA](https://momaa.org/benin-bronzes-2026-how-zurichs-museum-rietberg-returned-11-objects-to-nigeria/ "MoMAA, Mar 2026")
- **Indigenous land-back (2025)**: The Yurok Tribe reclaimed over 125 square miles in the largest California land-back deal; Leonard Peltier was released after nearly 50 years; Juukan Gorge traditional owners secured veto power over mining after Rio Tinto's 2020 destruction of a 46,000-year-old sacred site; NZ Parliament voted down the Treaty Principles Bill; Colombia recognised self-governance of nearly 200 Indigenous territories; Namibia held its first official Genocide Remembrance Day for the Herero and Nama (1904–1908 colonial campaign). [Intercontinental Cry](https://icmagazine.org/20-major-wins-for-indigenous-rights-in-2025/ "IC Magazine, '20 Major Wins for Indigenous Rights in 2025,' Jun 2025")
- **Decolonising the curriculum**: A 2025 Open University PhD documents institutional decolonisation processes in UK higher education. A 2026 *Higher Education* article examines South African universities where curricula remain "dominated by 'western' knowledge systems." A 2025 Taylor & Francis study of 34 UK university teachers found they are "key actors in efforts to decolonise higher education curricula" but face tensions between institutional mandates and practical challenges. [Open University PhD](https://oro.open.ac.uk/107483/1/Shannon%20Martin%202025%20PhD%20Decolonising%20the%20University%20A%20Case%20Study%20of%20The%20Open%20University.pdf "Martin 2025") [Springer](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1057/s41307-026-00452-4 "Higher Education, 2026") [Taylor & Francis](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/14703297.2025.2558222 "Innovations in Education and Teaching International, 2025")
- **CRT as institutionalisation case study**: Shaw (2025) demonstrates that "it was the states' misappropriation of CRT for political purposes that elevated the theory to doctrinal, popular, and pedagogical prominence." Since 2021, lawmakers in every U.S. state but Delaware introduced "anti-CRT" bills; 12 states adopted legislation banning "divisive concepts" between April 2021 and March 2023. These laws constitute "pedagogical censorship" targeting *how* race is taught rather than *what* is taught. Ironically, some provisions "though written with the aim of protecting white students, might actually promote a CRT analysis." [Shaw, *Washington and Lee Law Review*](https://scholarship.law.vanderbilt.edu/cgi/viewcontent.cgi?article=3015&context=faculty-publications "Shaw, 82 Wash. & Lee L. Rev. 1301, Fall 2025")
- **Subaltern Studies institutionalisation tensions**: The project moved from "a specific and focused intervention in the academic discipline of (Indian) history" to "a general designation for a field of studies often seen as a close relative of postcolonialism." Indian Marxist critics charged that "valorization of the fragment" hurt class-based solidarity; defenders responded that the public sphere "has fragmented under the pressure of democracy anyway." [Chakrabarty](https://files.libcom.org/files/subaltern.pdf "Chakrabarty 2000, pp. 9, 26–27")
- **African narrative reclamation**: Depelchin's *Silences in African History* (2005) argues that what appeared as a shift "from denial to recognition" was actually "a redefinition or reformulation of denial." Ngũgĩ wa Thiong'o's framework of colonial "dismemberment" and decolonial "re-membering" identifies epistemological, physical, and linguistic forms of colonial domination — "the most important area of domination was the mental universe of the colonized." [Ndlovu-Gatsheni](https://library.oapen.org/bitstream/handle/20.500.12657/41376/9781138588578_oachapter1.pdf "Epistemic Freedom in Africa, 2018, pp. 10–20")

## Chapter 4：Commemorative Practices and the Making of Historiography — Monuments, Museums, and Rituals
### 研究目标
- Analyse how material and performative commemorative practices (monuments, memorials, museums, national holidays, commemorative legislation, public rituals) shape both public memory and academic historiography.
- Explore the feedback loop between what is commemorated and what is researched: how monument controversies redirect scholarly attention, funding, and methodological priorities.

- Core questions: How do commemorative practices function as sites where public memory and professional historiography intersect? What do recent monument controversies reveal? How is digital commemoration reshaping memory and historiographic practice?

### 关键发现
- Kirk Savage's *Standing Soldiers, Kneeling Slaves: Race, War, and Monument in Nineteenth-Century America* (Princeton UP, 1997; new edition 2018) demonstrates how post-Civil War monument building functioned as "a process of national and racial definition," with sculptural monuments distorting the history of slavery and effectively perpetuating white supremacy through commemorative form. He also authored *Monument Wars* (Princeton UP) on how political power configures Washington D.C.'s memorial landscape. [Princeton University Press](https://press.princeton.edu/books/paperback/9780691183152/standing-soldiers-kneeling-slaves "Publisher page, 2018 edition")
- James Young's concept of the "counter-monument" (*Gegendenkmal*), introduced in *Critical Inquiry* 18 (Winter 1992) and *The Texture of Memory* (Yale UP, 1993), challenges traditional monumental forms. Key examples: Gerz/Shalev-Gerz's *Monument Against Fascism* (Hamburg-Harburg, 1986) — a column designed to sink into the ground and disappear; Hoheisel's "negative-form" Aschrott Fountain (Kassel, 1987) — an inverted monument sunk 12 metres underground; Ullman's empty underground library at Bebelplatz (Berlin, 1995); Whiteread's Vienna Holocaust memorial (2000). Young argues "the surest engagement with Holocaust memory in Germany may actually lie in its perpetual irresolution, that only an unfinished memorial process can guarantee the life of memory." [Harvard Design Magazine](https://www.harvarddesignmagazine.org/articles/memory-and-counter-memory/ "Young, 'Memory and Counter-Memory'")
- Erika Doss's *Memorial Mania: Public Feeling in America* (University of Chicago Press, 2010) argues the proliferation of memorials reflects "an obsession with issues of memory and history, and the urgent desire to express — and claim — particular emotional and cultural stakes in public commemoration." Memorials underscore anxieties about identity, belonging, grief, gratitude, and fear, functioning as sites of political contestation over who is worth remembering. [Goodreads / scholarly reviews](https://www.goodreads.com/en/book/show/9297910-memorial-mania "Book summary") [ResearchGate](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/265935264_Memorial_Mania_Public_Feeling_in_America_by_Erika_Doss_review "Scholarly review")
- Sanford Levinson's *Written in Stone: Public Monuments in Changing Societies* (Duke UP, 1998; 20th anniversary ed. 2018) examines how "those with political power configure public spaces in ways that shape public memory and politics." The 2018 edition addresses Confederate monument removals in New Orleans (2017) and the Charlottesville white-nationalist march. [Duke University Press](https://www.dukeupress.edu/written-in-stone-twentieth-anniversary-edition "Publisher page")
- Paul Williams's *Memorial Museums: The Global Rush to Commemorate Atrocities* (Berg/Bloomsbury, 2007) identifies memorial museums as "a novel hybrid" institution blurring commemoration and historiographic practice, covering institutions dedicated to the Holocaust, Cambodia, Rwanda, apartheid, political disappearances, and state repression. [Bloomsbury](https://www.bloomsbury.com/us/memorial-museums-9781845204891/ "Publisher page")

[NPR](https://www.npr.org/2021/02/23/970610428/nearly-100-confederate-monuments-removed-in-2020-report-says-more-than-700-remai "NPR on SPLC 2020 data") [The Hill](https://thehill.com/homenews/race-politics/5267685-confederate-memorials-report/ "Hill on SPLC 4th ed., April 2025") [SPLC](https://www.splcenter.org/resources/reports/whose-heritage/ "SPLC Whose Heritage? main page")

In February 2025, Oriel announced a sculpture competition with the Oxford Zimbabwe Arts Partnership at Chitungwiza Arts Centre, Harare, for an exhibition contextualising "the contested history of colonialism in Zimbabwe and legacy of Cecil Rhodes" (September–December 2025) — shifting from binary "remove or preserve" toward counter-contextualisation through additional art. [The Art Newspaper](https://www.theartnewspaper.com/2025/02/07/oxford-university-exhibition-aims-to-contextualise-the-legacy-of-british-imperialist-cecil-rhodes "Art Newspaper, Feb 2025")
- **Edward Colston / Bristol**: BLM protesters toppled the slave trader's statue on June 7, 2020; the "Colston Four" were acquitted of criminal damage. Since March 2024, the statue is on permanent display at M Shed museum, lying horizontally with graffiti preserved, in a gallery dedicated to the history of protest. Over 80% of survey respondents preferred horizontal display with graffiti; 8 of 10 wanted it in a museum. The case exemplifies the trajectory from contested monument to museum object — where curatorial decisions carry explicit historiographic weight. [History Workshop](https://www.historyworkshop.org.uk/museums-archives-heritage/colstons-toppling-five-years-on/ "History Workshop, June 2025")

[BBC](https://www.bbc.com/news/world-europe-53017188 "BBC, June 2020")
- **Feedback loop — commemoration ↔ historiography**: Frick (2024) identifies three philosophical positions on monument controversies: removalism, preservationalism, and interventionalism. Most Confederate monuments were erected after 1890 as "provocative assertions of white supremacy at moments when its defenders felt under threat" (citing Neiman) — a historiographic finding that reshapes political and legal arguments about monument retention. Huyssen observes "an inverse proportion between the memorialization of the past and its contemplation and study." Nora warns "the less memory is experienced from the inside, the more it exists through its exterior scaffolding and outward signs." [Taylor & Francis](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/21502552.2024.2372758 "Frick 2024, Public Art Dialogue") [Harvard Design Magazine](https://www.harvarddesignmagazine.org/articles/memory-and-counter-memory/ "Huyssen and Nora cited in Young")
- **Digital commemoration**: Ebbrecht-Hartmann (2021) identifies three modes of digital commemorative translation: (1) *transferring* existing forms (e.g., Zoom survivor testimony via *Zikaron BaSalon*, ~1 million participants during *Yom HaShoah* 2020); (2) *transitioning* to social media logics (e.g., Neuengamme Memorial's Instagram #digitalmemorials clips); (3) *transforming* into native digital forms (e.g., Eva.Stories Instagram project, 1 million+ followers, translating a Holocaust diary into Instagram Stories). Kansteiner observes that "digital memory no longer evolves along the individual-collective axis" and features "active individuals constantly posting, editing, liking and linking in pursuit of fluid 'we's.'" Hoskins's "memory of the multitude" concept describes decentralised, networked memory formations. The Körber-Stiftung's eCommemoration programme (active through 2025) convenes historians and digital practitioners, including the 2023 convention on "Artificial Memories: Decolonizing our Minds and Data." [SAGE Journals](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/0163443720983276 "Ebbrecht-Hartmann 2021, Media, Culture & Society") [Körber-Stiftung](https://koerber-stiftung.de/en/projects/ecommemoration/ "eCommemoration programme page")

## Chapter 5：Historical Memory as Political Resource — Mobilisation, Legitimation, and Conflict
### 研究目标
- Serve as the synthesising and forward-looking chapter: examine how historical memory functions as an active political resource for electoral mobilisation, regime legitimation, territorial claims, transitional justice, and international diplomacy.
- Analyse "memory wars" as a contemporary geopolitical phenomenon: Russia–Ukraine contest over WWII memory, East Asian history-textbook disputes, Middle Eastern competing origin narratives, domestic culture-war deployments in Western democracies.
- Assess institutional responses: truth commissions, reparations frameworks, international memory-governance norms, scholarly calls for "memory ethics."
- Core questions: How is historical memory strategically mobilised for political objectives? What role do "memory wars" play in geopolitical conflicts? What institutional mechanisms have been proposed/implemented, and how effective are they?

### 关键发现
- **Theoretical frameworks**: Alon Confino's *Foundational Pasts: The Holocaust as Historical Understanding* (CUP, 2012) argues certain events function as "foundational pasts" — so deeply embedded that they structure how a society understands all subsequent history. Duncan Bell's *Memory, Trauma and World Politics* (Palgrave, 2006) is the foundational text bridging memory studies and international relations, arguing that memory of traumatic episodes shapes political identities, post-conflict reconstruction, and the legitimacy of political violence. [Cambridge University Press](https://assets.cambridge.org/97805215/16655/frontmatter/9780521516655_frontmatter.pdf "Confino, Foundational Pasts frontmatter") [SpringerLink](https://link.springer.com/chapter/10.1057/9780230627482_1 "Bell, Memory, Trauma and World Politics, 2006")
- **Aleida Assmann's memory ethics**: Assmann distinguishes "memory from above" (state-directed, potentially instrumentalising) from "memory from below" (grassroots, potentially emancipatory), advocating "dialogical" memory culture incorporating self-critical acknowledgement of perpetration alongside recognition of victimhood. Her framework distinguishes the "right to remember" (victims' communities), the "duty to remember" (perpetrator societies), and broader democratic obligations to sustain plural, self-critical memorial culture. Her key works include *Shadows of Trauma* (Fordham UP, 2016) and *Is Time Out of Joint?* (Cornell UP, 2020). [OUP](https://global.oup.com/academic/product/cognition-culture-and-political-momentum-9780197788332 "Assmann chapter in Erll & Hirst 2026")
- **Russia–Ukraine memory war**: Russia elevated WWII memory to civic religion (2020 constitutional amendment protecting the Soviet war narrative; "denazification" justification for 2022 invasion). Ukraine's 2015 decommunisation laws renamed the "Great Patriotic War" as "Second World War" and elevated the OUN-UPA. Polling shifts: positive perception of Bandera rose from 36% (2018) to 74% (December 2022); 92% viewed the Holodomor as genocide in 2023 (up from 58% in 2011); positive attitudes toward Soviet Victory Day fell from 80% (2018) to 34% (2022). In occupied territories, Russia exports its memory regime — purging Ukrainian curricula, imposing Russian textbooks, vandalising Holodomor memorials, replacing Shevchenko monuments with Pushkin busts. McGlynn (2025) describes this as "cognitive warfare" operating through "memory traps" (the Yunarmiia youth movement has ~1.85 million members). [New Lines Institute](https://newlinesinstitute.org/intl-law-peace/how-ukraines-history-impacts-its-war-with-russia/ "How Ukraine's History Impacts its War with Russia, July 2024") [Jade McGlynn](https://smalldeedsbigwar.substack.com/p/memory-traps "Memory Traps, November 2025")
- **East Asian textbook disputes**: On 24 March 2026, Japan's Ministry of Education approved textbooks asserting "inherent" sovereignty over Dokdo/Takeshima and describing South Korea as "illegally occupying" them. Since a 2021 government position paper, expressions like "taken away" and "forcibly taken away" for Korean labourers have been replaced by "mobilised." South Korea expressed "deep regret" and summoned Japan's deputy chief of mission. The 2023 forced labour compensation plan (Korean-funded foundation bypassing direct Japanese corporate payments) drew strong domestic criticism as allowing Japan to evade historical responsibility. [The Straits Times](https://www.straitstimes.com/asia/east-asia/south-korea-expresses-regret-over-japans-approval-of-history-textbooks-urges-rectification "South Korea on Japan textbook approval, 24 March 2026") [The Guardian](https://www.theguardian.com/world/2023/may/07/japanese-pm-arrives-in-south-korea-amid-warming-ties "May 2023")
- **Israeli-Palestinian memory competition**: Wermenbol's *A Tale of Two Narratives* (CUP, 2021) demonstrates how the Holocaust and the Nakba function as rival "foundational traumas" — "ethnocentric victimhood narratives that serve as the theoretical foundations for an ensuing minimization — or even denial — of the other's past." Rotberg's *Israeli and Palestinian Narratives of Conflict: History's Double Helix* (Indiana UP, 2006) analyses how "two powerful, bitterly contested, competing historical narratives" function as active political resources for legitimating sovereignty and mobilising diasporic support. [Cambridge University Press](https://www.cambridge.org/core/books/tale-of-two-narratives/1AD44AAD479A135B7E44553F0FC06657 "Wermenbol 2021") [Belfer Center](https://www.belfercenter.org/publication/israeli-and-palestinian-narratives-conflict-historys-double-helix "Rotberg 2006")
- **U.S. culture wars — 1619 Project vs. 1776 Commission**: The *New York Times*' "1619 Project" (August 2019, led by Nikole Hannah-Jones) reframed American history around slavery as central to the national narrative. Trump's "1776 Commission" (November 2020; report January 2021) promoted a patriotic counter-narrative positioning the founding as defined by liberty, not slavery. Biden dissolved the commission on inauguration day. The conflict became a proxy war over national identity, with conservative legislatures passing laws restricting discussion of systemic racism explicitly targeting the 1619 Project's framing. [Opera Historica](https://www.opera-historica.com/pdfs/oph/2021/01/05.pdf "The 1619 Project and Living in Truth, scholarly analysis")
- **Transitional justice — Teitel's genealogy**: Ruti Teitel's "Transitional Justice Genealogy" (*Harvard Human Rights Journal* 16, 2003) proposes three phases: Phase I (postwar/1945 — international criminal accountability, Nuremberg); Phase II (post-Cold War/1989 — local restorative mechanisms, truth commissions, South Africa's TRC); Phase III (steady-state/late 1990s — normalisation, ICC, permanent humanitarian law). "A genealogy of transitional justice demonstrates, over time, a close relationship between the type of justice pursued and the relevant limiting political conditions." [Harvard HRJ](https://journals.law.harvard.edu/hrj/wp-content/uploads/sites/83/2020/06/16HHRJ69-Teitel.pdf "Teitel, Transitional Justice Genealogy, 2003")
- **Pablo de Greiff**: First UN Special Rapporteur on truth, justice, reparation and non-recurrence (2012–2018). Argued the four "pillars" must be understood as interconnected elements of a coherent framework. Key publications: *Handbook of Reparations* (2006), *Justice as Prevention* (2007), *Transitional Justice and Development* (2009). [OHCHR](https://www.ohchr.org/en/special-procedures/sr-truth-justice-reparation-and-non-recurrence/pablo-de-greiff-former-special-rapporteur-2012-2018 "OHCHR biography")
- **UN slavery reparations resolution (March 2026)**: On 25 March 2026, the UNGA adopted resolution A/80/L.48 (123-3, with 52 abstentions including UK and all EU members), declaring racialized chattel enslavement "the gravest crime against humanity" and calling for reparatory justice including "a full and formal apology, measures of restitution, compensation, rehabilitation, satisfaction, guarantees of non-repetition." The U.S. objected to "ranking crimes against humanity." [AP News](https://apnews.com/article/un-vote-africa-slavery-trafficking-reparations-a7497cdb7d24a89eedb50beb683adc0f "UN calls for reparations, 25 March 2026")
- **CHOGM 2024 (Samoa)**: Commonwealth member states agreed "the time has come for a meaningful, truthful and respectful conversation" on reparatory justice. [BBC News](https://www.bbc.com/news/articles/cd6vy79p750o "Commonwealth heads defy UK on slavery reparations, October 2024")
- **CARICOM reparations (2025)**: The CARICOM Reparations Commission undertook its first UK visit in November 2025. The Second Africa-CARICOM International Summit on Reparations was held in Addis Ababa (September 2025), broadening the coalition to include the African Union. [CARICOM](https://caricom.org/caricom-leaders-strengthen-reparations-agenda/ "CARICOM 2025") [Harvard FXB](https://fxb.harvard.edu/blog/2025/11/21/bridging-the-atlantic-legacy-solidarity-and-reparations/ "November 2025")
- **California reparations (2024–2025)**: Governor Newsom signed 5 reparations-related laws (including a Bureau for Descendants of American Slavery) but vetoed 5 others. The mixed results exemplify the tension between symbolic recognition and substantive redistribution. [The Guardian](https://www.theguardian.com/us-news/2024/oct/28/california-reparations-black-residents "October 2024")
- **Rothberg's "implicated subject"**: *The Implicated Subject* (Stanford UP, 2019) introduces a figure "neither a victim nor a perpetrator, but rather a participant in histories and social formations that generate the positions of victim and perpetrator." Implicated subjects "contribute to, inhabit, inherit, or benefit from regimes of domination." Rothberg distinguishes genealogical implication (inherited responsibility) from structural implication (participation in ongoing systems) and proposes "long-distance solidarity" as the ethical horizon. He argues "collective memory that avoids such a sense of implication tends toward empty rhetoric and platitude." [Stanford UP](https://www.sup.org/books/literary-studies-and-literature/implicated-subject/excerpt/introduction "Rothberg, The Implicated Subject, Introduction, 2019")
- **Memory ethics vs. memory laws**: Memory ethics (Assmann, Rothberg) operates through normative obligations, education, and civic culture; memory laws (Koposov) operate through legal coercion. The tension between these approaches is central to contemporary memory governance: ethics advocates argue legal mandates risk instrumentalising the past, while legislation proponents counter that without legal frameworks, powerful actors dominate memory production unchallenged. Koposov demonstrates how memory laws oscillate between protecting victims and serving state ideology. [Cambridge UP](https://www.cambridge.org/core/books/memory-laws-memory-wars/FAEB22B10B3C7E630662E6660C780B19 "Koposov, Memory Laws, Memory Wars, 2018")

# Section 2：给 Write 阶段的执行建议

- **Analytical register**: The report should read as a scholarly analysis, not a survey or textbook summary. Each chapter should advance an argument, not merely catalogue examples.
- **Temporal layering**: While the primary analytical window is April 2025–April 2026, each chapter should layer recent developments atop foundational scholarship. Present-day cases should illustrate — not replace — theoretical frameworks.
- **Cross-chapter coherence**: A recurring thread should run through all chapters: the tension between history as disciplined inquiry and memory as politically constituted narrative. Chapter 1 establishes this tension theoretically; Chapters 2–5 examine its manifestations.
- **Balanced geography**: Avoid Anglo-American parochialism. Each chapter should draw on examples from at least two distinct world regions.
- **Avoid false equivalence**: Do not treat state-sponsored ideologisation (Chapter 2) and subaltern counter-narrative recovery (Chapter 3) as symmetrical phenomena. Acknowledge the asymmetry of power while maintaining analytical rigour about both.
- **Definitional discipline**: Key terms — "memory," "history," "ideology," "commemoration," "instrumentalisation" — should be defined explicitly in Chapter 1 and used consistently thereafter. Avoid casual interchangeability.
- **Evidence standards**: Core claims should be anchored in peer-reviewed scholarship or primary-source documentation. Journalistic sources may illustrate recent events but should not serve as the evidentiary backbone.
- **Avoid presentism as methodology**: While the report examines presentist uses of the past, the analysis itself should avoid projecting contemporary moral frameworks onto historical actors without reflexive acknowledgement.
- **Digital dimension**: The role of digital platforms in reshaping memory practices is a cross-cutting theme relevant to Chapters 2, 4, and 5 — integrate it rather than isolating it.
- **Concluding synthesis**: Chapter 5 should function as both a substantive chapter and a synthesising conclusion, drawing connections back to Chapter 1's theoretical frameworks and offering forward-looking analytical observations without lapsing into prescriptive policy recommendations.
- **Time frame**: The research covers April 2025–April 2026 as the primary analytical window, with foundational works from earlier periods cited as needed. This temporal scope should be stated explicitly in the report introduction.
