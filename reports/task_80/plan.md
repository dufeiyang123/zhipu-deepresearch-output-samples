# Section 1：章节研究计划

## Chapter 1：Gendered Language in Japanese — Theoretical Foundations

### 研究目标
- Define and historicize the system of gendered speech in Japanese (女言葉 onna-kotoba / 男言葉 otoko-kotoba, 役割語 yakuwarigo), distinguishing prescriptive norms from contemporary usage.
- Present the key sociolinguistic frameworks — indexicality (Ochs, Silverstein), language ideology (Irvine & Gal), and performativity (Butler) — that underpin the analysis.
- Introduce the theoretical link between media consumption and linguistic behavior (cultivation theory, social learning theory) as it applies to language features.

### 关键发现
- 发现 1: The prescriptive system of "women's language" (女言葉) is a modern construct, not an ancient tradition. Inoue demonstrates that the speech forms now identified as women's language were codified during the Meiji era (late 19th–early 20th century) as part of state formation; prior to this, the particle わ was considered vulgar, not feminine. [Inoue 2002](https://web.stanford.edu/~eckert/Courses/l1562018/Readings/inoue2002.pdf "Gender, Language, and Modernity, American Ethnologist 29(2), 392–422")
- 発見 2: Inoue's comparison of sentence-ending particles in pre-Meiji (*Ukiyoburo*, 1813) and Meiji fiction (*Sanshirō*, 1909) shows particles used by both genders in 1813 had been systematically divided into male-exclusive and female-exclusive forms by 1909, demonstrating that binary gendering of particle usage was an artifact of the gembun'itchi literary movement and state nation-building. [Inoue 2002](https://web.stanford.edu/~eckert/Courses/l1562018/Readings/inoue2002.pdf "Tables 1–2 comparing particles in Ukiyoburo and Sanshirō")
- 発見 3: Codification of women's language was tied to Meiji-era kokugo (national language) policy: Ueda Kazutoshi designated "educated Tokyo middle-class male speech" as standard in 1894; the 1899 Girls' High School Directive institutionalized speech regulation under "good wife, wise mother" (良妻賢母) ideology; the 1902 nationwide dialect survey found far less gender-based speech differentiation outside Tokyo than expected. [Inoue 2002](https://web.stanford.edu/~eckert/Courses/l1562018/Readings/inoue2002.pdf "pp. 396–409")
- 発見 4: Nakamura Momoko's *Gender, Language and Ideology: A Genealogy of Japanese Women's Language* (John Benjamins, 2014) provides the first comprehensive book-length historical analysis of how women's language was ideologically constructed through discourse across multiple periods. [Nakamura 2014](https://benjamins.com/catalog/dapsac.58 "ISBN 978-90-272-0649-7")
- 発見 5: Kinsui Satoshi's yakuwarigo (役割語) framework (first proposed 2000; book *ヴァーチャル日本語：役割語の謎*, Iwanami Shoten, 2003) explains why gendered speech stereotypes persist in fictional media even as real-world usage diverges — yakuwarigo are speech styles recognized by audiences as stereotypical character markers, relying heavily on variation in first-person pronouns and sentence-ending particles. [Teshigawara & Kinsui 2011](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/274286972_Modern_Japanese_Role_Language_Yakuwarigo_fictionalised_orality_in_Japanese_literature_and_popular_culture "Sociolinguistic Studies 5(1): 37–58")
- 発見 6: Ochs's (1992) two-tier indexicality model argues that few linguistic features directly index gender; instead, forms directly index stances (e.g., softness), which indirectly index gender through cultural association. However, Inoue critiques this model for naturalizing a historically contingent indexical order — the link between わ and softness/femininity did not exist until the late 19th century. [Inoue 2002](https://web.stanford.edu/~eckert/Courses/l1562018/Readings/inoue2002.pdf "Critique of Ochs's indexicality model, pp. 394–395")
- 発見 7: Silverstein's (2003) "orders of indexicality" (*Language and Communication* 23(3–4): 193–229) extends Ochs's model into multi-layered indexical relationships subject to ideological regimentation, critical for understanding how gendered speech forms simultaneously index stance, gender, class, and urbanity.
- 発見 8: Irvine & Gal's (2000) framework identifies three semiotic processes — iconization, fractal recursivity, and erasure — explaining how linguistic differentiation is ideologically constructed and how prescriptive norms erase actual diversity in speech. [Irvine & Gal 2000](https://web.stanford.edu/~eckert/PDF/IrvineGal2000.pdf "Language Ideology and Linguistic Differentiation, in Regimes of Language, pp. 35–84")
- 発見 9: Butler's performativity theory (1988, 1990) provides the philosophical foundation: gendered speech is a constitutive practice rather than expression of a pre-existing identity — "an identity tenuously constituted in time through a stylized repetition of acts." Inoue and Nakamura have adapted this for Japanese. [Butler 1988](https://www.amherst.edu/system/files/media/1650/butler_performative_acts.pdf "Performative Acts and Gender Constitution, Theatre Journal 40(4): 519–531")
- 発見 10: Ide Sachiko's foundational work (1982) characterized women's speech as "more polite than men's" and identified specific morphological features; later critiqued by Inoue and Nakamura for treating prescriptive norms as descriptive realities. [Ide 1982](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/0024384182900092 "Linguistics 20(5–6), 357–377")
- 発見 11: Gerbner's cultivation theory posits that prolonged, cumulative media exposure shapes consumers' perceptions of social reality. Applied to language, heavy consumers of yakuwarigo-laden media may internalize fictional gendered particle usage as normative. No study has yet directly applied this to linguistic feature adoption — the chapter must frame this as a theoretical extension.
- 発見 12: Bandura's social cognitive theory (1986) — particularly symbolic modeling and vicarious reinforcement — provides a complementary mechanism: manga readers who observe characters narratively rewarded for certain speech forms may internalize those forms. [Bandura 1986](https://psycnet.apa.org/record/1985-98423-000 "Social Foundations of Thought and Action, Prentice Hall")
- 発見 13: Stuart-Smith et al. (2013) found that phonological changes among Glasgow teenagers correlated with psychological engagement with the soap opera *EastEnders* — not passive exposure or positive attitudes — suggesting media influence on language operates through parasocial identification, directly relevant to manga character identification. Stuart-Smith, J. et al. (2013). *Language* 89(3): 501–536.
- 発見 14: Inoue's *Vicarious Language* (UC Press, 2006) develops the concept of "vicarious language" — language experienced primarily through mediated consumption rather than production — directly relevant to manga as a site of gendered language socialization. [Inoue 2006](https://sts.stanford.edu/publications/vicarious-language-gender-and-linguistic-modernity-japan "University of California Press, ISBN 978-0-520-24585-7")

## Chapter 2：First-Person Pronouns and Sentence-Ending Particles — A Linguistic Inventory

### 研究目标
- Catalog the primary first-person pronouns in modern Japanese, characterizing each form's gendered indexicality, formality, and pragmatic connotations.
- Catalog the primary sentence-ending particles, distinguishing strongly gendered from weakly gendered or gender-neutral forms, and noting diachronic shifts (e.g., decline of certain feminine SEPs).
- Synthesize survey and corpus-based evidence on how pronoun and SEP usage varies by speaker gender, age cohort, and social setting in mainstream Japanese.

### 关键发现
- 発見 1: Japanese has an exceptionally large open-class inventory of first-person pronouns, each encoding gender, formality, age, and social context; speakers routinely switch pronouns across contexts within a single day. [Tofugu — Japanese First-Person Pronouns](https://www.tofugu.com/japanese/japanese-first-person-pronouns/ "Detailed pragmatic guide, March 2019")
- 発見 2: Saegusa (2009) survey of university students shows pronoun use varies dramatically by context: among female students with friends, うち uchi was most popular (39%), followed by あたし atashi (30%) and わたし watashi (22%); in classrooms, watashi dominated (89%). Among male students with friends, 俺 ore dominated (87%); in classrooms, watashi (48%), 自分 jibun (28%), 僕 boku (22%). [Saegusa 2009, cited in Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pronouns_in_Japanese "Tables based on Saegusa, Yuko (2009). The Japanese Language and Literature Society of Korea 44: 97–109")
- 発見 3: 僕 boku occupies a unique intermediate position on the gender indexicality continuum. Miyazaki (2004) found Tokyo junior high school girls perceived a gender continuum — ore (most masculine), atashi (most feminine), with boku and uchi positioned as relatively neutral. Ito (2006) documented girls as young as 6–7 facing peer pressure for using boku. [Ito 2006](https://www.inst.at/trans/16Nr/01_4/ito16.htm "BOKU or WATASHI: Variation in self-reference terms among Japanese children, TRANS 16"); Miyazaki (2004) in Okamoto & Shibamoto Smith (eds.), *Japanese Language, Gender, and Ideology*, OUP, pp. 256–274.
- 発見 4: Okamoto & Shibamoto Smith (2022), analyzing eight dyadic conversations, found SFF use was "hardly gendered" — both female speakers used masculine SFFs 87–88% of the time, higher than the two male speakers (both 67%). None of eight speakers used strongly feminine わ wa, かしら kashira, or strongly masculine ぞ zo, ぜ ze. [Okamoto & Shibamoto Smith 2022](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/josl.12569 "Journal of Sociolinguistics, BTSJ corpus data from 2007")
- 発見 5: Despite near-absence of gendered SFF differentiation, other features remained strongly gendered in the same data: pronouns were gendered without exception (women used atashi/watashi exclusively; men used ore exclusively), backchannel frequency was much higher among women, and women spoke at higher pitch with wider range. [Okamoto & Shibamoto Smith 2022](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/josl.12569 "Tables 4–8")
- 発見 6: Okamoto (1995) provided foundational evidence for the "decline of feminine speech" thesis: young women in their 20s used masculine and neutral sentence-final forms substantially more often than women in their 30s and 50s. Okamoto (1995), "'Tasteless' Japanese," in Hall & Bucholtz (eds.), *Gender Articulated*, Routledge, pp. 297–325.
- 発見 7: Carpi & Iacus (2020) analyzed 408 million Japanese tweets (2015–2019), finding gendered language features appear in ~6% of tweets with "remarkable exceptions" to prescriptive gender classifications. [Carpi & Iacus 2020](https://www.ojcmt.net/article/is-japanese-gendered-language-used-on-twitter-a-large-scale-study-9141 "Online Journal of Communication and Media Technologies 10(4), e202024")
- 発見 8: SturtzSreetharan (2004) showed men's use of "manly" sentence-final particles varies significantly by life stage (students, sarariiman, seniors) and context, not conforming to a monolithic masculine norm. [SturtzSreetharan 2004](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/language-in-society/article/students-sarariiman-pl-and-seniors-japanese-mens-use-of-manly-speech-register/FA0EDF56766A578A350CABD68F829C13 "Language in Society 33(1): 81–107")
- 発見 9: Noguchi (2022) reports female university students are "slowly changing the gendered nature of Japanese," adopting masculine features (おまえ, きみ, うまい, くそ) and using "very few of the traditional elements of onna kotoba" in casual settings, though shifts are "not yet evident in most public contexts." [Noguchi 2022](https://hal.science/hal-05514023/ "The Journal of Asian Linguistic Anthropology 4(2): 1–19")
- 発見 10: The particle わ wa has undergone a functional split: falling intonation = relatively gender-neutral (common in Kansai male speech); rising intonation = strongly feminine but "no longer common in ordinary interactions," now primarily used to emphasize femininity in queer speech or fictional characters. [Tofugu 2024](https://www.tofugu.com/japanese-grammar/sentence-ending-particle-wa/ "Comprehensive grammar page on わ")
- 発見 11: Abe (2010), *Queer Japanese* (Palgrave Macmillan), documented that gender-neutral pronoun 自分 jibun was popular among women who self-identified as onabe at lesbian bars in Shinjuku. [Abe 2010](https://link.springer.com/content/pdf/10.1057/9780230106161.pdf "Queer Japanese: Gender and Sexual Identities through Linguistic Practices")
- 発見 12: Women's use of SFFs shifted toward more feminine variants in mixed-gender conversations (58–67% masculine SFFs with men vs. 87–88% in same-gender talk), and pitch increased +8–11% with wider pitch range (+22–26%), suggesting gendered linguistic performance is activated more strongly in heterosocial contexts. [Okamoto & Shibamoto Smith 2022](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/josl.12569 "Section 4.4")

### 研究目标
- Characterize the linguistic conventions of shoujo manga dialogue regarding pronoun selection and SEP usage, drawing on manga-linguistics scholarship.
- Compare shoujo manga's gendered-language patterns with those in shōnen, seinen, and josei manga and with naturalistic spoken Japanese.
- Discuss how BL and yuri manga handle gendered pronouns and SEPs, particularly for characters whose gender or sexuality diverges from heteronormative norms.
- Examine the narrative and semiotic functions of pronoun/SEP choice within shoujo manga storytelling.

### 关键发现
- 発見 1: Unser-Schutz's (2015) corpus of 10 manga series (~260,000 words) found female characters in shōjo manga used atashi (78%) and watashi (22%) almost exclusively; zero examples of uchi or jibun despite real-world popularity. Manga shows "a lag in adopting" real-world pronoun innovation. [Unser-Schutz 2015](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/265729754_Influential_or_influenced_The_relationship_between_genre_gender_and_language_in_manga "Gender and Language 9(2), 2015")
- 発見 2: Strongly feminine SFPs account for only 4.1% of all SFPs by female characters in shōjo manga — remarkably close to the 4% in Okamoto (1995)'s real-world data for women 18–23. By contrast, shōnen manga hyper-feminizes female speech at 18.4% strongly feminine SFPs. [Unser-Schutz 2015](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/265729754_Influential_or_influenced_The_relationship_between_genre_gender_and_language_in_manga "Tables 9–10")
- 発見 3: Shōjo manga dialogue is closer to naturalistic speech than shōnen manga regarding gendered SFPs, but conservative regarding pronouns. Historical data (Aizawa 2003, Chinami 2001) confirm feminine SFP usage has been declining in shōjo manga over time, mirroring real-life trends. [Unser-Schutz 2015](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/265729754_Influential_or_influenced_The_relationship_between_genre_gender_and_language_in_manga "Discussion section")
- 発見 4: Male characters in shōjo manga use ore at 88.8% of male 1PPs (similar to real-life 87% among male university students), but use moderately feminine SFPs at 2.7% — significantly higher than shōnen manga (1.1%, p=0.0017), suggesting greater linguistic realism or the romance genre's demand for emotionally expressive male characters. [Unser-Schutz 2015](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/265729754_Influential_or_influenced_The_relationship_between_genre_gender_and_language_in_manga "Tables 7 and 10")
- 発見 5: Kloutvorová (2021), analyzing 10 shōjo manga titles surveyed among 701 junior high school girls (657 tokens), found 9 of 10 protagonists used exclusively watashi; only the *Hana yori Dango* heroine used atashi throughout. No protagonist switched pronouns based on context — a notable divergence from real-life pragmatics. [Kloutvorová 2021](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/353607241_First_Person_Expressions_Used_by_Teenage_Girl_Characters_in_Shojo_Manga "Silva Iaponicarum 64–65, 2021")
- 発見 6: Shibamoto Smith (2004) found romance heroines in "happy ending" stories use watashi more than tragic heroines (who use atashi/uchi/name), suggesting watashi indexes a desirable, mature femininity — shoujo manga constructs femininity through linguistic restraint rather than exaggeration. [Kloutvorová 2021](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/353607241_First_Person_Expressions_Used_by_Teenage_Girl_Characters_in_Shojo_Manga "Citing Shibamoto-Smith 2004:123")
- 発見 7: Kinsui's yakuwarigo framework predicts protagonists use least-marked speech while secondary characters use more stereotypical role language. Corpus data confirm this: protagonists use standard watashi; four yakuwarigo types identified for young female characters (ojō-sama, schoolgirl, gal, delinquent-girl language), but actual deployment is "very limited" in corpus. [Kloutvorová 2021](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/353607241_First_Person_Expressions_Used_by_Teenage_Girl_Characters_in_Shojo_Manga "Section on Yakuwarigo"); Kinsui & Yamakido (2015), *Acta Linguistica Asiatica* 5(2): 29–42.
- 発見 8: Takahashi (2009) analyzed *Life* (shōjo manga), finding the villainess deploys strongly feminine onna-kotoba (wa, no-yo, kashira) to construct social power, while the protagonist uses neutral speech indexing sincerity — reversing the assumption that "more feminine = more positively portrayed." Takahashi (2009), *Gender and Sexuality* 4: 17–38.
- 発見 9: The Japanese Agency for Cultural Affairs' 2007 survey found 45% of respondents considered manga influential on young people's speech (ranking after TV 85.8%, mothers 73.9%, fathers 69.3%, ahead of friends 63.8%), but Unser-Schutz argues manga are "given as a post-facto reason for linguistic change" while being actually conservative in dialogue norms. [Unser-Schutz 2015](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/265729754_Influential_or_influenced_The_relationship_between_genre_gender_and_language_in_manga "Discussion section")
- 発見 10: Shōjo manga has more balanced gender representation in dialogue (female 56.0%, male 43.1%) vs. shōnen manga (male 83.4%, female 15.7%). Girls read across genres (shōnen + shōjo), exposing them to both linguistic environments. [Unser-Schutz 2015](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/265729754_Influential_or_influenced_The_relationship_between_genre_gender_and_language_in_manga "Table 4"); Allen & Ingulsrud (2005), *Language and Education* 19(4): 265–280.
- 発見 11: In BL manga, seme/uke differentiation operates primarily through visual codes (persona, emotion, action) rather than explicit linguistic feminization; 60% of BL opening illustrations distinguish seme/uke by facial features. [Sihombing 2011](https://www.imageandnarrative.be/index.php/imagenarrative/article/download/130/101/461 "Image & Narrative 12(1): 150–165")
- 発見 12: Onee-kotoba (オネエ言葉) combines hyper-feminine markers (atashi, wa, kashira) with crude masculine vocabulary, creating a parodic hybrid — a third category subverting the binary. Now visible in mainstream media but less systematically studied in manga. [Tofugu — Queer Japanese](https://www.tofugu.com/japanese/queer-japanese/ "Beyond the Binary, Lombardo 2020")
- 発見 13: ~77% of female high school students read shōjo/josei manga (LINE Research, May 2020, n=709). ~40% of *Weekly Shōnen Jump* readers are female (Shueisha), confirming cross-genre readership. [Statista/LINE Research 2020](https://www.statista.com/statistics/1214343/japan-reader-share-female-targeted-manga-high-school-students-by-gender/ "LINE Research survey")
- 発見 14: Pronoun switching in manga functions as narrative device for maturation (Chihaya in *Chihayafuru*: atashi→watashi) and transgressive identity (Nana: masculine temee for others + feminine atashi for self, constructing a punk persona). In manga, the relative fixity of each character's pronoun makes any switch narratively salient. [Kloutvorová 2021](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/353607241_First_Person_Expressions_Used_by_Teenage_Girl_Characters_in_Shojo_Manga "Section on Chihaya"); [Unser-Schutz 2015](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/265729754_Influential_or_influenced_The_relationship_between_genre_gender_and_language_in_manga "Nana discussion")

### 研究目标
- Map current terminology and self-identification categories used by queer young adults in Japan (Xジェンダー, ノンバイナリー, クィア, etc.), noting culturally specific vs. globally circulating concepts.
- Review sociolinguistic literature on how queer Japanese speakers select first-person pronouns — motivations including identity expression, passing, comfort, political stance, and context-dependent code-switching.
- Examine documented patterns of SEP usage among queer speakers, including avoidance of strongly gendered particles, mixing masculine/feminine forms, and creation of binary-resistant idiolects.

### 关键发现
- 発見 1: X-gender (Xジェンダー) is a Japan-originated non-binary identity that emerged in the late 1990s in Kansai queer organizations, with four recognized subgroups (ryōsei 両性, chūsei 中性, musei 無性, futei-sei 不定性), each implying different relationships to the masculine–feminine linguistic binary. In a 2019 survey by the Japan LGBT Research Institute (n=348,000), 2.5% identified as X-gender. [Dale 2012](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/X-gender "Dale, S.P.F. (2012), 'An Introduction to X-Jendā,' Intersections 31")
- 発見 2: Unlike Western LGBTQ+ taxonomies separating orientation from gender identity, Japanese indigenous terms okama and onabe historically conflated these categories. Abe classifies onabe as "transgender and/or transsexual rather than lesbian." [Abe 2010](https://transreads.org/wp-content/uploads/2021/12/2021-12-30_61cdc9d64808c_QueerJapaneseGenderandSexualIdentitiesthroughLinguisticPracticesbyHidekoAbez-lib.org_.pdf "Queer Japanese, pp. 10–13")
- 発見 3: At lesbian bars in Shinjuku Ni-chōme, onabe speakers preferred the gender-neutral pronoun 自分 jibun because feminine pronouns (watashi, atashi) "do not match their identity at all," while masculine pronouns (ore, boku) also felt inappropriate; jibun was perceived as "adequately rough" yet gender-neutral. [Abe 2010](https://transreads.org/wp-content/uploads/2021/12/2021-12-30_61cdc9d64808c_QueerJapaneseGenderandSexualIdentitiesthroughLinguisticPracticesbyHidekoAbez-lib.org_.pdf "Queer Japanese, Ch. 2")
- 発見 4: Queer women who typically used watashi or boku would switch to ore in heated situations, demonstrating context-dependent code-switching where both identity and interactional stance drive pronoun selection. [Abe 2010](https://transreads.org/wp-content/uploads/2021/12/2021-12-30_61cdc9d64808c_QueerJapaneseGenderandSexualIdentitiesthroughLinguisticPracticesbyHidekoAbez-lib.org_.pdf "Ch. 2: Lesbian Bar Talk")
- 発見 5: Nakamura (2026) argues the study of binary pronouns must expand to address Japanese first-person pronouns, where a binary norm prevents not only trans/nonbinary speakers but also girls and women from freely choosing pronouns. She cites Endō Mameta's 2018 memoir *Ore wa zettai ni watashi jyanai* ("I am definitely not watashi") as a transgender counterattack against this norm. [Nakamura 2026](https://brill.com/view/journals/jocp/7/1/article-p62_4.xml "Contrastive Pragmatics 7(1): 62–84")
- 発見 6: Onee-kotoba combines hyper-feminine markers (atashi, wa, no yo, kashira) with crude masculine vocabulary, creating a parodic hybrid. Abe identifies five claims about it: (1) product of bar culture; (2) negatively evaluated among some gay men; (3) imitation of women's language; (4) always involves dokuzetsu (毒舌, sharp wit); (5) parody of straight women's language. [Abe 2010](https://transreads.org/wp-content/uploads/2021/12/2021-12-30_61cdc9d64808c_QueerJapaneseGenderandSexualIdentitiesthroughLinguisticPracticesbyHidekoAbez-lib.org_.pdf "Ch. 5: Queen's Speech")
- 発見 7: Maree (2020) demonstrates how the "queerqueen" figure is commodified in Japanese media, proposing "language labor" as central to understanding how onee-kotoba is entextualized and mediatized, with media transcription practices encoding queer speech as "inherently excessive and in need of containment." [Maree 2020](https://global.oup.com/academic/product/queerqueen-9780190869618 "queerqueen: Linguistic Excess in Japanese Media, OUP")
- 発見 8: Among lesbians at Shinjuku bars, Abe observed a mixed idiolect combining neutral, feminine, and masculine forms, whereas gay men's linguistic practices tended toward either masculine norms or the distinct onee-kotoba register — suggesting lesbian speakers create more non-binary idiolects. [Abe 2010](https://transreads.org/wp-content/uploads/2021/12/2021-12-30_61cdc9d64808c_QueerJapaneseGenderandSexualIdentitiesthroughLinguisticPracticesbyHidekoAbez-lib.org_.pdf "Ch. 1: Queer Etiquette")
- 発見 9: Abe (2021) examines six transgender speakers' strategies for negotiating gendered speech, covering voice quality, communication strategies, and grammatical indexicality — representing the first micro-level linguistic analysis of Japanese trans women's speech. [Abe 2021](https://www.degruyterbrill.com/document/doi/10.21832/9781800411562-018/html "In Language Standardisation and Language Variation, Multilingual Matters, pp. 298–314")
- 発見 10: The metalinguistic verb ホゲる (hogeru) — meaning to let one's queer identity be recognized through actions such as speaking onee-kotoba — indicates community-level awareness that linguistic style is a conscious, deployable identity marker. [Tofugu — Queer Japanese](https://www.tofugu.com/japanese/queer-japanese/ "Lombardo 2020, hogeru section")
- 発見 11: Some queer Japanese speakers use onee-kotoba exclusively in texting/LINE rather than in-person speech, suggesting digital contexts provide lower-stakes environments for linguistic experimentation with gendered forms. [Tofugu — Queer Japanese](https://www.tofugu.com/japanese/queer-japanese/ "Lombardo 2020, texting onee-kotoba section")

## Chapter 5：Media Influence on Linguistic Behavior — Mechanisms and Evidence

### 研究目标
- Synthesize media-effects frameworks (cultivation theory, social cognitive theory, uses-and-gratifications) as they apply to linguistic features specifically.
- Review empirical studies — in Japanese and cross-linguistically — investigating media influence on language use (TV dialect diffusion, anime/manga loanword adoption, character-speech imitation).
- Analyze the specific affordances and constraints of manga as a medium for linguistic influence (written dialogue without audio, visual gender cues, parasocial identification).
- Identify the key methodological challenges (self-selection, directionality, confounds) for studies linking manga consumption to linguistic output.

### 关键发现
- 発見 1: No published study directly applies Gerbner's cultivation theory to linguistic feature adoption. The theory's core mechanism — that prolonged, cumulative media exposure shapes consumers' perceptions of social reality — constitutes a theoretical extension when applied to gendered speech norms. Bandura (2001) provides complementary grounding, noting that "verification of personal conceptions against televised versions of social reality can foster some collective illusions." [Bandura 2001](https://techofcomm.wordpress.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/09/bandura-sct-of-mass-communication.pdf "Social Cognitive Theory of Mass Communication, in Media Effects, 2nd ed., pp. 121–153")
- 発見 2: Bandura's social cognitive theory provides the most developed mechanism for media-to-language influence through symbolic modeling. Bandura explicitly discusses language: "Through abstract modeling, people acquire, among other things, standards for categorizing and judging events, linguistic rules of communication, thinking skills..." — directly authorizing the hypothesis that manga readers extract gendered linguistic rules from repeated character dialogue exposure. [Bandura 2001](https://techofcomm.wordpress.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/09/bandura-sct-of-mass-communication.pdf "Section on Abstract Modeling")
- 発見 3: Vicarious reinforcement is the specific Bandura mechanism most applicable to shoujo manga: characters using watashi receive happy endings (Shibamoto Smith 2004), while villains use hyperfeminine onna-kotoba (Takahashi 2009), creating a reinforcement structure where readers learn which gendered speech patterns are "rewarded." [Bandura 2001](https://techofcomm.wordpress.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/09/bandura-sct-of-mass-communication.pdf "Section on Motivational Effects")
- 発見 4: Stuart-Smith et al. (2013) demonstrated empirically that among Glaswegian adolescents, TH-fronting and L-vocalization correlated significantly with *psychological engagement* with EastEnders characters, not passive exposure or positive attitudes toward London accents. Stuart-Smith characterized the mechanism as "the effective enhancement of existing variation... media may act as another means of enabling existing or latent variation to 'bubble up' into changes." Stuart-Smith, J. et al. (2013), *Language* 89(3): 501–536. [University of Glasgow 2013](https://www.gla.ac.uk/news/archiveofnews/2013/september/headline_289308_en.html "Press release on Language 89(3) article")
- 発見 5: Naro (1981) found syntactic changes in Brazilian Portuguese (subject/verb agreement) were significantly correlated with reported soap-opera exposure, providing one of the earliest quantitative demonstrations of media effects at the morphosyntactic level — the same level (pronouns, particles) relevant to Japanese gendered speech. Naro, A.J. (1981), *Language* 57(1): 63–98. [Strawn 2022](https://digitalcommons.spu.edu/cgi/viewcontent.cgi?article=1175&context=honorsprojects "Citing Naro 1981, p. 19")
- 発見 6: Carvalho (2004) provides a critical counterexample: Uruguayan Portuguese speakers who reported wanting to "speak like the guys on TV" showed no greater palatalization, demonstrating that self-reported media influence may overstate actual effects. Carvalho, A.M. (2004), *Language Variation and Change* 16: 127–151. [Strawn 2022](https://digitalcommons.spu.edu/cgi/viewcontent.cgi?article=1175&context=honorsprojects "p. 19")
- 発見 7: Sayers (2014) proposed the "mediated innovation model" in *Journal of Sociolinguistics* 18(2): 185–212, stimulating a landmark special-issue debate with responses from Trudgill (2014, "Diffusion, Drift, and the Irrelevance of Media Influence"), Tagliamonte (2014), Stuart-Smith (2014), and Androutsopoulos (2014, "Beyond 'Media Influence'") — the most comprehensive disciplinary discussion of media influence on language change. [Strawn 2022](https://digitalcommons.spu.edu/cgi/viewcontent.cgi?article=1175&context=honorsprojects "Discussion of the 2014 debate, pp. 11–24")
- 発見 8: The dominant variationist position (Chambers 1998; Trudgill 2014; Labov 2001) holds that media can diffuse lexical items but cannot influence phonological or morphosyntactic features, which require face-to-face interaction (Labov's density principle). Stuart-Smith et al. (2013) and the 2014 debate opened the door to a nuanced revision: media may not introduce new variants but may accelerate changes already present in a community. [Strawn 2022](https://digitalcommons.spu.edu/cgi/viewcontent.cgi?article=1175&context=honorsprojects "pp. 3–10")
- 発見 9: Bell & Raymond (2026) conducted the first controlled study of anime/manga exposure effects on pronoun perception: beginner learners with high anime/manga exposure rated cross-gender pronoun usage as far more acceptable than heritage speakers (53.9% vs. 0% for men using atashi, χ²=11.94, p=0.0026; 46.2% vs. 0% for women using ore, χ²=9.81, p=0.0074). [Bell & Raymond 2026](https://digitalcommons.dartmouth.edu/cgi/viewcontent.cgi?article=1000&context=ascl_60-20 "Learning Gendered Speech, Dartmouth College, March 2026")
- 発見 10: Manga's medium-specific affordances constrain and shape its linguistic influence: (a) written-only dialogue limits influence to morphosyntactic features (pronouns, particles, vocabulary), not phonology; (b) characters' fixed pronoun systems (unlike real-world speakers' context-dependent switching) create unusually clear gendered-speech models; (c) repeated reading enables deeper processing of dialogue patterns. [Unser-Schutz 2015](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/265729754_Influential_or_influenced_The_relationship_between_genre_gender_and_language_in_manga "Discussion section")
- 発見 11: Inoue's (2006) concept of "vicarious language" — language experienced primarily through mediated consumption rather than production — applies with particular force to manga, where readers encounter hundreds of hours of gendered dialogue without producing it themselves, making the medium a primary site of gendered-language socialization. [Inoue 2006](https://sts.stanford.edu/publications/vicarious-language-gender-and-linguistic-modernity-japan "UC Press")
- 発見 12: Japanese gendered pronouns occupy an ambiguous position in the "off the shelf" vs. "under the counter" distinction (Eckert 2003; Milroy 2007): they are conscious lexical choices (a speaker can deliberately select ore over watashi), yet their habitual deployment may involve unconscious processes — meaning manga influence on pronoun choice may be easier to establish than influence on phonology, but harder to distinguish from conscious identity work. [Strawn 2022](https://digitalcommons.spu.edu/cgi/viewcontent.cgi?article=1175&context=honorsprojects "pp. 12–14")
- 発見 13: The Japanese Agency for Cultural Affairs' 2007 survey found 45% of respondents considered manga influential on young people's speech, but Unser-Schutz argues manga is "given as a post-facto reason for linguistic change" while being actually conservative — invoking Aitchison's (1998) "garbage heap fallacy." [Unser-Schutz 2015](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/265729754_Influential_or_influenced_The_relationship_between_genre_gender_and_language_in_manga "Discussion section")
- 発見 14: Androutsopoulos (2014) argues for moving beyond binary "media influence" to a "mediatization" approach examining how media and language change are co-constitutive — audiences recontextualize and transform media speech. This is especially relevant to fan communities, where media-derived speech is consciously adopted as markers of subcultural belonging. Androutsopoulos, J. (2014), *Journal of Sociolinguistics* 18(2): 242–249.

# Section 2：给 Write 阶段的执行建议

- **Terminology consistency**: Establish a consistent glossary early. Japanese terms should appear in kanji/kana on first use with romanization and English gloss (e.g., 終助詞 shūjoshi, 'sentence-ending particles'), then use a consistent abbreviation (e.g., "SEPs") throughout.
- **Tone and register**: Maintain formal academic register. Use measured scholarly hedging where empirical certainty is limited ("existing evidence suggests," "this pattern is consistent with").
- **Positionality toward the queer community**: Write about queer individuals as agents with complex motivations. Use terminology sourced from Japanese-language queer community discourse and flag cases where English-origin and Japanese-origin terms carry different connotations.
- **Avoid overclaiming media effects**: Maintain clear distinction between (a) attested correlations, (b) theoretically plausible mechanisms, and (c) speculative extrapolations. The report's value lies in carefully building a framework, not asserting under-evidenced conclusions.
- **Cross-chapter coherence**: Each chapter should connect to the preceding one and preview how its content feeds into the next. The pronoun/SEP inventory in Chapter 2 should serve as the explicit reference framework invoked in Chapters 3, 4, and 6.
- **Empirical vs. prescriptive framing**: Consistently distinguish prescriptive norms from descriptive realities. This distinction is critical for Japanese gendered language, where the gap is well-documented and wide.
- **BL/yuri genre handling**: Treat BL and yuri as illuminating comparators, not as the central object; keep their treatment proportionate relative to shoujo manga.
- **Visual and structural clarity**: Use well-structured tables for inventories of pronouns and particles. Summary tables at the end of Chapters 2 and 3 mapping forms to gendered associations and manga vs. real-world frequency would aid comprehension.
- **Time coverage**: Literature and cultural trends primarily from 2010–2026, with foundational works cited as needed. Current date anchor: 2026-04-06.
- **Report language**: English, with Japanese terms romanized or given in kanji/kana with English gloss.
