# Section 1: Chapter Research Plan

## Chapter 1: Origins and Intellectual Foundations
### Research Objectives
- Trace the formative influences, mentors, and intellectual traditions that shaped Buffett, Munger, and Duan Yongping as investors.
- How did Buffett evolve from Graham-style "cigar butt" investing to quality-focused compounding, and what role did Munger play?
- How did Duan Yongping's entrepreneurial success (Subor, BBK Electronics, OPPO/Vivo ecosystem) inform his investment approach, and what did he specifically adopt from Buffett and Munger?
- What distinct intellectual traditions (Graham's quantitative value investing, Munger's multidisciplinary mental models, Duan's synthesis of Eastern business pragmatism with Western value investing) converge in and differentiate these three investors?

### Key Findings
- Warren Buffett (born August 30, 1930, Omaha) bought his first stock at age 11, enrolled at Columbia Business School in 1950 to study under Benjamin Graham, and received the only A+ in Graham's class. Graham's *The Intelligent Investor* (1949) provided the foundational framework of intrinsic value, margin of safety, and the "Mr. Market" allegory. [Alice Schroeder, *The Snowball: Warren Buffett and the Business of Life* (Bantam Books, 2008), Chapters 1–17]
- Buffett worked at Graham-Newman Corporation (1954–1956), then launched Buffett Partnership Ltd. on May 1, 1956, with $105,100 from seven limited partners. Over 13 years, the partnership compounded at 29.5% annually (before fees) vs. 7.4% for the Dow, never posting a losing year. [Buffett Partnership Letters, 1957–1969]
- Philip Fisher's *Common Stocks and Uncommon Profits* (1958) introduced Buffett to qualitative, growth-oriented investing — emphasis on management quality, long-term holding, and "scuttlebutt" research. Buffett has described himself as "85% Graham and 15% Fisher," though this quote's exact provenance remains unverified from a T1 source.
- The 1972 acquisition of See's Candies for $25 million — then earning about $4 million pre-tax on $8 million of net tangible assets — was the pivotal turning point. By the 2014 letter, Buffett reported See's had earned $1.9 billion pre-tax cumulatively, requiring only $40 million in added investment. [Buffett, *Berkshire Hathaway Annual Letter* (2014), https://www.berkshirehathaway.com/letters/2014ltr.pdf "2014 Annual Letter — 50th Anniversary"]
- Munger's role in Buffett's evolution: "Forget what you know about buying fair businesses at wonderful prices; instead, buy wonderful businesses at fair prices" — Buffett crediting Munger in the 2014 letter. The 1989 letter reinforced: "It's far better to buy a wonderful company at a fair price than a fair company at a wonderful price. Charlie understood this early; I was a slow learner." [Buffett, *Berkshire Hathaway Annual Letter* (1989), https://www.berkshirehathaway.com/letters/1989.html "1989 Annual Letter"]
- Charlie Munger (born January 1, 1924, Omaha; died November 28, 2023, age 99) studied math at Michigan, trained as a meteorologist at Caltech during WWII, then graduated magna cum laude from Harvard Law School in 1948 without completing an undergraduate degree. He co-founded Munger, Tolles & Olson in 1962. [Berkshire Hathaway press release, November 28, 2023, https://www.berkshirehathaway.com/news/nov2823.pdf "Official announcement of Munger's death"; Janet Lowe, *Damn Right!* (2000)]
- Munger met Buffett in 1959 via the Davis family. Munger cited Benjamin Franklin as his most admired historical figure; Franklin's *Poor Richard's Almanack* influenced the title of *Poor Charlie's Almanack* (2005). Munger's "latticework of mental models" — drawing from psychology, physics, biology, economics — was articulated most fully in his 1994 USC Business School speech. [*Poor Charlie's Almanack*; *Damn Right!*, Chapter 5]
- Duan Yongping (段永平, born 1961, Nanchang, Jiangxi) earned degrees from Zhejiang University and Renmin University (econometrics). He transformed a struggling Zhongshan electronics factory into Subor Electronics (小霸王, ~1989), producing educational computers and game consoles. He left in 1995 over ownership disputes (SOE structure prevented equity).
- Duan founded BBK Electronics (步步高) in 1995 in Dongguan, implementing an innovative equity-sharing structure for key managers. This spawned OPPO (Tony Chen/陈明永), Vivo (Shen Wei/沈炜), and OnePlus. His core business philosophy centered on "本分" (běn fèn) — doing the right thing, fulfilling one's duty. He emigrated to the U.S. (~2001, Atherton, California), transitioning from entrepreneur to full-time investor.
- Duan began studying Buffett's philosophy in the early 2000s, reading shareholder letters, *The Intelligent Investor*, and *Poor Charlie's Almanack*. He has described Buffett's annual letters as "the best MBA education you can get." In June 2006, Duan won the Buffett charity lunch auction with a bid of $620,100 (then-record), with proceeds going to the Glide Foundation. He reportedly brought a young associate, Huang Zheng (黄峥), who later founded Pinduoduo (拼多多/PDD Holdings). [Chinese and English financial media coverage of the 2006 charity lunch]
- All three investors trace intellectual lineage to Benjamin Graham. Munger uniquely broadened the tradition via multidisciplinary mental models (his "Psychology of Human Misjudgment" — 25 causes, originally presented at Harvard 1995). Duan synthesized Western value investing with Chinese business culture and Confucian pragmatism ("本分"), adding an entrepreneurial operator lens.

## Chapter 2: Core Investment Principles and Selection Criteria
### Research Objectives
- How does each investor define and estimate "intrinsic value," and where do their methodologies differ?
- What role does "margin of safety" play in each framework, and how wide a margin does each require?
- How do the three investors evaluate competitive advantages ("moats") — what types of moats do they prioritize, and how do they assess moat durability?
- What is each investor's stance on "great company at a fair price" vs. "fair company at a great price"?
- How does Duan Yongping's emphasis on "本分" (ben fen — doing the right thing / duty-bound management) compare with Buffett and Munger's emphasis on management integrity and owner-operator culture?

### Key Findings
- Buffett's canonical intrinsic value definition appears in the 1994 Owner's Manual (Principle #11): "Intrinsic value can be defined simply: It is the discounted value of the cash that can be taken out of a business during its remaining life." He cautioned the calculation is inherently imprecise. [Buffett, *An Owner's Manual* (1994), https://www.berkshirehathaway.com/ownman.pdf "Berkshire Hathaway Owner's Manual"]
- Buffett's "owner earnings" concept (1986 letter): reported earnings + depreciation/amortization − required maintenance capex. He warned against treating EBITDA as earning power: "References to EBITDA make us shudder — does management think the tooth fairy pays for capital expenditures?" (2000 letter). [Buffett, *Berkshire Hathaway Annual Letter* (1986), https://www.berkshirehathaway.com/letters/1986.html "1986 Annual Letter"]
- Munger's practical approach: "We don't actually do discounted cash flow calculations. We sort of do it in our heads." At the 2007 Berkshire annual meeting, Munger said: "Warren often talks about these discounted cash flows. I've never seen him do one." Both treat intrinsic value as a conceptual anchor rather than a precise spreadsheet output. [*Poor Charlie's Almanack* (2005); Berkshire Hathaway Annual Meeting transcripts]
- Duan Yongping follows Buffett's DCF framework conceptually but simplifies: "If you need a calculator to figure out whether something is cheap, it probably isn't cheap enough." He emphasizes deep business understanding over precise modeling. [Duan Yongping, Xueqiu (雪球) posts; Chinese media interviews]
- Buffett on margin of safety (1992 letter): "In *The Intelligent Investor*, Ben Graham explained a margin-of-safety concept that I believe to be — almost 50 years after first reading it — the cornerstone of investment success." Buffett does not specify a fixed numerical margin; it varies with business quality and predictability. [Buffett, *Berkshire Hathaway Annual Letter* (1992), https://www.berkshirehathaway.com/letters/1992.html "1992 Annual Letter"]
- Munger redefined margin of safety: "The best way to get a margin of safety is to buy a wonderful business that throws off lots of cash." For Munger, business quality itself is the primary source of protection, not merely a discount to static valuation. [*Poor Charlie's Almanack* (2005)]
- Duan's margin of safety comes from understanding: "If you truly understand a company, a 30–40% discount to your estimate of value is sufficient. The key is truly understanding — most people overestimate their understanding." [Duan Yongping, Xueqiu (雪球) posts]
- Buffett popularized the "economic moat" metaphor (1995 letter): "The products or services that have wide, sustainable moats around them are the ones that deliver rewards to investors." Moat types: brand/intangible assets (See's, Coca-Cola), low-cost producer (GEICO), switching costs, and regulatory advantages. [Buffett, *Berkshire Hathaway Annual Letter* (1995), https://www.berkshirehathaway.com/letters/1995.html "1995 Annual Letter"]
- Buffett on pricing power as the ultimate moat test: "The single most important decision in evaluating a business is pricing power. If you've got the power to raise prices without losing business to a competitor, you've got a very good business." [Buffett, interview with Financial Crisis Inquiry Commission, February 18, 2011]
- Munger emphasizes "lollapalooza" combinations where multiple moats reinforce each other (e.g., Costco's cost discipline + buying power + customer loyalty). He also views culture as a moat: "The culture of the company is the moat." [Munger, Wesco Financial annual meeting, 2003; *Poor Charlie's Almanack*]
- Duan on moats: "A good moat is when consumers buy your product not because it's the cheapest, but because they want your product specifically." He highlights Apple's ecosystem (switching costs + brand + network effects) and WeChat's billion-user network effect as paradigmatic moats. [Duan Yongping, Xueqiu (雪球) posts]
- Buffett's definitive "great company at a fair price" statement (1989 letter): "It's far better to buy a wonderful company at a fair price than a fair company at a wonderful price. Charlie understood this early; I was a slow learner." [Buffett, *Berkshire Hathaway Annual Letter* (1989), https://www.berkshirehathaway.com/letters/1989.html "1989 Annual Letter"]
- Munger's long-term ROE convergence logic (*Poor Charlie's Almanack*): "If a business earns 18% on capital over 20 or 30 years, even if you pay an expensive-looking price, you'll end up with a fine result." This crystallizes why business quality trumps entry price over long holding periods. [*Poor Charlie's Almanack* (2005)]
- Duan: "I only buy great companies. I never buy a mediocre company no matter how cheap it is." His portfolio has been dominated by a handful of exceptional companies — Apple, Tencent, NetEase — with no evidence of bargain-hunting in mediocre businesses. [Duan Yongping, Xueqiu (雪球) posts]
- Buffett's franchise vs. commodity framework (1991 letter): A franchise "(1) is needed or desired; (2) is thought by its customers to have no close substitute; and (3) is not subject to price regulation." A franchise has pricing power and can tolerate mismanagement; a commodity business competes on price and earns low returns. [Buffett, *Berkshire Hathaway Annual Letter* (1991), https://www.berkshirehathaway.com/letters/1991.html "1991 Annual Letter"]
- Buffett's three criteria for management: intelligence, energy, and integrity — "without the third, the first two will kill you." [Berkshire Hathaway Annual Meeting remarks; confirmed in *The Snowball* by Alice Schroeder]
- Munger on management incentives: "Show me the incentive and I will show you the outcome." Incentive-caused bias is the first of his 25 standard causes of human misjudgment. [Munger, "The Psychology of Human Misjudgment," Harvard, 1995; *Poor Charlie's Almanack*]
- Duan's "本分" (ben fen) applied in two dimensions: (1) evaluating management — companies whose leaders focus on product quality, treat stakeholders fairly, avoid speculation; (2) investor self-discipline — "本分 means doing the right thing, not the easy thing. It means admitting when you don't understand something." The three frameworks converge on integrity as non-negotiable but differ in emphasis: Buffett → capital allocation skill + honesty; Munger → incentive alignment + rational behavior; Duan → cultural integrity + long-term duty. [Duan Yongping, Xueqiu (雪球) posts; Chinese media interviews]
- Key investment examples illustrating principles: Coca-Cola (1988, ~$1.3B — brand moat, pricing power, forever holding period); Apple (2016–, Buffett called it "probably the best business I know in the world" at 2023 annual meeting); Costco (Munger on board 1997–2023, "builds its moat wider every day"); BYD (2008, ~$232M for 10% stake — Munger compared Wang Chuanfu to "a combination of Thomas Edison and Jack Welch"); NetEase (Duan bought at ~$0.80/share in 2002, near delisting, returned ~50–100x); Duan on Apple and Tencent's ecosystem moats. [Berkshire Hathaway annual letters, 13-F filings, Annual Meeting remarks; Duan Yongping, Xueqiu posts; Chinese and English financial media]
- Circle of competence as universal filter: Buffett (1996 Owner's Manual): "You don't have to be an expert on every company, or even many. You only have to be able to evaluate companies within your circle of competence. The size of that circle is not very important; knowing its boundaries, however, is vital." Duan's circle is notably narrower — focused almost entirely on consumer technology/internet businesses he personally uses. [Buffett, *An Owner's Manual* (1996), https://www.berkshirehathaway.com/ownman.pdf "Owner's Manual"]

## Chapter 3: Valuation Methodology and Decision-Making Frameworks
### Research Objectives
- What specific valuation methods (DCF, owner earnings, earnings yield vs. bond yield, etc.) does each investor favor?
- How does Munger's "latticework of mental models" function in practice, and to what extent have Buffett and Duan adopted similar multidisciplinary thinking?
- What is each investor's approach to the "too hard" pile and circle of competence?
- How does each investor handle uncertainty, probabilistic thinking, and opportunity cost?

### Key Findings
- Buffett's "owner earnings" (1986 letter) — reported earnings + depreciation/amortization − required maintenance capex — is his preferred valuation metric, not EBITDA. The 2000 letter warned: "References to EBITDA make us shudder — does management think the tooth fairy pays for capital expenditures?" [Buffett, *Berkshire Hathaway Annual Letter* (1986), https://www.berkshirehathaway.com/letters/1986.html "1986 Annual Letter"; (2000), https://www.berkshirehathaway.com/letters/2000pdf.pdf "2000 Annual Letter"]
- Buffett's DCF is a conceptual anchor, not a spreadsheet: "If the value of a company doesn't just scream at you, it's too close" (1997 annual meeting). The 2000 letter used the college-education analogy — discounted future earnings differential is real but impossible to calculate precisely. [Buffett, *An Owner's Manual* (1994), https://www.berkshirehathaway.com/ownman.pdf "Owner's Manual"; (2000 letter)]
- Buffett's earnings-yield vs. bond-yield framework: compares owner-earnings yield to long-term Treasury yield as a valuation benchmark. Featured prominently in the 1992 letter (citing John Burr Williams' *The Theory of Investment Value*, 1938) and the 1999 *Fortune* article. [Buffett, *Berkshire Hathaway Annual Letter* (1992), https://www.berkshirehathaway.com/letters/1992.html "1992 Annual Letter"; "Mr. Buffett on the Stock Market," *Fortune*, Nov 22, 1999]
- See's Candies valuation case: purchased 1972 for $25M; $4M pre-tax on $8M tangible assets = 50% pre-tax return on tangible capital, ~6.25x pre-tax earnings. By 2007, See's earned $82M pre-tax on $40M tangible assets; cumulative pre-tax earnings reached $1.9B by 2014 with only $40M incremental capital. [Buffett, *Berkshire Hathaway Annual Letter* (2007), https://www.berkshirehathaway.com/letters/2007ltr.pdf "2007 Annual Letter"; (2014), https://www.berkshirehathaway.com/letters/2014ltr.pdf "2014 Annual Letter"]
- Munger explicitly rejects formal DCF: "Warren often talks about these discounted cash flows. I've never seen him do one" (2007 annual meeting). "We sort of do it in our heads." He relies on qualitative pattern recognition and long-term ROE convergence logic: "If a business earns 18% on capital over 20 or 30 years, even if you pay an expensive-looking price, you'll end up with a fine result." [*Poor Charlie's Almanack* (2005); Berkshire Hathaway Annual Meeting, 2007]
- Duan's radically simplified approach: "If you need a calculator to figure out whether something is cheap, it probably isn't cheap enough." Binary rather than quantitative — either obviously cheap or pass. He reframes valuation as a test of understanding: "Most people think investing is about valuation. It's not. It's about understanding the business." [Duan Yongping, Xueqiu (雪球) posts]
- Munger's "latticework of mental models" (1994 USC lecture): investors must build a lattice of models from psychology, physics, biology, economics, engineering, history. Key named models: (1) incentive bias ("Show me the incentive and I will show you the outcome"), (2) social proof/herd behavior, (3) availability bias, (4) commitment/consistency bias, (5) lollapalooza effects (multiple forces compounding), (6) second-order thinking ("and then what?"), (7) inversion, (8) compound interest, (9) redundancy/margin of safety, (10) critical mass. [Munger, "A Lesson on Elementary, Worldly Wisdom," USC Business School, 1994; *Poor Charlie's Almanack*]
- Munger's "Psychology of Human Misjudgment" (Harvard 1995): 25 standard causes of human misjudgment, from incentive superresponse to lollapalooza tendency. Used as a decision-audit checklist. [Munger, "The Psychology of Human Misjudgment," Harvard, 1995; expanded in *Poor Charlie's Almanack* (2005)]
- Buffett uses many of Munger's models implicitly but never systematized them. Duan adopted selected models — especially inversion, incentive analysis, and circle of competence — applying them within a narrower range focused on consumer technology. [Duan Yongping, Xueqiu posts; Berkshire annual letters]
- Buffett's circle of competence (1996 Owner's Manual): "You don't have to be an expert on every company, or even many... The size of that circle is not very important; knowing its boundaries, however, is vital." Declined Amazon, Google, Microsoft — acknowledged missing them but considered the uncertainty too great. [Buffett, *An Owner's Manual* (1996), https://www.berkshirehathaway.com/ownman.pdf "Owner's Manual"; Berkshire Annual Meeting, 2017]
- Munger's three-basket system: "yes, no, and too hard to understand" (2002 Wesco annual meeting). Estimates 98–99% of opportunities belong in the "too hard" pile. Includes pharma, short-cycle tech, unreliable-rule-of-law jurisdictions, macro forecasting. [Munger, Wesco annual meeting, 2002; Berkshire Annual Meeting, 2006]
- Duan's notably narrow circle: "I only invest in businesses I can understand. For me, that means consumer technology companies — Apple, Tencent, NetEase. I don't understand banks, oil companies, pharmaceutical companies." Declines banks (can't evaluate balance sheet risks), commodities, real estate, Bitcoin ("no way to estimate intrinsic value"). [Duan Yongping, Xueqiu (雪球) posts]
- Munger's inversion ("Invert, always invert") traces to mathematician Jacobi: "Man muss immer umkehren." Applied in the 1986 Harvard speech "How to Guarantee a Life of Misery." In investing: "It is remarkable how much long-term advantage people like us have gotten by trying to be consistently not stupid, instead of trying to be very intelligent" (2007 Berkshire annual meeting). Duan applies inversion to moat analysis: "What could erode this moat? What would make consumers switch away?" [*Poor Charlie's Almanack*; Berkshire Annual Meeting, 2007; Duan Yongping, Xueqiu posts]
- Buffett's Ted Williams "fat pitch" analogy (1997 letter): "The trick in investing is just to sit there and watch pitch after pitch go by and wait for the one right in your sweet spot." Key difference from baseball: "In investing, there are no called strikes." [Buffett, *Berkshire Hathaway Annual Letter* (1997), https://www.berkshirehathaway.com/letters/1997.html "1997 Annual Letter"]
- Buffett's "20-slot punch card" concept: if limited to 20 lifetime investments, "you'd really think carefully about what you did, and you'd be forced to load up on what you'd really thought about" (1999 annual meeting). [Berkshire Annual Meeting, 1999]
- Munger on opportunity cost: "The whole concept of opportunity cost is that you want to find the best thing you can do with each incremental dollar" (2006 Berkshire meeting). In his Stanford "Academic Economics" speech, he criticized economics departments for treating opportunity cost as a textbook abstraction rather than a decision tool. [Munger, Wesco annual meeting, 2003; Berkshire Annual Meeting, 2004/2006; *Poor Charlie's Almanack*]
- Buffett's opportunity cost in practice: holds enormous cash when no compelling opportunity exists (cash surpassed $325B by late 2024). Compares investments to best alternative use, not to a benchmark index. [Berkshire Hathaway annual letters and 10-Q filings]
- Duan on opportunity cost and concentration: "Every dollar I put into my second-best idea is a dollar not in my best idea." This drives his extreme concentration — typically 3–5 positions. [Duan Yongping, Xueqiu (雪球) posts]

## Chapter 4: Portfolio Construction, Concentration, and Risk Management
### Research Objectives
- How concentrated are each investor's portfolios, and what rationale does each offer for concentration vs. diversification?
- What is each investor's approach to position sizing?
- How does each investor define and manage "risk" (volatility vs. permanent capital loss)?
- What is each investor's attitude toward cash holdings and deployment timing?
- How has Duan Yongping's portfolio (Apple, Tencent, NetEase) reflected concentration principles similar to or different from Berkshire Hathaway's public equity portfolio?

### Key Findings
- Berkshire's public equity portfolio has been extremely concentrated in Apple: at peak (~Q1 2024), ~905M shares worth $135–175B, representing ~40–50% of total public equity portfolio. Buffett sold roughly half during 2024, reducing to ~300M shares by Q3 2024. Top 5 holdings (Apple, Bank of America, American Express, Coca-Cola, Chevron) have historically represented 60–80% of the equity portfolio. [Berkshire Hathaway 13-F filings, SEC EDGAR; Buffett, *Berkshire Hathaway Annual Letter* (2023), https://www.berkshirehathaway.com/letters/2023ltr.pdf "2023 Annual Letter"]
- Buffett's concentration philosophy (1993 letter): "We believe that a policy of portfolio concentration may well decrease risk if it raises, as it should, both the intensity with which an investor thinks about a business and the comfort level he must feel with its economic characteristics before buying into it." Also: "I cannot understand why an investor of that sort elects to put money into a business that is his 20th favorite rather than simply adding that money to his top choices." [Buffett, *Berkshire Hathaway Annual Letter* (1993), https://www.berkshirehathaway.com/letters/1993.html "1993 Annual Letter"]
- Buffett's 1984 "Superinvestors of Graham-and-Doddsville" speech presented track records of nine Graham-trained investors, many with concentrated portfolios, arguing concentration with genuine knowledge enhances risk-adjusted returns. [Buffett, "The Superinvestors of Graham-and-Doddsville," *Columbia Business School Magazine*, Fall 1984]
- Buffett defines risk as permanent capital loss, not volatility (1993 letter): "We define risk, using dictionary terms, as 'the possibility of loss or injury.'" Illustrated with Washington Post (1973): market cap $80M vs. $400M business value — lower price = less risk, despite higher "beta." Three sources of true risk: (1) overpaying, (2) competitive position deterioration, (3) financial leverage converting temporary problems into permanent losses. [Buffett, *Berkshire Hathaway Annual Letter* (1993), https://www.berkshirehathaway.com/letters/1993.html "1993 Annual Letter"]
- Munger on concentration: "The wise ones bet heavily when the world offers them that opportunity. They bet big when they have the odds. And the rest of the time, they don't." Referenced the Kelly Criterion (optimal position sizing based on edge and odds) to support concentrated portfolios. "The idea of excessive diversification is madness." [*Poor Charlie's Almanack* (2005); Munger, USC lecture, 1994]
- Munger rejects volatility as risk: "Using volatility as a measure of risk is nuts. Risk to us is 1) the risk of permanent loss of capital, or 2) the risk of inadequate return" (2000 Wesco annual meeting). [*Poor Charlie's Almanack*; Munger, Wesco annual meeting, 2000]
- Duan's extreme concentration — typically 3–5 major positions (Apple, Tencent, earlier NetEase): "If you truly understand a company, you don't need to diversify. Diversification is an admission that you don't know what you're doing." NetEase was his most famous concentration bet (~$0.80/share in 2002, ~50–100x return). Apple has been his largest and longest-held position (entry ~2011–2012, years before Buffett). [Duan Yongping, Xueqiu (雪球) posts]
- Berkshire cash surpassed $325B by Q3 2024, a record. Buffett views cash as "an option without an expiration date" and maintains "fortress-like balance sheet" (2012 letter). Crisis deployment: $15.6B in 25 days during Sep–Oct 2008, including $5B Goldman Sachs preferred (10% coupon) and $3B GE preferred (10% coupon). "Be fearful when others are greedy, and be greedy when others are fearful" (NYT op-ed, Oct 16, 2008). [Buffett, *Berkshire Hathaway Annual Letter* (2008), https://www.berkshirehathaway.com/letters/2008ltr.pdf "2008 Annual Letter"; "Buy American. I Am," *The New York Times*, Oct 16, 2008; Berkshire 10-Q filings]
- Insurance float as structural capital advantage: Berkshire float grew from $39M (1970) to ~$168B (2023). "If our premiums exceed the total of our expenses and eventual losses, we register an underwriting profit that adds to the investment income that our float produces" (2004 letter). Functions as permanent, cost-free (or negative-cost) leverage. [Buffett, *Berkshire Hathaway Annual Letter* (2004), https://www.berkshirehathaway.com/letters/2004ltr.pdf "2004 Annual Letter"; (2023 letter)]
- Selling criteria: Buffett's "favorite holding period is forever" (1988 letter), with three exceptions — (1) competitive position deteriorated, (2) management no longer trustworthy, (3) clearly better capital use. 2024 Apple sales partly driven by tax strategy (anticipated capital gains rate increases). Airlines sold in 2020 ("The world changed for the airline business... I was wrong"). Wells Fargo position eliminated as fake-accounts scandal eroded management integrity. [Buffett, *Berkshire Hathaway Annual Letter* (1988), https://www.berkshirehathaway.com/letters/1988.html "1988 Annual Letter"; Berkshire Annual Meeting, May 2024 and May 2020]
- Munger's extreme selling reluctance: "The first rule of compounding is to never interrupt it unnecessarily." "We've made most of our money by sitting" (2019 Daily Journal meeting). [*Poor Charlie's Almanack*; Daily Journal annual meeting, 2019]
- Duan sells only when (1) his understanding was flawed, or (2) the business fundamentally changed. Does not sell based on price targets. [Duan Yongping, Xueqiu posts]
- Buffett's critical nuance: concentration for "know-something" investors, but index funds for most. 2013 letter: will instructs trustee to invest 90% of wife's inheritance in "a very low-cost S&P 500 index fund" and 10% in short-term government bonds. [Buffett, *Berkshire Hathaway Annual Letter* (2013), https://www.berkshirehathaway.com/letters/2013ltr.pdf "2013 Annual Letter"]
- Cost of concentration: Munger Partnership returned ~19.8% annually (1962–1975) vs. 5.2% Dow, but suffered -31.9% (1973) and -31.5% (1974). Berkshire stock experienced ~50% drawdowns in 2008–09 and 1998–2000. Concentration amplifies both gains and losses. [*Poor Charlie's Almanack*; Buffett, "Superinvestors of Graham-and-Doddsville," 1984]
- Duan uses put-selling as entry strategy: sells cash-secured puts at desired entry prices, collecting premium while waiting. "Selling puts on a company I want to own is a way of getting paid to wait." Consistent with Buffett's own 2007–08 equity index put-selling. [Duan Yongping, Xueqiu posts; Buffett, *Berkshire Hathaway Annual Letter* (2008)]

### Key Findings
- Buffett's contrarian temperament formulation: "Be fearful when others are greedy, and greedy when others are fearful" (first appeared in 2004 letter; gained widest circulation in the Oct 16, 2008 NYT op-ed "Buy American. I Am," published while the S&P 500 was in a 57% peak-to-trough decline). [Buffett, *Berkshire Hathaway Annual Letter* (2004), https://www.berkshirehathaway.com/letters/2004ltr.pdf "2004 Annual Letter"; "Buy American. I Am," *The New York Times*, Oct 16, 2008]
- Buffett's behavior across five crises: (1) 1973–74: told *Forbes* "I feel like an oversexed guy in a harem," bought Washington Post at $80M market cap vs. $400M business value; (2) 1987 crash: did not sell, used the aftermath to buy Coca-Cola ($1.3B); (3) 1999–2000: refused dot-com stocks, *Barron's* mocked him ("What's Wrong, Warren?"), vindicated when Nasdaq fell 78%; (4) 2008: deployed $15.6B in 25 days (Goldman Sachs, GE preferred); (5) 2020: notably more cautious — sold airlines at a loss, did not buy aggressively. [Buffett, *Berkshire Hathaway Annual Letters* (1987–2020); "Mr. Buffett on the Stock Market," *Fortune*, Nov 22, 1999; *Barron's*, Dec 27, 1999]
- Munger's endurance of severe drawdowns: Partnership suffered -31.9% (1973) and -31.5% (1974) — two consecutive years of ~32% losses — yet compounded at ~19.8% annually overall (1962–1975). "If you're not willing to react with equanimity to a market price decline of 50% two or three times a century, you're not fit to be a common shareholder." [*Poor Charlie's Almanack* (2005); Buffett, "Superinvestors of Graham-and-Doddsville," 1984]
- Duan's "本分" as behavioral anchor during stress: "本分 means sticking to what you know is right, even when the market is telling you you're wrong. The market is not your boss — it's your servant." His 2002 NetEase purchase at ~$0.80 (near delisting, under SEC investigation) with a large fraction of net worth was his most dramatic contrarian act. [Duan Yongping, Xueqiu (雪球) posts]
- All three reject market timing. Buffett (1987 letter): "We have no idea — and never have had — whether the market is going to go up, down, or sideways." (1994 letter): "Charlie and I never have an opinion on the market because it wouldn't be any good and it might interfere with the opinions we have that are good." (2013 letter): "Forming macro opinions or listening to the macro or market predictions of others is a waste of time." Munger: "I've never been able to forecast the macroeconomy. I don't even try." Duan: "I never try to predict where the market is going." [Buffett, *Berkshire Hathaway Annual Letters* (1987, 1994, 2013); Munger, Daily Journal annual meeting, 2019; Duan Yongping, Xueqiu posts]
- Munger applies the 25 tendencies as a personal decision-audit checklist (not just an analytical tool): guards against social proof, commitment bias, over-optimism, and deprival-superreaction in his own behavior. "Knowing the psychological tendencies that lead to error doesn't automatically protect you from them. But it gives you a fighting chance if you stay vigilant." [Munger, "Psychology of Human Misjudgment," Harvard, 1995; *Poor Charlie's Almanack*]
- Patience examples: Buffett held Coca-Cola ~37 years (1988–present), AmEx ~34 years (1991–present); Munger coined "sit on your ass investing" — "We've made most of our money by sitting" (2019 Daily Journal meeting); Duan held Apple 13+ years through multiple 30–40% drawdowns. Buffett held $325B+ cash by Q3 2024 waiting for opportunity. [Berkshire annual reports; *Poor Charlie's Almanack*; Duan Yongping, Xueqiu posts]
- Three parallel restraint frameworks converge: (1) Duan's "不做什么比做什么更重要" — avoid leverage, short-selling, investing beyond understanding, frequent trading, market timing, and tips/rumors; (2) Buffett's 20-slot punch card — forcing extreme selectivity; (3) Munger's inversion — "It is remarkable how much long-term advantage people like us have gotten by trying to be consistently not stupid, instead of trying to be very intelligent" (2007 Berkshire meeting). All agree success comes from what you avoid, not what you pursue. [Duan Yongping, Xueqiu posts; Berkshire Annual Meeting, 1999 and 2007; *Poor Charlie's Almanack*]
- All three agree temperament trumps intelligence. Buffett: "Success in investing doesn't correlate with IQ once you're above the level of 125. Once you have ordinary intelligence, what you need is the temperament to control the urges that get other people into trouble." Munger: "We're trying to be rational, not brilliant. Rationality is in much shorter supply than intelligence." Duan: "Investing is not about being the smartest person in the room. It's about being the most disciplined." [Buffett, various annual meetings; *Poor Charlie's Almanack*; Duan Yongping, Xueqiu posts]
- Handling mistakes publicly: Buffett systematically catalogs errors in annual letters — Dexter Shoe (1993 purchase, cost ~$3.5B in opportunity, called "worst deal" in 2007 letter), Tesco ($444M pre-tax loss, "I made a big mistake by dawdling" in 2014 letter), airlines (sold 2020 at a loss, "I was wrong"), IBM (~$10.7B invested 2011, sold ~breakeven by 2018). Munger credits Darwin's practice of noting disconfirming evidence. Duan: "When I made mistakes, it was usually because I wasn't being 本分 — I was reaching beyond what I truly understood." [Buffett, *Berkshire Hathaway Annual Letters* (2007, 2014); Berkshire Annual Meeting, 2020; *Poor Charlie's Almanack*; Duan Yongping, Xueqiu posts]
- Specific discipline practices: Buffett operates from Omaha (deliberate Wall Street isolation), reads 5–6 hours/day, judges by "inner scorecard" not "outer scorecard." Munger reads voraciously ("a book with legs"), sits and thinks daily. Duan simplifies lifestyle: "I probably spend less than an hour a week on investment decisions." [*The Snowball* by Alice Schroeder; *Poor Charlie's Almanack*; Duan Yongping, Xueqiu posts]
- Graham's "Mr. Market" allegory as shared foundation: all three treat the market as a servant offering prices to accept or refuse, not a guide to value. Buffett called it "the most useful thing I ever learned from Ben Graham." [Benjamin Graham, *The Intelligent Investor* (1949); Buffett annual letters; Duan Yongping, Xueqiu posts]

## Chapter 6: Comparative Synthesis and Enduring Relevance
### Research Objectives
- Where do the three investors show strongest philosophical alignment, and where are the most meaningful differences?
- How does Duan Yongping's philosophy function as a cross-cultural adaptation of value investing?
- What are the structural limitations or criticisms of their shared value-investing framework?
- How have they adapted (or not) to changing market environments — technology disruption, passive investing, AI-era valuations?
- What actionable, transferable principles can individual investors extract from their combined philosophies?

### Key Findings
- **Seven points of strongest alignment**: (1) intrinsic value as conceptual compass (not spreadsheet output); (2) risk = permanent capital loss, not volatility; (3) concentration over diversification; (4) wonderful company at fair price > fair company at wonderful price; (5) temperament over intellect; (6) rejection of market timing and macro forecasting; (7) restraint as primary source of alpha (Buffett's punch card, Munger's "not stupid," Duan's "不做什么比做什么更重要"). [Buffett, *An Owner's Manual* (1994); (1993 letter); (1989 letter); *Poor Charlie's Almanack*; Duan Yongping, Xueqiu posts]
- **Five meaningful divergences**: (1) breadth of intellectual framework — Munger's systematic "latticework" vs. Buffett's intuitive pattern recognition vs. Duan's focused business-model analysis; (2) management vs. business quality weighting — Buffett holds great business can survive mediocre management, Duan treats management culture ("本分") as integral to the moat; (3) circle of competence width — Buffett (broad: consumer, insurance, energy, tech since 2016) > Munger (broader geographically: BYD/China) > Duan (narrowest: consumer tech only); (4) institutional vs. individual context — Berkshire's $1T+ assets and float vs. Duan's maximum flexibility as individual investor; (5) derivatives use — Duan actively uses put-selling as entry strategy.
- **Duan as cross-cultural adaptation case study**: Preserved virtually the entire core framework. Added "本分" (ben fen) as a uniquely Chinese cultural integration — operates on two levels: management evaluation criterion and investor self-discipline tool, rooted in Confucian notions of role-based duty. Modified by bringing tech-native competence (NetEase 2002, Apple 2011–12, years before Buffett's tech entry). Added tactical put-selling methodology. Served as primary intermediary transmitting Buffett/Munger philosophy into Chinese investment culture via Xueqiu posts reaching millions. [Duan Yongping, Xueqiu posts; Chinese financial media]
- **Berkshire 60-year track record**: 1965–2024 CAGR of ~19.8% in per-share market value vs. S&P 500's ~10.4% (cumulative: 5,502,284% vs. 39,054%). Deceleration with scale: ~23.8% (1965–1984) → ~21.4% (1985–2004) → ~10.3% (2005–2024). Buffett acknowledged in 2014 letter: "Berkshire's per-share intrinsic value... can't increase at rates close to what it has achieved in the past." Market cap exceeded $1T by 2024, cash/T-bills exceeded $334B at year-end 2024. [Buffett, *Berkshire Hathaway Annual Letter* (2024), https://www.berkshirehathaway.com/letters/2024ltr.pdf "2024 Annual Letter"; (2014 letter)]
- **"Buffett's Alpha" decomposition (AQR, 2018)**: Frazzini, Kabiller & Pedersen found Berkshire's Sharpe ratio (1976–2017) was 0.79 vs. S&P 500's 0.50. Much of outperformance explained by: (1) leverage via insurance float (~1.7:1 average), (2) systematic exposure to "cheap, safe, quality" stocks (BAB and QMJ factors). Concluded: "Buffett's returns appear to be neither luck nor magic, but rather a reward for using leverage to invest in cheap, safe, quality stocks." [Frazzini, Kabiller & Pedersen, "Buffett's Alpha," *Financial Analysts Journal*, Vol. 74, No. 4, 2018, https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/abs/10.2469/faj.v74.n4.3 "Buffett's Alpha — AQR/FAJ 2018"]
- **Value factor underperformance**: Fama-French HML underperformed significantly since ~2007; Russell 1000 Growth outperformed Value by >300 percentage points (2007–2023). Asness (AQR, 2020) argued in "Is (Systematic) Value Investing Dead?" that the value spread reached historically extreme levels, predicting future value outperformance. Key distinction: Buffett/Munger/Duan practice qualitative, business-focused value investing closer to the "quality" factor than the mechanical book-to-market "value" factor.
- **Adaptation evidence**: Buffett's Apple (2016) = adaptation of application domain, not principles — evaluated using existing framework (brand, switching costs, pricing power). Munger's BYD (2008) = expansion to non-U.S., capital-intensive manufacturing — bet on founder Wang Chuanfu. Both represent mature tech with established moats, not early-stage high-growth. Buffett at 2024 annual meeting: "I don't have any great insights into AI." The circle-of-competence concept faces tension in AI era where competitive landscapes shift within months.
- **Passive investing dominance**: Index funds held ~50% of U.S. equity mutual fund assets by 2024, up from <10% in 2000. Buffett's $1M Protégé Partners bet (2008–2017): Vanguard S&P 500 index fund returned 125.8% (~8.5% annualized) vs. ~36% (~2.9% annualized) for five fund-of-funds. Buffett (2017 letter): "To a great extent their efforts are self-neutralizing, and their IQ will not overcome the costs they impose on investors." [Buffett, *Berkshire Hathaway Annual Letter* (2016–2017), https://www.berkshirehathaway.com/letters/2017ltr.pdf "2017 Annual Letter — bet conclusion"]
- **Interest rate regime shift**: Buffett (2020 letter): "Interest rates are to asset prices what gravity is to the apple. When there are virtually no interest rates, there is almost no gravitational pull on valuations." Near-zero rates (2009–2021) undermined the earnings-yield-vs-bond-yield framework. Return to 4%+ Treasury yields (2022–2024) restored its discriminating power. [Buffett, *Berkshire Hathaway Annual Letter* (2020), https://www.berkshirehathaway.com/letters/2020ltr.pdf "2020 Annual Letter"]
- **Munger's death and succession**: Munger died Nov 28, 2023 (age 99). Buffett (2023 letter): "Charlie was the 'architect' of the present Berkshire, and I acted as the 'general contractor.'" Greg Abel confirmed as CEO successor (2021 annual meeting), with full capital allocation responsibilities (2024 annual meeting). Central question: Is Berkshire's performance attributable to Buffett's non-replicable personal genius or to the durable system/culture he and Munger built? [Berkshire Hathaway press release, Nov 28, 2023, https://www.berkshirehathaway.com/news/nov2823.pdf; Buffett, *Berkshire Hathaway Annual Letter* (2023), https://www.berkshirehathaway.com/letters/2023ltr.pdf "2023 Annual Letter"]
- **"Stated philosophy vs. revealed behavior" gap**: Buffett avoided tech for decades, then made $35B+ Apple commitment. Duan's NetEase bet (massive net worth share in SEC-investigated near-delisting company) exceeded strict margin-of-safety prescriptions. Munger's BYD bet stretched circle-of-competence and governance standards. Judgment-based flexibility in application — rather than rigid rule-following — may be central to their success.
- **Seven actionable principles for individual investors**: (1) circle of competence with honest self-assessment; (2) patience as structural advantage (no quarterly pressure, no benchmark tracking); (3) inversion as decision tool — ask what could go wrong first; (4) avoid leverage, short-selling, frequent trading (Duan's "不做什么"); (5) if you cannot do the above, index (Buffett's 90/10 will instruction); (6) use business quality as primary margin of safety (Munger's ROE convergence); (7) read primary sources — annual reports, SEC filings — not analyst summaries. [Buffett, *An Owner's Manual* (1996); (2013 letter); *Poor Charlie's Almanack*; Duan Yongping, Xueqiu posts]

# Section 2: Writing Advice for the Write Stage
- **Thematic over biographical structure**: Each chapter should weave all three investors together around a shared theme. Avoid stacking three mini-biographies sequentially. Use direct comparisons — e.g., "While Buffett frames this as X, Munger extends it to Y, and Duan adapts it as Z."
- **Source hierarchy**: Buffett's shareholder letters (1957–2025) and Berkshire annual meeting transcripts are T1. Munger's "Poor Charlie's Almanack," Wesco/Daily Journal annual meeting remarks, and "The Psychology of Human Misjudgment" are T1. For Duan, verified Xueqiu (雪球) posts, public interviews, and university talks are T1. Be cautious with paraphrased social media accounts of Duan's views.
- **Source asymmetry**: Buffett and Munger have extensive English-language sources. Duan's primary materials are mostly Chinese. Acknowledge this transparently; provide original Chinese terms alongside translations (e.g., "本分", "不做什么比做什么更重要").
- **Stated philosophy vs. revealed behavior**: Note where actions diverge from stated principles (e.g., Buffett's historical tech-aversion followed by massive Apple position; Duan's concentrated bets that may exceed strict "margin of safety" prescriptions).
- **Avoid hagiography**: Maintain critical analytical distance. Address limitations — difficulty of replicating at smaller scale, survivorship bias, applicability in post-2020 market regimes.
- **Quote attribution**: Verify provenance of maxims before attributing. "Be fearful when others are greedy" = Buffett. "Invert, always invert" = Jacobi via Munger. "Price is what you pay, value is what you get" = originally Graham, widely associated with Buffett.
- **Consistent comparative framing**: Each chapter: introduce theme → present each investor's position with evidence → synthesize comparison.
- **Tone**: Formal, analytical, evidence-grounded. Use hedged language where evidence is interpretive.
- **Time scope**: Historical overview up to April 2026, covering enduring principles and notable recent developments.
